> **TL;DR**
> 1. Half-width ROW is ROW defined as a distance from the road centerline — e.g., "25 feet each side of centerline" for a 50 ft ROW. This is the standard method for county roads and many older municipal streets in Indiana.
> 2. The critical question is: where is the centerline? The legal centerline may not coincide with the current pavement centerline, especially where the road has been shifted, widened unevenly, or relocated.
> 3. Asymmetric ROW occurs when one side has been widened by additional dedication while the other side retains the original half-width. Always check both sides independently.

## How half-width ROW works

When a road is established by dedication (plat or deed) or by statute, the ROW is typically described as a total width centered on a defined line. For example:

- A plat may show "60' R/W" on a street, meaning 30 ft on each side of the street centerline.
- A county road may have been established at "30 feet each side of the center of the existing roadway" by a county commissioners' order.
- An INDOT state highway ROW plan shows left and right ROW offsets from a survey centerline.

The surveyor locates the ROW by determining the centerline position and measuring the half-width perpendicular to it.

## Determining the centerline

The centerline used for ROW measurement is the **legal centerline** — the line established by the original dedication or establishment document. This may be:

- **The centerline shown on the recorded plat.** Defined by the plat geometry (bearings, distances, curve data). This is the most reliable source.
- **The center of the original road.** For roads established by use (prescriptive) or by commissioners' order referencing "the existing roadway," the centerline is the center of the road as it existed at the time of establishment.
- **A survey centerline.** For state highways, INDOT establishes a survey centerline with station and offset. ROW plans reference this line.

### When the road has moved

Roads shift over time. Pavement is relocated, ditches are regraded, lanes are added asymmetrically. The legal centerline does not move with the pavement unless a new dedication or taking occurs. Common scenarios:

- **Road resurfaced with offset.** The pavement center has shifted 3 ft to the north, but the legal centerline has not. The ROW is still measured from the original centerline.
- **Road reconstructed on a new alignment.** If the entity acquired new ROW for the new alignment, the old ROW may have been vacated. If no new ROW was acquired, the legal ROW may still follow the original alignment.
- **Ditch widened on one side.** The ditch work may have required additional ROW on one side. Check for recorded additional ROW dedications or takings.

The surveyor should not assume the current pavement centerline is the legal centerline. When the original plat or road plan is available, the legal centerline is derived from that document.

## Asymmetric ROW

Asymmetric ROW is common and is not the same as half-width ROW. It occurs when:

- The original dedication was symmetric (e.g., 25 ft each side), but subsequent widening acquired additional ROW on one side only, making the total ROW 25 ft + 35 ft = 60 ft.
- The original dedication specified different widths on each side (rare but occurs in some plats near intersections or curves).
- A taking for road widening increased the ROW on one side.

The surveyor must determine each ROW line independently by examining all dedication and acquisition documents for each side of the road.

## Practical field approach

1. **Research the ROW documents.** Plat, deed, commissioners' order, INDOT plans. Determine the total width and whether it is symmetric.
2. **Locate the legal centerline.** Use the plat geometry, the road plan, or the best evidence of the original road position.
3. **Measure the half-width.** Measure perpendicular to the legal centerline to establish the ROW line on each side.
4. **Compare to evidence.** Check the ROW line position against fence lines, utility poles, right-of-way markers, and adjacent property corner positions. Consistent fence lines along the ROW line support the determination.
5. **Note discrepancies.** If the current pavement, ditches, or public improvements extend beyond the determined ROW, note this on the survey. The public entity may be using private land without ROW, or additional ROW may exist in an unrecorded taking.

## Indiana county roads

Many Indiana county roads were established in the 19th century by county commissioners' orders. The order may specify a width (commonly 30 ft or 40 ft total) or may simply establish the road "as now traveled." For roads established without a specific width, the ROW is the width of the actual public use (traveled surface, shoulders, ditches) at the time of establishment, which can be difficult to determine today.

The county highway department may have records, but many older records have been lost. When the ROW width cannot be determined from documents, the surveyor notes this on the survey and bases the depicted ROW on the best available evidence.
