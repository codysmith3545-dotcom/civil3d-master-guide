> **TL;DR**
> 1. Express easements are created by a recorded written instrument and are the most straightforward to locate — read the deed or plat. Implied easements arise from circumstances (necessity, prior use, plat) and may not appear in any recorded document.
> 2. Prescriptive easements in Indiana require open, notorious, continuous, hostile use for **10 years** (IC 32-23-1-1). They are not recorded until adjudicated and are invisible in a title search.
> 3. The surveyor must understand easement types because each affects how the boundary survey is conducted and what must be shown on the plat — an unrecorded prescriptive or implied easement can be just as real as a recorded one.

## Express easements

An express easement is created intentionally by a written instrument: a deed, a plat, a separate easement agreement, or a dedication. It is recorded with the county recorder and appears in the chain of title. Express easements are the type surveyors encounter most often.

Key characteristics:
- **Grant or reservation.** The grantor may grant an easement to the grantee, or reserve an easement over the conveyed parcel. The language matters: "grants an easement" creates a right in the grantee; "reserves an easement" retains a right in the grantor.
- **Appurtenant or in gross.** An appurtenant easement benefits a specific parcel (the dominant estate) and runs with the land. An easement in gross benefits a person or entity (a utility company) and may or may not be transferable.
- **Defined or blanket.** A defined easement has specific dimensions and location. A blanket easement covers an entire parcel without specifying where the encumbrance lies.

## Implied easements

Implied easements are not written; they arise by operation of law from the circumstances of the conveyance. They are enforceable but harder to identify. Types:

### Easement by necessity

Created when a parcel is landlocked by a conveyance — the grantor conveys land that has no access except across the grantor's remaining land (or vice versa). Requirements: common ownership at the time of severance, necessity existed at the time of severance, and the necessity is strict (not merely convenient).

### Easement by prior use (quasi-easement)

Created when, before severance, one part of a tract was used for the benefit of another part in a way that would constitute an easement if the two parts were separately owned. Requirements: common ownership, apparent and continuous use before severance, reasonable necessity after severance.

### Easement by plat

When a subdivision plat shows streets, alleys, parks, or utility areas, purchasers of lots acquire implied easements in those features consistent with the plat's depiction, even if the deeds do not expressly mention them.

## Prescriptive easements

A prescriptive easement is acquired by long, continuous use without the owner's permission. In Indiana, the elements are:

1. **Open and notorious** — the use must be visible and apparent, not hidden.
2. **Continuous** — uninterrupted for the statutory period.
3. **Hostile (adverse)** — without the owner's permission. Use by permission is a license, not adverse use.
4. **For 10 years** — the statutory period under IC 32-23-1-1.

Prescriptive easements are not recorded until a court adjudicates them. The surveyor cannot identify a prescriptive easement from a title search alone. Evidence of prescriptive use may appear during fieldwork: a worn path, a driveway crossing a neighbor's land, a fence that does not follow the property line.

The surveyor should note evidence of potential prescriptive use on the survey but should not make a legal determination about whether a prescriptive easement exists — that is a question for the courts.

## Easement by estoppel

An easement by estoppel arises when a property owner represents (by words or conduct) that another party has a right to use the property, the other party relies on that representation, and it would be inequitable to deny the easement. This is an equitable remedy and is fact-specific. It is not common in survey practice but may arise when a landowner has allowed a neighbor to build a driveway or install utilities based on a verbal promise.

## Surveyor's role

The surveyor does not create or adjudicate easements. The surveyor's responsibilities are:

- **Locate express easements** by reading the recorded instruments and depicting them on the survey.
- **Note evidence of possible unrecorded easements** (worn paths, utility lines, driveways crossing property lines) on the survey or in the narrative.
- **Measure and depict** the dimensions and position of visible easement uses.
- **Advise the client** to consult an attorney when unrecorded easement claims are apparent.
