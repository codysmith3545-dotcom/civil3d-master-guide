> **TL;DR**
> 1. ROW is dedicated (transferred to the public) through plat dedication, separate deed, condemnation, or prescriptive use. The mechanism determines what documents to look for and how to verify the ROW width.
> 2. Plat dedication is the most common source: the recorded subdivision plat shows street ROW with dimensions. The dedication language in the plat's certificate transfers the fee or an easement interest to the public entity.
> 3. Verifying ROW width requires examining the original dedication document, not assuming a standard width. Indiana county roads may have ROW ranging from 30 ft to 80 ft depending on era and classification.

## Mechanisms of dedication

### Plat dedication

When a subdivision plat is recorded, the streets, alleys, and public areas shown on the plat are dedicated to the public. The plat contains a dedication certificate signed by the owner and (in Indiana) approved by the plan commission or advisory board under IC 36-7-3.

The dedication may convey fee title to the street area or may create a public easement, depending on the language. Indiana generally presumes fee dedication to the centerline of the street, with the underlying fee remaining in the abutting lot owners subject to the public's right of use. However, some dedications use easement language.

The surveyor determines the ROW width from the dimensions shown on the plat. Older plats may show ROW widths that differ from current standards.

### Separate deed instrument

A landowner may dedicate ROW by recording a separate deed to the public entity (county, city, or state). This is common for road widening projects where additional ROW is needed beyond the original plat dedication. The deed specifies the area dedicated by legal description.

### Condemnation (eminent domain)

When voluntary dedication fails, the public entity may condemn the needed ROW through eminent domain proceedings. The condemnation order and court records define the area taken. The surveyor references the court case number and legal description.

### Prescriptive ROW

A public road may acquire ROW by prescription — continuous public use for the statutory period without the owner's permission. In Indiana, prescriptive ROW for public roads involves use by the public at large, maintenance by a public entity, and a period of use that establishes a public right. The width of a prescriptive ROW is the width of actual public use (typically the maintained road surface plus shoulders and ditches), not a standard platted width.

Prescriptive ROW is common with older county roads that were never formally dedicated. The surveyor identifies prescriptive ROW by the absence of a recorded dedication and the presence of a publicly maintained road.

### Acceptance

Dedication alone does not create a public road. The public entity must accept the dedication, either formally (by resolution or ordinance) or informally (by maintaining the road and exercising control). An offered dedication that is never accepted does not create a public ROW. The surveyor should verify acceptance when dedication is in question.

## Verifying ROW width

Do not assume a standard ROW width. Steps:

1. **Check the recorded plat.** If the road is in a platted subdivision, the plat dimensions control.
2. **Check road records.** County highway departments and municipal engineering departments maintain ROW records, right-of-way maps, and road plans.
3. **Check for additional dedications.** Road widening may have been accomplished by separate deeds or plat amendments.
4. **Check condemnation records.** The county clerk's office has eminent domain records.
5. **Check state highway records.** For state routes, INDOT maintains ROW plans accessible through the INDOT Real Estate Division.

### Common ROW widths in Indiana

These are typical, not universal:

| Road type | Common ROW width |
|---|---|
| County road (older) | 30 ft to 40 ft |
| County road (modern) | 50 ft to 60 ft |
| Municipal residential street | 50 ft to 60 ft |
| Municipal collector street | 60 ft to 80 ft |
| State highway (two-lane rural) | 80 ft to 100 ft |
| State highway (urban) | Varies widely |
| Interstate | 200 ft to 300 ft (varies) |

The actual width is determined by the recorded document, not by the table above.

## Surveyor's depiction

On a boundary survey or ALTA/NSPS Land Title Survey, the ROW line is shown as a boundary element with:

- The recording reference for the dedication (plat book and page, instrument number).
- The ROW width dimension.
- A note if the ROW width could not be determined from recorded documents.
- Any encroachments by the subject property into the ROW.
