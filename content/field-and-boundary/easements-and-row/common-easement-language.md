> **TL;DR**
> 1. Easement documents use specific phrasing — "over, across, and through," "a strip of land ___ feet in width," "lying ___ feet on each side of centerline" — and each phrase has a distinct legal and spatial meaning the surveyor must correctly interpret.
> 2. Blanket easements ("over the entire parcel") have no fixed location and cannot be plotted as a boundary on a survey. Specific easements include a legal description or a reference to a defined corridor.
> 3. When language is ambiguous, the surveyor notes the ambiguity, depicts the most reasonable interpretation, and advises the client to seek legal counsel.

## Standard granting phrases

Easement documents typically include a granting clause that defines the right. Common phrases:

- **"An easement over, across, and through..."** — grants the right to use the surface and subsurface of the described area. This is the broadest common phrasing.
- **"An easement over and across..."** — same as above in most jurisdictions; the omission of "through" rarely narrows the right in practice.
- **"An easement for ingress and egress"** — limits the use to access (passage). Does not include the right to install utilities or structures unless separately granted.
- **"An easement for public utilities"** — grants the right to install, maintain, repair, and replace utility infrastructure. The specific utilities covered depend on the document's definitions.
- **"Together with the right to trim, cut, and remove trees and vegetation"** — a maintenance right that often accompanies utility easements.

## Width and centerline references

### Fixed-width easement

"A strip of land 20 feet in width" — the 20 ft is the total width. The location is then defined by a centerline, one edge, or a reference to a boundary.

### Centerline-based

"A strip of land 20 feet in width, lying 10 feet on each side of the following described centerline..." — symmetric about the centerline. The centerline is described by bearings and distances or by reference to an existing feature (a pipeline, a ditch).

### Offset

"A strip of land 20 feet in width, the westerly line of which is described as follows..." — the entire width extends easterly from the described line. This is common for utility easements along property boundaries.

### Adjacent to boundary

"A strip of land 15 feet in width lying adjacent to and northerly of the southerly line of said lot..." — the easement abuts the property boundary and extends inward. The boundary itself is not the centerline; the boundary is one edge.

## Blanket vs. specific easements

### Blanket easement

"An easement over the entirety of Lot 5 for the installation and maintenance of underground utilities." This easement has no fixed corridor. It cannot be depicted as a strip on a survey. The surveyor should note its existence with a general note referencing the recording information.

Blanket easements are common in older plats and early utility franchise agreements. They are disfavored in modern practice because they restrict development of the entire parcel.

### Specific easement

"An easement over that part of Lot 5 described as: Beginning at the northwest corner of said Lot 5; thence S 00-15-22 E along the westerly line of said lot, 150.00 feet..." This easement has a defined location that the surveyor can plot from the legal description.

## Utility easement vs. access easement

The purpose clause controls what the easement holder can do:

- **Utility easement** — allows installation, maintenance, repair, replacement, and sometimes relocation of utility infrastructure. The owner retains title but cannot build permanent structures within the easement. Trees may be removed at the utility's discretion.
- **Access easement** — allows passage by foot, vehicle, or both. The owner retains title. The access holder typically may maintain the surface (grading, gravel, paving) but may not install utilities unless separately authorized.
- **Drainage easement** — allows storm water to flow across the parcel. The owner typically may not fill, obstruct, or alter the grade within the easement. Maintenance responsibility varies by document.
- **Conservation easement** — restricts development. Governed in Indiana by IC 32-23-5. The easement holder (often a land trust) has enforcement rights.

## Interpreting ambiguous language

Easement documents are frequently ambiguous. Common ambiguities:

- **Width unstated.** "An easement for a sanitary sewer" with no width specified. Indiana courts may imply a reasonable width based on the purpose. The surveyor cannot determine the width; the client needs legal counsel.
- **Location unstated.** A blanket utility easement with no description of where the utilities will go. The easement exists but has no plottable boundaries.
- **"As built" vs. "as described."** A pipeline easement may describe a centerline that does not match the as-built pipeline location. The surveyor should show both if both are known.
- **Conflicting dimensions.** An easement described as "25 feet in width" with a legal description that scales to 20 feet. The legal description typically controls, but the surveyor should note the conflict.

When the language is genuinely ambiguous, the surveyor depicts the most reasonable interpretation, labels it as approximate or interpreted, and includes a note recommending that the client consult an attorney for a legal determination.
