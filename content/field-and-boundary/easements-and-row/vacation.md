> **TL;DR**
> 1. Vacation is the legal process of extinguishing a public easement or ROW. In Indiana, it requires a petition, notice, a hearing before the appropriate body (plan commission, BPW, or legislative body), and recording of the vacation order.
> 2. When a public street is vacated, the underlying fee typically reverts to the abutting property owners to the centerline of the vacated street (unless the dedication conveyed fee to the municipality).
> 3. Partial vacations are common — vacating a cul-de-sac bulb, an alley, or excess ROW width. The surveyor must determine the exact area vacated and depict the revised property boundaries.

## Vacation vs. abandonment

Vacation and abandonment are distinct concepts:

- **Vacation** is an affirmative legal act — a governmental body formally extinguishes the public right by ordinance, resolution, or court order. The vacation instrument is recorded.
- **Abandonment** occurs when the public entity ceases to use and maintain a public way and manifests an intent to abandon. Abandonment is harder to prove and is less certain than formal vacation.

Surveyors should look for recorded vacation instruments. A road that "looks abandoned" (overgrown, barricaded, not maintained) may not be legally vacated, and the public ROW may still exist.

## Indiana vacation procedures

### Public streets and ROW

Under IC 36-9-2, a public way may be vacated by:

1. **Petition.** Filed by the abutting property owners (or their authorized agent) with the appropriate body. For municipalities with a board of public works (BPW), the petition goes to the BPW. For others, the petition goes to the legislative body (city council, town council, county commissioners).
2. **Notice.** Published notice and written notice to affected parties, including utility companies with facilities in the ROW.
3. **Hearing.** The body holds a public hearing. It considers the public interest, the impact on access, and the effect on utilities.
4. **Order.** If the body approves, it issues an order or ordinance vacating the ROW. The order may impose conditions (retention of a utility easement within the former ROW, for example).
5. **Recording.** The vacation order is recorded with the county recorder. Until recorded, the vacation is not effective against subsequent purchasers.

### Platted streets and alleys

Under IC 36-7-3-12, a platted street, alley, or public area may be vacated by petition to the plan commission (if the subdivision control ordinance so provides) or to the legislative body. The process is similar to the general vacation process.

### Utility easements

Private utility easements are vacated by agreement between the easement holder and the property owner, recorded as a release or quitclaim. If the utility has abandoned its facilities and has no further need for the easement, it may execute a release upon request. Some utilities charge a fee for easement releases.

Public utility easements dedicated on a plat require the same vacation process as public streets, or a release from each utility that holds rights under the easement.

## Effect on property rights

### Fee reversion

When a public street is vacated, the underlying fee reverts to the abutting owners. In Indiana, the general rule is that each abutting owner takes to the centerline of the vacated street. This means:

- Owner A on the north side receives the north half of the former ROW.
- Owner B on the south side receives the south half.
- If the ROW was asymmetric, each owner receives to the centerline, not to the original property line.

This reversion is automatic upon vacation unless the dedication conveyed fee to the municipality (unusual in Indiana) or the vacation order specifies otherwise.

### Retained easements

Vacation orders frequently retain a utility easement within the former ROW. The surveyor must read the vacation order carefully to determine what rights survive. A typical condition: "The City retains a perpetual easement for existing public utilities within the vacated right-of-way."

### Partial vacation

A partial vacation extinguishes the public right over part of the ROW while retaining it over the remainder. Common examples:

- Vacating the turnaround portion of a cul-de-sac when the street is extended.
- Vacating excess ROW width (reducing a 100 ft ROW to 60 ft).
- Vacating an alley while retaining the adjacent street.

The surveyor must determine the precise limits of the partial vacation from the vacation order and legal description.

## Surveyor's depiction

On a survey or plat:

- Show the former ROW line as a dashed line labeled "Former R/W — Vacated per [recording reference]."
- Show the new property boundary resulting from reversion.
- Note any retained easements.
- Dimension the area gained by each abutting owner.
