> **TL;DR**
> 1. The standard of care for a surveyor is what a reasonably prudent surveyor would do under the same circumstances — not perfection, but competence. Failure to meet this standard is negligence.
> 2. Common claims against surveyors include boundary errors, staking mistakes, missed easements, and flood zone misrepresentation. Most arise from inadequate research or poor documentation rather than bad measurements.
> 3. Indiana's statute of limitations for professional negligence is two years from discovery of the injury (IC 34-11-2-4), subject to a ten-year statute of repose for improvements to real property (IC 32-30-1).
> 4. Defensive practices — thorough research, clear scope letters, comprehensive documentation, and E&O insurance — are the surveyor's best protection.

## Standard of care

The standard of care in a professional negligence claim against a surveyor is the degree of care, skill, and learning expected of a reasonably prudent member of the profession acting in the same or similar circumstances. It is not a guarantee of accuracy; it is a measure of process.

A surveyor who follows sound methodology, performs adequate research, uses properly calibrated equipment, and documents the work thoroughly has likely met the standard of care — even if the survey later proves to have an error. Conversely, a surveyor who skips deed research, ignores conflicting evidence, or fails to set monuments has likely fallen below the standard, even if the final line happens to be correct.

Expert testimony from another licensed surveyor is almost always required to establish the standard of care in litigation.

## Common claims

### Boundary errors

The most frequent claim. Typical causes:

- Failing to research the full chain of title or adjoiner deeds.
- Holding the wrong monument (ignoring the hierarchy of evidence).
- Failing to reconcile conflicting evidence or document the resolution.
- Using GIS/tax-parcel data as if it were a survey record.
- Ignoring occupation evidence (fences, hedgerows, improvements).

### Staking errors

Construction staking mistakes can cause structures to be built in the wrong location — on the wrong lot, over setback lines, or in easements. These claims often involve significant damages because the structure is already built by the time the error is discovered.

### Missed easements

Failure to identify and depict recorded easements on an ALTA or boundary survey. This usually results from incomplete title research or failure to read all Schedule B-II exceptions in the title commitment.

### Flood zone misrepresentation

Showing a building site as outside a FEMA flood zone when it is within one, or vice versa. If the survey shows a flood zone line, the surveyor should note the source (FIRM panel number, effective date) and state the limitations of the depiction.

## E&O insurance

Errors and omissions (E&O) insurance is not legally required in Indiana for licensed surveyors, but it is strongly advisable. Most institutional clients (title companies, lenders, government agencies) require it as a condition of engagement.

Typical policy considerations:

- **Claims-made vs. occurrence.** Most surveyor E&O policies are claims-made, meaning the policy in force when the claim is made (not when the work was done) is the responding policy. The surveyor must maintain continuous coverage or purchase tail coverage upon retirement.
- **Coverage limits.** Common limits are $500,000 to $2,000,000 per claim. The appropriate limit depends on the type of work and the size of projects.
- **Exclusions.** Most policies exclude intentional acts, criminal conduct, and contractual liability beyond professional negligence. Some exclude specific services (environmental, subsurface).
- **Deductible.** Typically $5,000 to $25,000 per claim.

## Statute of limitations and repose

Indiana has two overlapping time limits:

- **Statute of limitations (IC 34-11-2-4).** An action for injury to property (including professional negligence claims against surveyors) must be brought within **two years** after the cause of action accrues. Accrual occurs when the plaintiff knew or should have discovered the injury.
- **Statute of repose (IC 32-30-1).** For deficiencies in the design, planning, or supervision of improvements to real property, no action may be brought more than **ten years** after substantial completion of the improvement. This statute protects design professionals, including surveyors whose work is part of a construction project.

The statute of repose is an absolute bar — even if the injury is not discovered until year nine, the claim must be filed before year ten. However, the statute of repose applies only to "improvements to real property," and courts have not uniformly treated boundary surveys (as opposed to construction surveys) as falling within its scope. A boundary survey that is not connected to an improvement project may be subject only to the two-year statute of limitations with a discovery rule and no outer repose period.

## Defensive practices

1. **Define the scope in writing.** Before beginning work, provide a written scope letter or proposal that states what will be surveyed, what standard applies (ALTA, boundary, topographic), and what is excluded. A clear scope prevents the client from later claiming the surveyor should have done more.

2. **Research thoroughly.** Read the full chain of title. Read adjoiner deeds. Obtain the title commitment and read all Schedule B-II exceptions. Check county surveyor corner records. A surveyor who can demonstrate thorough research is in a strong defensive position.

3. **Document everything.** Field notes, adjustment reports, research notes, communications with the client, basis-of-bearing computations, photographs of found monuments. If it is not documented, it did not happen.

4. **Write a narrative.** For boundary surveys, a narrative in the surveyor's report explaining the reasoning behind each boundary decision is the single most valuable defensive document. See [Surveyor's report (Indiana)](../monuments-and-evidence/surveyors-report-indiana.md).

5. **Carry adequate insurance.** E&O insurance protects both the surveyor and the client. Maintain continuous coverage.

6. **Know when to say no.** If the scope is unclear, the fee is inadequate, or the client is pressuring for a predetermined result, decline the work.
