> **TL;DR**
> 1. Indiana subdivision control is authorized by IC 36-7-4-700 series. Local plan commissions adopt subdivision control ordinances that specify design standards, platting requirements, and approval procedures.
> 2. The surveyor's work feeds the entire subdivision pipeline: boundary survey, topographic survey, preliminary plat, construction staking, and final (record) plat.
> 3. Common municipal design standards include minimum lot widths of 50 ft to 80 ft (varies by zoning district), maximum block lengths of 1,200 ft to 1,600 ft, street right-of-way widths of 50 ft to 60 ft for local streets, and utility easement widths of 10 ft to 20 ft along lot lines.
> 4. The surveyor should understand the approval pipeline — sketch plan, preliminary plat, improvement plans, final plat — because timing errors can delay or derail a project.

## Indiana statutory framework

IC 36-7-4-700 series grants local plan commissions the authority to regulate the subdivision of land within their jurisdictions. Key provisions:

- **IC 36-7-4-701:** Defines "subdivision" broadly. Dividing any parcel into two or more lots for the purpose of sale or building development triggers the statute, unless an exemption applies.
- **IC 36-7-4-702:** Exemptions from platting. Common exemptions include divisions by court order, divisions along existing public right-of-way where no new streets are created, and agricultural divisions (subject to local interpretation).
- **IC 36-7-4-711:** Primary plat (preliminary plat) requirements.
- **IC 36-7-4-713:** Secondary plat (final/record plat) requirements.

Local plan commissions adopt ordinances that implement the statute. These ordinances vary significantly between jurisdictions. Hamilton County municipalities (Carmel, Fishers, Westfield, Noblesville) each have their own subdivision control ordinance with different design standards and procedures.

## Approval pipeline

A typical subdivision goes through these stages:

### 1. Sketch plan (optional but common)

An informal submission to the plan commission or planning staff showing the general concept: number of lots, street layout, utility connections. No formal action is taken; the purpose is to identify issues early. The surveyor may prepare the base map from a preliminary boundary and topographic survey.

### 2. Preliminary plat

A detailed plat submission showing:

- Boundary of the subdivision with bearings, distances, and area.
- Proposed lot layout with dimensions, areas, and lot/block numbers.
- Proposed streets with names, widths, and right-of-way.
- Proposed easements (utility, drainage, access).
- Existing and proposed topography (1 ft or 2 ft contour interval, depending on the jurisdiction).
- Floodplain boundaries, wetlands, and other environmental constraints.
- Proposed stormwater management (detention/retention basin locations and sizes).

The plan commission reviews the preliminary plat at a public hearing and approves, approves with conditions, or denies it. Approval of the preliminary plat grants the developer the right to proceed with improvement plans, subject to any conditions.

### 3. Improvement plans

Engineering plans for streets, storm sewers, sanitary sewers, water mains, grading, and erosion control. These are prepared by a professional engineer, but the surveyor provides the base survey (boundary and topographic data) and may perform construction staking during the build.

The improvement plans are reviewed by the plan commission's technical advisory committee, the county or municipal engineer, and the utility providers.

### 4. Final (record) plat

The final plat is the legal document that creates the subdivision. It is prepared after the improvements are substantially complete (or bonded). Content requirements are described in [Survey plat preparation](survey-plat-preparation.md).

The plan commission approves the final plat. After approval, the plat is signed by the appropriate officials and recorded with the county recorder. Upon recording, the lots become legally separate parcels that can be conveyed individually.

## Common design standards

Local subdivision control ordinances typically specify:

### Lot standards

| Standard | Typical range |
|----------|---------------|
| Minimum lot width at building line | 50 ft (urban) to 80 ft (suburban) |
| Minimum lot area | 7,000 sq ft (urban) to 20,000 sq ft (suburban/rural) |
| Minimum lot depth | 100 ft to 150 ft |
| Lot width-to-depth ratio | Not to exceed 1:3 or 1:4 |
| Flag lots | Prohibited or restricted (minimum stem width 25 ft to 40 ft) |

### Block and street standards

| Standard | Typical range |
|----------|---------------|
| Maximum block length | 1,200 ft to 1,600 ft |
| Local street ROW width | 50 ft to 60 ft |
| Collector street ROW width | 60 ft to 80 ft |
| Cul-de-sac radius (ROW) | 50 ft to 60 ft |
| Minimum centerline curve radius | 150 ft (local) to 300 ft (collector) |
| Maximum cul-de-sac length | 600 ft to 1,000 ft |

### Easement standards

| Standard | Typical range |
|----------|---------------|
| Side/rear lot-line utility easement | 10 ft to 20 ft (5 ft to 10 ft each side of lot line) |
| Drainage easement | Width varies by drainage study; minimum 20 ft |
| Access easement (shared drive) | 20 ft to 24 ft |

### Setback lines

Setback lines are established by the zoning ordinance, not the subdivision ordinance, but they must be shown on the plat:

| Setback | Typical range |
|---------|---------------|
| Front | 25 ft to 30 ft |
| Side | 5 ft to 10 ft |
| Rear | 20 ft to 30 ft |

## Improvement bonding

When the developer records the final plat before all improvements are complete, the municipality requires a performance bond or letter of credit to guarantee completion. The bond amount is typically 110% to 125% of the estimated cost of remaining improvements.

A maintenance bond (typically 10% to 20% of improvement cost, lasting 1 to 3 years after acceptance) guarantees that the developer will repair defects in the improvements after the municipality accepts them.

## Variance and waiver process

If a proposed subdivision cannot meet a specific design standard, the developer may request a variance or waiver from the plan commission (or, in some jurisdictions, the board of zoning appeals). The applicant must demonstrate:

- Strict compliance would cause an unnecessary hardship.
- The variance will not be detrimental to adjacent property or the public interest.
- The spirit and intent of the ordinance will be observed.

Variances are granted on a case-by-case basis. The surveyor should identify potential variance issues early in the project and discuss them with the developer and the planning staff.

## Surveyor's role in the pipeline

The surveyor is involved at every stage:

1. **Boundary survey** — establishes the limits of the parent tract.
2. **Topographic survey** — provides the base for lot layout and engineering design.
3. **Preliminary plat preparation** — working with the developer and engineer to create the lot layout.
4. **Construction staking** — staking streets, utilities, grades, and lot corners during construction.
5. **Final plat preparation** — preparing the record plat, obtaining signatures, and recording.
6. **Individual lot surveys** — staking building corners and preparing lot surveys for homebuilders.
