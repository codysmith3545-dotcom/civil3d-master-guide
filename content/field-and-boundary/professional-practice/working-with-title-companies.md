> **TL;DR**
> 1. The title commitment is the surveyor's road map for easements and encumbrances. Read Schedule B-II exceptions line by line before going to the field — each exception is something the survey may need to show.
> 2. The title company needs the surveyor to certify the ALTA survey, note exceptions that are visible on the ground, and identify encroachments or conflicts. This certification enables the title company to issue survey-related endorsements.
> 3. Coordinate timing early. The title commitment must be in hand before fieldwork. Survey delivery must precede the closing date by enough time for the title company to review and issue endorsements.

## The title commitment

A title commitment (or title insurance commitment) is a preliminary report issued by a title insurance company that describes the conditions under which the company will insure title. It has three schedules:

- **Schedule A:** Identifies the proposed insured, the type of policy, the amount, the legal description, and the vesting (current owner).
- **Schedule B-I:** Requirements that must be satisfied before the policy will be issued (e.g., recording a deed, paying off liens, obtaining releases).
- **Schedule B-II:** Exceptions from coverage — matters that the title policy will not insure against. These are the items that matter most to the surveyor.

## Reading Schedule B-II for survey purposes

Each Schedule B-II exception is a potential survey item. Common types:

| Exception type | What the surveyor does |
|----------------|------------------------|
| Recorded easement (utility, access, drainage) | Locate from the recorded instrument. Plot on the survey with book/page or instrument number. Note visible evidence of use. |
| Setback lines (plat restriction) | Plot from the recorded plat. Note any improvements within the setback. |
| Encroachments noted in prior survey | Verify whether the encroachment still exists. Update the depiction. |
| Rights of parties in possession | Inspect the property. Note any occupants, tenants, or possessors other than the owner. |
| Unrecorded easements (blanket exception) | Look for visible evidence of utility lines, paths, driveways, or other uses that cross the property. Note what is found. |
| Mineral rights reservation | Not a survey item (subsurface). Note in schedule of exceptions but no fieldwork required. |
| Covenants and restrictions | Review for setbacks and use limitations that have a spatial dimension. Plot setback lines. |

If the surveyor cannot locate a recorded easement on the ground (e.g., a blanket utility easement with no visible facilities), the survey should note the exception by reference: "Easement to Indiana Electric per Instrument No. 2019-012345 — no visible evidence of use observed."

## What the title company needs from the surveyor

### ALTA certification

For an ALTA/NSPS Land Title Survey, the surveyor provides a certification statement on the plat. The standard certification language is prescribed by the 2021 ALTA/NSPS standards and reads (in part):

> "This is to certify that this map or plat and the survey on which it is based were made in accordance with the 2021 Minimum Standard Detail Requirements for ALTA/NSPS Land Title Surveys..."

The certification is addressed to specific parties — typically the buyer, the lender, and the title company. The surveyor should confirm the exact names and spelling with the title company or closing attorney before certifying.

### Exceptions noted on the survey

The title company reviews the survey to determine whether any Schedule B-II exceptions create a risk that should be excluded from the policy. If the survey shows an encroachment, a gap between the legal description and the fence line, or an easement conflict, the title company may:

- Except the matter from coverage.
- Require the seller to cure the problem before closing.
- Issue an endorsement insuring over the matter (if the underwriter approves).

### Survey-related endorsements

Common title insurance endorsements that rely on the survey:

- **Survey endorsement (ALTA 25 series):** Insures against loss from encroachments, boundary discrepancies, and matters that would have been disclosed by an accurate survey.
- **Location endorsement:** Insures that the improvements are within the boundary lines.
- **Access endorsement:** Insures that the insured has legal access to a public road.
- **Zoning endorsement:** May reference the survey for building coverage and setback compliance.

The title company cannot issue these endorsements without a current, certified survey. This is why the survey must be delivered in time for the title company's review.

## Workflow and timing

A typical ALTA survey coordinated with title follows this sequence:

1. **Client engages surveyor.** Scope letter specifies ALTA/NSPS survey with applicable Table A items.
2. **Surveyor requests title commitment.** The commitment must be received before fieldwork so the surveyor knows which easements and exceptions to locate.
3. **Surveyor reviews Schedule B-II.** Each exception is listed in a tracking log. The surveyor obtains copies of referenced instruments (easement deeds, plat restrictions, prior surveys) from the title company or county recorder.
4. **Fieldwork.** Boundary, monuments, improvements, easements, and evidence of use are collected.
5. **Draft survey preparation.** All Schedule B-II items are depicted or noted. Encroachments and conflicts are identified.
6. **Survey delivery.** The certified survey is delivered to the client, title company, and lender. Allow at least five to ten business days before closing for review and endorsement issuance.
7. **Title company review.** The title officer compares the survey to Schedule B-II. If new matters are found (unreported easements, encroachments), the title company decides how to handle them.
8. **Closing.** The survey is part of the closing package. The buyer and lender rely on it.

## Insured closing letters

An insured closing letter (ICL) is a letter from the title insurance underwriter to the lender stating that the title agent is authorized to issue policies. The surveyor does not issue an ICL, but the surveyor's certification is a prerequisite for the title agent to close under the ICL's terms. If the survey reveals problems, the ICL process may be delayed.

## Practical tips

- Ask the title company for a copy of every recorded instrument referenced in Schedule B-II. Do not assume you can find them all independently.
- If the title commitment is updated after you begin fieldwork, request the update immediately — new exceptions may have been added.
- If you find something in the field that is not in the title commitment (e.g., an unrecorded utility line, a neighbor's fence encroaching), note it on the survey and notify the title company. The title officer needs to know.
- Confirm the certification parties (exact names, entity types, addresses) before printing the final survey. Incorrect names can delay closing.
