> **TL;DR**
> 1. The surveyor's primary duty is to the public, not the client. The boundary is where it is, regardless of who hired you or what result they want.
> 2. A boundary opinion is a professional judgment based on evidence and law — not advocacy for a client's position. A surveyor who moves a line to please a client has committed fraud, not customer service.
> 3. Indiana's Professional Licensing Agency (IPLA) investigates complaints and can impose discipline up to license revocation. The complaint process is administrative, not judicial.
> 4. When in doubt about an ethical question, the answer is almost always "disclose, document, and decline if necessary."

## Duty to the public

Unlike many professions where the practitioner's primary obligation is to the client, the surveyor has a paramount duty to the public. Boundaries are public facts: they affect not only the client but also neighbors, future purchasers, lenders, and government agencies. A surveyor who places a boundary incorrectly to benefit a client harms everyone who later relies on that survey.

This duty is codified in IC 25-21.5-8, which lists prohibited acts including fraud, deceit, and negligence in the practice of surveying. It is also reflected in 865 IAC 1-12, which requires the surveyor to follow the evidence regardless of the client's wishes.

## Boundary opinion is professional judgment

The boundary survey is not an adversarial proceeding. The surveyor is a fact-finder:

- Gather the evidence (deeds, plats, monuments, occupation, testimony).
- Apply the law (hierarchy of evidence, senior/junior rights, rules of construction).
- Render a professional opinion on the location of the boundary.

The opinion must be based on the evidence. If the evidence supports a boundary that the client does not like, the surveyor explains the basis and stands by the opinion. The client is free to hire another surveyor for a second opinion or to challenge the boundary in court, but the original surveyor does not change the opinion to accommodate the client's wishes.

## Conflicts of interest

Situations that create a conflict of interest or the appearance of one:

- **Surveying for both parties in a boundary dispute.** Decline the second engagement. The surveyor's opinion should be based on evidence, but the appearance of impartiality is important.
- **Financial interest in the outcome.** A surveyor who owns adjacent land, has a business relationship with the developer, or will profit from a particular boundary location has a conflict. Disclose or decline.
- **Prior relationship with an adjoiner.** If the surveyor has previously surveyed the adjoining property and rendered a boundary opinion, that prior opinion may affect the new survey. Disclose the prior work to the new client.
- **Pressure from employers or clients.** A surveyor employed by a development firm may face pressure to locate boundaries favorably. The surveyor's professional obligation overrides the employment relationship.

## When to decline work

A surveyor should decline an engagement when:

- The client's objective is to obtain a predetermined boundary location rather than an honest opinion.
- The scope is inadequate to perform the work competently (e.g., insufficient budget for research).
- The surveyor lacks the competence or equipment for the specific project type.
- A conflict of interest exists that cannot be resolved by disclosure.
- The work would require the surveyor to violate 865 IAC or any other law.

Declining work is a professional act, not a business failure.

## Reporting boundary discrepancies

When a surveyor discovers that a boundary is different from what the parties believed — for example, a fence is 3 ft on the neighbor's side of the property line, or a driveway encroaches onto the subject property — the surveyor has an obligation to:

1. **Document the discrepancy** on the survey plat and in the report.
2. **Inform the client** of the discrepancy and its potential significance.
3. **Not conceal** the discrepancy to avoid conflict or to please the client.

The surveyor is not required to notify the neighbor (the neighbor is not the client), but some surveyors consider it good practice to do so as a courtesy. The surveyor should not offer legal advice about the consequences of the discrepancy — that is the attorney's role.

## Relationship with engineers and architects

Surveyors, engineers, and architects have overlapping but distinct scopes of practice:

- **Surveyors** establish boundaries, determine positions, and prepare legal descriptions and plats.
- **Engineers** design infrastructure and buildings within the framework established by the survey.
- **Architects** design structures.

An engineer who locates property lines is practicing surveying without a license (unless also licensed as a surveyor). A surveyor who designs a storm sewer is practicing engineering. Each profession should respect the scope boundaries and refer work appropriately.

In Indiana, IC 25-21.5-1-7 defines surveying, and IC 25-31-1-2 defines engineering. There is some overlap in practice (e.g., topographic mapping), but boundary determination is exclusively surveying.

## The IPLA complaint process

The Indiana Professional Licensing Agency (IPLA) administers the complaint process for surveyors:

1. **Filing.** Anyone may file a complaint — a client, a neighbor, another surveyor, a government agency. Complaints are filed with IPLA in writing.
2. **Investigation.** IPLA staff (often including a licensed surveyor serving as a technical consultant) investigate the complaint. They may review the survey, request records, and interview parties.
3. **Determination.** If the investigation finds probable cause of a violation, the case is referred to the Board of Registration for a hearing.
4. **Hearing.** The Board conducts an administrative hearing. The surveyor has the right to present evidence and be represented by counsel.
5. **Disposition.** The Board may dismiss the complaint, issue a letter of reprimand, impose probation, suspend the license, or revoke the license. The Board may also impose civil penalties.
6. **Appeal.** The surveyor may appeal the Board's decision to the courts.

The complaint process is separate from civil litigation. A surveyor may face both a licensing complaint and a civil lawsuit from the same incident.
