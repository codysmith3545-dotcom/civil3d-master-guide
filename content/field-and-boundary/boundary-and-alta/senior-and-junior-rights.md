> **TL;DR**
> 1. **First in time, first in right.** When a common owner conveys two or more parcels from a single tract at different times, the earlier conveyance is **senior** and controls the shared boundary. The later (junior) conveyance gets what remains.
> 2. A grantor cannot derogate from his own grant — the senior grantee's boundary is fixed at the moment of conveyance and cannot be diminished by a later deed from the same grantor.
> 3. For retracement, the surveyor must read the entire chain of title for the subject parcel and every adjoiner, determine which conveyance is senior along each shared line, and hold the senior boundary.

## The doctrine

The senior-junior rights doctrine is one of the most fundamental rules in boundary law. It applies whenever a single owner (the common grantor) divides a tract by successive conveyances:

1. **The first conveyance out** creates the senior parcel. The boundary described in that conveyance is fixed.
2. **The second conveyance out** creates the junior parcel. It takes whatever is left after the senior parcel is removed from the parent tract. If the junior description overlaps the senior boundary, the overlap belongs to the senior parcel. If a gap exists between them, the gap belongs to the grantor (or passes with the junior parcel if the grantor intended to convey the entire remainder).

The doctrine applies regardless of whether the parties know about it. A buyer who records a deed after a neighbor does not retroactively gain priority by paying more, occupying sooner, or having a better survey.

## How it controls common boundaries

On the ground, the practical effect is that the senior parcel's described boundary cannot be pushed by the junior description:

- If the senior deed calls for "the centerline of the creek" and the junior deed calls for a bearing-distance line that falls 10 feet east of the creek, the creek centerline controls. The junior parcel is 10 feet narrower than its deed says.
- If the senior deed calls for a specific distance and the junior deed calls for a different distance along the same line, the senior distance governs.
- If the common grantor made a surveying error and the two descriptions overlap, the senior parcel holds the overlap. The junior grantee's remedy is against the grantor, not against the senior grantee.

## Priority beyond the first conveyance

When the common grantor conveys three or more parcels in succession, the priority chain extends:

- Parcel A (conveyed first) is senior to Parcel B and Parcel C.
- Parcel B (conveyed second) is senior to Parcel C but junior to Parcel A.
- Parcel C (conveyed last) is junior to both.

Along the A-B shared boundary, A controls. Along the B-C shared boundary, B controls. Along the A-C shared boundary, A controls.

If a subsequent owner of Parcel B later subdivides it, the same doctrine applies within B: the first lot out of B is senior to the second, and so on.

## Recording acts and exceptions

Most states, including Indiana, have a race-notice recording statute. Under Indiana's recording act (IC 32-21-4), a subsequent purchaser for value who records first and takes without notice of a prior unrecorded conveyance may prevail. This means that priority is determined by the order of recording, not the order of signing, when one conveyance is unrecorded and the subsequent buyer had no actual or constructive notice of it.

In practice, the surveyor resolves senior-junior questions by reading the recording dates and instrument numbers in the chain of title. When two deeds out of the same grantor are recorded on the same day, read the instrument numbers (earlier number is senior) or the execution dates.

## Practical implications for retracement

1. **Read every adjoiner deed.** The surveyor cannot determine which boundary is senior without reading the adjoiner's chain of title. A subject parcel that appears to occupy a certain area may be junior to an adjoiner whose deed was recorded a year earlier.
2. **Work from the senior outward.** Lay down the senior parcel's boundary first. Then fit the junior parcel to what remains. This is the opposite of the common mistake of surveying only the subject parcel and assuming the deed is right.
3. **Document the priority analysis.** On the plat or in the [boundary report](boundary-report-writing.md), explain which parcel is senior along each shared line and cite the recording references. This protects the surveyor and informs the title company.
4. **Gaps and overlaps.** If the analysis reveals a gap or overlap, report it. Do not silently adjust the junior boundary to eliminate the discrepancy — that masks the title issue and may mislead the client.

## Examples of conflicting deeds

**Example 1: Overlap.** Owner conveys Parcel A in 2005, describing its south line as "S 89 degrees 45 minutes 00 seconds E, 300.00 feet." In 2007, the same owner conveys the remainder as Parcel B, describing B's north line as "N 89 degrees 42 minutes 30 seconds W, 300.00 feet." The two lines are not collinear — they create a 2.5-foot overlap strip. Parcel A (2005, senior) holds its described line. Parcel B's north boundary is constrained to whatever is left.

**Example 2: Gap.** Owner conveys Parcel A in 1998 with a west boundary along a fence. In 2001, the owner conveys Parcel B with an east boundary "150.00 feet east of the centerline of the road." The fence is 155 feet east of the centerline. A 5-foot gap exists between the two parcels. The gap was never conveyed and remains with the grantor (or the grantor's successors).
