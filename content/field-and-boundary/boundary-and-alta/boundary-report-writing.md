> **TL;DR**
> 1. A boundary analysis report documents the **evidence found**, the **evidence not found**, the **reasoning chain** from evidence to boundary opinion, and the **conclusion**. It is the written record that defends the surveyor's decisions.
> 2. The report is not the plat. The plat shows the result; the report explains why the result is what it is. A plat without a supporting report invites the next surveyor to re-litigate every decision.
> 3. Write for two audiences: the **client** (who needs to understand the boundary and any risks) and the **attorney or court** (who may review the report years later in a dispute).

## Purpose

A boundary analysis report serves three purposes:

1. **Preservation of evidence.** Monuments rust, fences rot, witnesses die. The report captures what existed at the time of the survey.
2. **Explanation of decisions.** Every boundary survey involves judgment — which monument to hold, which deed to treat as senior, how to resolve a pincushion. The report explains those judgments so they can be evaluated by others.
3. **Standard of care.** A report that shows thorough research, careful field work, and reasoned analysis demonstrates that the surveyor met the standard of care. A report that merely lists coordinates without explanation does not.

## Components

A complete boundary analysis report includes the following sections. Not every survey needs every section in full, but the structure should be consistent.

### 1. Project identification

- Property address and legal description.
- Client name and purpose of the survey (sale, mortgage, boundary dispute, ALTA, etc.).
- Date of field work and date of report.
- Surveyor's name, license number, and firm.

### 2. Record research summary

- **Subject deed.** Recording reference (book/page or instrument number), grantor, grantee, date, legal description.
- **Chain of title.** Key instruments in the chain back to the common source or the original government patent.
- **Adjoiner deeds.** For each shared boundary, the adjoiner's recording reference and the relevant calls.
- **Recorded plats.** Subdivision plats, highway plats, railroad plats, or other recorded surveys affecting the boundary.
- **Prior surveys.** Surveys by other surveyors found during research, including any plats recorded with the county surveyor or county recorder.
- **County surveyor records.** Section-corner perpetuation records under IC 36-2-12, regulated-drain records, or other county surveyor files consulted.
- **Title commitment (if ALTA).** The Schedule B-II exceptions and how each was addressed.

For each record consulted, note the source (recorder's office, county surveyor, title company, etc.) and the date retrieved.

### 3. Field evidence

Describe what was found (and not found) in the field:

- **Monuments.** For each corner of the subject parcel, describe every monument found: type, condition, markings, coordinates. If no monument was found, state that explicitly.
- **Occupation evidence.** Fences, walls, hedgerows, ditches, driveways, buildings that indicate where the parties have been treating the boundary.
- **Natural features.** Creeks, ridges, tree lines, rock outcrops called for in the deeds.
- **Improvements.** Buildings, pavement, utilities within or near the boundary.
- **Pincushion corners.** If multiple monuments were found at a single corner, list all of them with coordinates and cap-stamp identification.
- **Evidence not found.** Specifically note any monument or feature called for in the record that was not found in the field. Absence of evidence is itself evidence and must be documented.

### 4. Analysis and reasoning

This is the core of the report. For each boundary line or corner where a judgment was required:

- State the controlling record (which deed or plat governs this line).
- Identify the evidence (monument, occupation, natural feature) that supports the boundary position.
- If evidence conflicts, explain how the conflict was resolved: hierarchy of calls, senior-junior rights, original-monument priority, or other applicable rule.
- If a pincushion was resolved, explain which monument was held and why the others were not. See [pincushion and adjoiners](pincushion-and-adjoiners.md).
- If no evidence was found and the boundary was established by measurement from other controlling points, explain the method.

Avoid conclusory statements without support. "The northeast corner was established as shown" tells the reader nothing. "The northeast corner was held at a 5/8-inch rebar with cap stamped 'ABC LS-98765', found in good condition, consistent with the 2012 Smith survey recorded as Instrument No. 2012-045678 and corroborated by the fence corner 0.15 feet south" tells the reader everything.

### 5. Opinion and conclusion

- State the boundary opinion: the surveyor's professional conclusion about where the boundary is, based on the evidence and analysis.
- Note any unresolved issues: gaps, overlaps, encroachments, conflicting senior claims, or missing evidence that the client or attorney should be aware of.
- Recommend follow-up actions if needed: quiet-title action, boundary-line agreement, corrective deed, or additional research.

### 6. Exhibits

- The survey plat (the primary graphic exhibit).
- Copies of key record documents referenced in the analysis (deeds, prior surveys, plat details).
- Photographs of key evidence (monuments, occupation, pincushions).
- Coordinate table, if applicable.

## Formatting for different audiences

### For the client

- Lead with the conclusion. The client wants to know where the boundary is and whether there are problems.
- Use plain language for the summary. Technical detail belongs in the analysis section.
- Identify any risks (encroachments, title issues, unresolved conflicts) and recommended next steps.

### For the attorney or court

- Emphasize the evidence chain. Attorneys evaluate whether the surveyor's opinion is supported by the record and the field evidence.
- Cite the legal principles applied (senior-junior rights, hierarchy of calls, original-monument priority) by name and source.
- Avoid advocacy. The surveyor's report is an expert opinion, not a brief. State the facts, explain the reasoning, and let the conclusion follow.

## Indiana-specific considerations

- **865 IAC 1-12** requires certain minimum content on the survey plat (seal, signature, basis of bearings, closure, monumentation). The report supplements the plat but does not replace it.
- **IC 36-2-12-14** governs the Surveyor's Report for drainage-board proceedings — a different document with specific statutory requirements. Do not confuse a boundary analysis report with the statutory Surveyor's Report.
- Indiana courts have recognized the hierarchy of calls and senior-junior rights in case law. Cite Indiana decisions when applicable and available.
