> **TL;DR**
> 1. A **pincushion** is a cluster of monuments — set by different surveyors at different times — all purporting to mark the same corner. It is evidence of disagreement, not evidence of multiple corners.
> 2. **Adjoiner harmony** is the principle that a surveyor should strive to hold a boundary position that is consistent with the record, the physical evidence, and the expectations of affected landowners, rather than introducing a new position that conflicts with established lines.
> 3. When multiple monuments exist at a corner, the surveyor must investigate which (if any) is original, document the reasoning, and set or hold a single corner with a written explanation.

## The pincushion problem

A pincushion corner results when successive surveyors arrive at slightly different positions for the same corner and each sets a new monument rather than holding an existing one. Over time, a cluster of rebars, pipes, or pins accumulates within a small area — sometimes within inches, sometimes within a foot or more.

Pincushions are not a modern phenomenon. They occur wherever:

- Multiple surveyors retrace the same corner using different control (different PLSS corners, different GPS sessions, different measurement equipment).
- Surveyors hold different interpretations of the record (senior vs. junior, different basis of bearings, different deed).
- Surveyors set monuments without searching for prior evidence or without reading adjoiner surveys.
- PLSS corner positions are disputed or poorly perpetuated.

## Why pincushions matter

A pincushion is a title problem in waiting. Each new monument creates a potential claim by a landowner who relied on it. If a surveyor sets a pin 0.30 feet north of an existing pin and a fence is built to the new pin, the neighbor's surveyor may disagree and place yet another pin. The result is uncertainty, expense, and potential litigation.

The professional obligation is to resolve the pincushion, not to add to it.

## Resolution methodology

When a surveyor encounters a pincushion (or suspects one may exist based on record research), the following steps apply:

### 1. Identify every monument

Locate and describe every monument in the cluster:
- Type (rebar, pipe, nail, concrete monument, axle, railroad spike).
- Condition (in place, leaning, disturbed, partially exposed).
- Markings (cap stamps identifying the surveyor's license number and firm).
- Position (coordinate each monument relative to the survey control).

### 2. Research the source of each monument

- Match cap stamps to license numbers and firms.
- Find the recorded plats or surveys that set each monument.
- Determine the date and purpose of each survey.
- Contact prior surveyors if possible — professional courtesy calls yield information that no record search can.

### 3. Determine which monument is controlling

Apply the evidence hierarchy (see [hierarchy of evidence](../monuments-and-evidence/hierarchy-of-evidence.md)):

- **Original monument, undisturbed:** controls. An original monument is one set by the surveyor who created the boundary — the platting surveyor for a subdivision lot, the GLO deputy surveyor for a section corner, or the original grantor's surveyor for a metes-and-bounds parcel.
- **Re-established original position:** if the original monument is gone, a position re-established from proportionate measurement, witness marks, or adjoiners' records may be controlling.
- **Subsequent survey monuments:** monuments set by later surveyors are evidence of those surveyors' opinions. They are useful as corroboration but do not override original evidence.
- **If no original evidence survives:** the position that best harmonizes with the overall pattern of evidence — adjoiner fences, occupation, multiple consistent surveys — is the most defensible choice.

### 4. Document the decision

On the survey plat or in the [boundary report](boundary-report-writing.md):

- Show all found monuments, with labels identifying each.
- State which monument was held and why.
- State which monuments were not held and why.
- If a new monument was set, explain why the existing monuments were insufficient.

Silence is the worst outcome. A plat that shows one rebar and ignores three others invites the next surveyor to re-open the question.

## Achieving adjoiner harmony

Adjoiner harmony is the surveyor's goal of establishing a boundary position that is consistent with the expectations of all affected landowners, the record, and the physical evidence. It does not mean compromising the record to avoid conflict — it means:

- **Holding stable lines.** A fence line that has been honored for decades, that is consistent with the deed within measurement tolerance, and that multiple surveyors have recognized is a harmonious line. Rejecting it in favor of a new calculation requires strong evidence that the line is wrong.
- **Communicating with adjoiners.** Before setting monuments on a disputed line, contact the adjoining landowners and their surveyors. A conversation often reveals evidence (prior surveys, agreements, construction records) that the record alone does not.
- **Reconciling conflicting surveys.** When two surveys disagree, read both. Determine which was performed with better control, which holds the senior record, and which is corroborated by other evidence. Do not adopt one blindly and do not split the difference without a legal basis for doing so.

## When to hold an existing monument vs. re-establish

**Hold the existing monument when:**

- It is original to the controlling survey and undisturbed.
- It is corroborated by adjoiners, occupation, and the record.
- Moving it would displace established lines and create a worse pincushion.

**Re-establish when:**

- The existing monument is demonstrably in the wrong location (e.g., a subsequent surveyor set it 2 feet off the original corner, and the original position can be recovered from witness monuments or proportionate measurement).
- The existing monument is from a survey that was based on incorrect assumptions (wrong section corner, wrong deed, wrong basis of bearings) and the correct position can be determined from the record.

In either case, document the decision. Explain why you held or why you moved. The next surveyor — and potentially a court — will read your notes.
