> **TL;DR**
> 1. The title commitment is the surveyor's controlling document for what to plot and what to certify against. Read it carefully before you go to the field.
> 2. Schedule A defines the insured estate and the legal description. Schedule B-1 lists requirements. **Schedule B-2 lists exceptions** — those are what you plot.
> 3. Every B-2 item must end up either plotted on the plat or noted "blanket / not plottable" with a citation, and any plotted easement must include a source instrument.

## What a commitment is

A title commitment is a title insurance company's offer to issue a policy in a specific form, subject to listed conditions and exceptions. It has three substantive schedules:

- **Schedule A** — the insured (buyer or lender), the policy amount, the estate (fee simple, leasehold, easement, etc.), and the legal description. The legal description on the commitment is the legal description the survey must match.
- **Schedule B-1** — requirements that must be met before the policy issues (releases, deeds, payoffs).
- **Schedule B-2** — exceptions to coverage. Standard exceptions are the printed ones the underwriter always includes. Special exceptions are the matters of record specific to this parcel.

The standard "survey exception" on B-1 (covering matters that an accurate survey would disclose) is what an ALTA/NSPS survey is meant to clear; once the survey is delivered the underwriter can drop it for extended coverage.

## Pre-fieldwork review

Before any field crew rolls, the project surveyor should:

1. Confirm Schedule A's legal description matches the deed of record. If not, ask the title officer.
2. Read every Schedule B-2 item.
3. For each B-2 item, request the **underlying recorded document**, not just the citation. Never assume what an exception covers from the title officer's summary.
4. Build a B-2 spreadsheet keyed by the title commitment's exception number. Columns: number, brief description, instrument and recording reference, plot status, fieldwork required, drawing notes.

## Mapping B-2 items

For each special exception, decide one of:

- **Plot it.** Easements with definite locations (a 20-foot drainage easement along the south line; a recorded right-of-way) are drawn with width, source instrument, and recording reference.
- **Plot the affected area.** When the location is described loosely ("a strip across the parcel") but is bounded by other features, draw the bounded area and label the source.
- **Note as blanket.** Many utility easements are recorded as blanket easements covering the entire parcel. Note them on the plat: "Item 8: Blanket easement to [utility], Inst. No. [x]; not plottable as a discrete location."
- **Note as not plottable, with reason.** When the description cannot be drawn from the document furnished — for example, an old easement referenced only by metes-and-bounds across an adjoiner — note that fact with the citation. Surveyors do not extrapolate.
- **Identify as not survey-related.** Tax liens, mortgages, leases without a memorandum, judgments, and similar items do not need to be plotted but should be acknowledged on the B-2 schedule with a "no survey content" note.

The 2021 standard expects each B-2 item to be addressed. Silence on a B-2 number invites a reviewer's comment.

## Reporting

The plat carries an **exceptions table** keyed to the commitment's numbering, with one of the following dispositions per row:

- "Plotted hereon as shown."
- "Plotted hereon, location approximate."
- "Blanket in nature; affects subject property."
- "Not plottable; document does not contain locatable description."
- "Does not affect the subject property" (when, on review, the exception is found to apply to a different parcel — only assert this in writing after a careful read).
- "Non-survey matter; not addressed by this survey."

When the title commitment was supplied with an effective date older than the standard's currency window, request an update before signing. An out-of-date commitment is a recurring underwriter pushback.

## Communicating with the title officer

- Send written questions on ambiguous exceptions; keep the email thread.
- If the survey reveals a fact that contradicts the commitment — an unrecorded easement honored on the ground, an apparent gap to the adjoiner — communicate it before signing. Title companies often issue a revised commitment.
- Surveyors do not opine on title; do not characterize an exception's legal effect. State what the document says and what is on the ground; the title officer and counsel decide significance.

## Common pitfalls

- Plotting a generic "10 ft utility easement along all lot lines" from a plat note without confirming the plat actually creates such an easement.
- Treating a blanket easement as if it were a 10-foot strip just because that is the typical width.
- Forgetting to reconcile the commitment legal description against the surveyed boundary; if they differ, both descriptions must appear on the plat with a clear note.
- Not flagging that the commitment is older than 90 days at signing.
