> **TL;DR**
> 1. A retracement survey re-establishes the boundary that was originally created — it does not create a new line. The retracing surveyor follows the original surveyor's footsteps, not the deed in the abstract.
> 2. Senior rights control. The earlier conveyance prevails; later conveyances take what is left.
> 3. Resolve pincushion corners and adjoiner conflicts in writing. Do not pick a monument silently.

## What retracement is

Retracement is the act of recovering the boundary as it was first established, using whatever evidence has survived: original monuments, original blazed lines, calls in the original deed, and corroborating records. The retracing surveyor's job is investigative, not creative. Boundary law treats the original survey, when its evidence can be found, as the controlling thing — even if its measurements are imperfect by modern standards.

This stands in contrast to an original survey, where the surveyor *creates* the line by setting monuments and writing the controlling description.

## Record research first

A retracement is built on the documentary record:

- **Subject deed** and the entire chain of title back to the original conveyance out of common ownership.
- **Adjoiner deeds** along every shared line. The senior adjoiner's description controls the shared line.
- **Recorded plats** and prior surveys.
- **Section corner records** (in PLSS jurisdictions). In Indiana, the county surveyor is statutory custodian under IC 36-2-12 and maintains a corner perpetuation file.
- **GLO field notes** for the original PLSS subdivision in Indiana — the 1815 Public Lands Survey for most of the state — give the original measurements and witness trees.
- **Tax maps** as a low-quality cross-check, never as authority.

## Junior and senior rights

When a single owner conveys two parcels at different times out of one larger tract, the earlier conveyance is **senior**. The later, **junior**, conveyance gets only what was left. If the descriptions overlap or leave a gap, the senior parcel wins along the shared line and the junior description is constrained.

This rule has practical bite at retracement time:

- Read every adjoiner deed, oldest first. Don't trust a tax map or a recent plat to sort the priority.
- If your subject is junior to an adjoiner, your line cannot push the adjoiner's senior line.
- If two of your adjoiners conflict with each other, the senior of the two controls; document the rationale.
- A common ownership grantor cannot derogate from his earlier grant; this is the principle behind the rule.

## The pincushion problem

A pincushion corner is one where multiple monuments — set by different surveyors at different times — appear within inches of each other. None of them are necessarily wrong; they are evidence of disagreement.

The retracing surveyor must not just pick the closest monument and walk away. Required steps:

1. Identify each monument: source, date, surveyor, condition.
2. Establish which monument, if any, is original to the controlling survey.
3. If the original is found and undisturbed, it controls. Note the others as evidence and explain why they were not held.
4. If the original is gone, fall back to the hierarchy of evidence: relocated original > original lines marked > calls > measurements. (See [hierarchy of evidence](../monuments-and-evidence/hierarchy-of-evidence.md).)
5. Write the rationale on the plat or in the report. A pincushion left unexplained invites the next surveyor to add another pin.

## Harmony with adjoiners

Modern retracement practice favors **harmony** — the surveyor who finds a stable line of monuments along a fence-and-tree-row that has been honored for decades, and whose dimensions are within reason of the deed, has likely found the originally-intended line.

Indicators of a harmonious line:

- Continuous occupation evidence (fence, hedgerow, ditch) along the line.
- Monuments set by multiple surveyors over time on or near the line.
- Adjoiner descriptions that close on the same line.
- Local custom and long-acquiesced-to use.

When you find harmony you write it up: cite the evidence and explain why the line you held is the line that has been honored. When you cannot find harmony — when occupation conflicts with deed, or two senior chains conflict — say so plainly, hold the senior record, and let the parties decide whether to litigate or to convey to cure.

## Common errors

- Trusting GIS or tax-parcel data as if it were record.
- Treating modern measurements as superior to original calls when the original monuments are intact.
- Failing to read the adjoiner deeds before going to the field.
- Holding a measurement-perfect coordinate over a found, undisturbed original monument.
