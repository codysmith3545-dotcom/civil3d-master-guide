> **TL;DR**
> 1. The ALTA/NSPS Land Title Survey is a national survey product written so that title insurance underwriters will rely on it.
> 2. The current edition is the **2021 Minimum Standard Detail Requirements**, effective **February 23, 2021**, jointly adopted by ALTA and NSPS.
> 3. Five required deliverables: research, fieldwork, the plat or map, the surveyor's certification (the exact NSPS-specified language), and any optional Table A items the client requested.

## What an ALTA/NSPS survey is

An ALTA/NSPS Land Title Survey is a boundary survey performed to a uniform national standard so that a title insurance underwriter can issue extended (non-standard) coverage and so that lenders, buyers, and title companies in any state get the same baseline product. The standard is jointly written and maintained by the American Land Title Association (ALTA) and the National Society of Professional Surveyors (NSPS). It supplements — it does not replace — state minimum technical standards. Where state law is stricter, the surveyor follows state law.

The product is most commonly ordered for commercial real estate closings, refinancings, and large land assemblages. Residential transactions seldom require it; a boundary survey or mortgage location report usually suffices.

## Current edition

The standard in force is the **2021 Minimum Standard Detail Requirements for ALTA/NSPS Land Title Surveys**, effective February 23, 2021. It superseded the 2016 edition. There are no amendments as of the date of this page; check the NSPS site before quoting an ALTA. Editions are typically revisited on a five-year cycle.

## Required elements

The 2021 standard organizes requirements into sections. Summarized in plain English:

- **Section 3 — Research.** Surveyor obtains the title commitment with legible copies of all Schedule B-2 documents, and conducts whatever additional records research is appropriate. The client is responsible for furnishing the commitment.
- **Section 4 — Fieldwork.** The surveyor recovers and reports monumentation, observes corners, and locates evidence of possession, occupation, and improvements relevant to the boundary and to Schedule B-2 exceptions.
- **Section 5 — Plat or map.** A drawing showing the boundary, monumentation found and set, exceptions plotted (or noted as not plotted with a reason), improvements, easements, encroachments, and the standard set of notes and certifications.
- **Section 6 — Certification.** The surveyor signs the **specific certification language** prescribed in Section 6 of the standard. Surveyors should not edit that language; doing so may void underwriter reliance.
- **Section 7 — Deliverables.** Hard copy and/or electronic deliverables as agreed.

In addition, **Table A** lists optional items (typically 1 through 20). The client and surveyor negotiate which items the client wants and the fee reflects that scope. See [ALTA/NSPS Table A items](alta-table-a.md).

## Who relies on it

- **Title insurance underwriters.** They are the principal audience. Extended coverage requires an ALTA so they can clear out the standard "survey exception" on Schedule B-1.
- **Lenders.** Commercial lenders almost always require an ALTA before closing.
- **Buyers and sellers.** They use the survey to confirm what they are conveying and what burdens run with the land.
- **Tenants** in long-term commercial leases.

The certification block names the specific parties who may rely on the survey. Adding or removing names after the fact is a re-certification; surveyors should not list parties they have not vetted.

## Common pitfalls

- **Stale title commitment.** The standard requires a current commitment. A six-month-old commitment will leave new exceptions unaddressed.
- **Editing the certification.** Underwriters compare the signed certification to the published 2021 language. Any deviation triggers a redo.
- **Table A items quoted but not delivered.** If item 11 (utilities) is on the certification, the corresponding work and notes must be on the plat.
- **Out-of-state work.** ALTA/NSPS does not waive licensure. The signing surveyor must be licensed in the jurisdiction where the property lies.
