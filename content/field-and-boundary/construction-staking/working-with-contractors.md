> **TL;DR**
> 1. Attend the **pre-construction meeting** and establish marking conventions, offset distances, contact procedures, and re-stake protocols with the superintendent before the first stake goes in.
> 2. Respond to **re-stake requests promptly** — the contractor's crew is idle or improvising while they wait, and both cost money.
> 3. Understand the **liability boundary**: the surveyor is responsible for the accuracy of the staked position; the contractor is responsible for building to that position. Document everything.

## Pre-construction meeting

The pre-construction meeting (pre-con) sets the ground rules. The surveyor should attend and cover:

- **Marking conventions.** What color flagging, how lath is marked (cut/fill, station, offset), whether hubs and tacks or lath only. See [lath conventions](lath-conventions.md).
- **Offset distances.** Agree on standard offsets (e.g., 5 ft for curb, 10 ft for utilities). The contractor may request specific offsets to suit their equipment.
- **Reference line.** Confirm whether stakes reference back of curb, face of curb, flow line, pipe centerline, etc.
- **Grade reference surface.** Subgrade, top of stone, or finished grade? This changes every phase of construction — agree on how the reference will be communicated as the project progresses.
- **Communication protocol.** Who calls the surveyor for re-stakes? How much lead time is needed? (24 to 48 hours is typical.) What is the emergency contact number?
- **Staking schedule.** Coordinate with the construction schedule so stakes are in the ground before the crew needs them, but not so far in advance that they get destroyed.
- **Safety requirements.** Hard hat, high-visibility vest, steel-toe boots, traffic control plan, confined-space protocol if manhole work is involved.

## Communication during construction

Good communication prevents expensive mistakes.

- **Check in with the superintendent** at the start of each staking visit. Ask what they need, what has changed, and where their crew will be working. Avoid staking in an area where heavy equipment is operating.
- **Explain the stakes.** Not every laborer or equipment operator knows how to read survey lath. Take 5 minutes to walk the foreman through a few stakes and confirm they understand the markings.
- **Flag conflicts immediately.** If you find a discrepancy between the plan and the field (e.g., a utility conflict, a grade that does not work, an alignment that hits an existing structure), tell the superintendent and the engineer the same day. Do not stake known problems silently.
- **Keep a field diary.** Note the date, who you spoke to, what was discussed, and any decisions made. This record protects you if a dispute arises.

## Re-stake requests

Stakes get destroyed during construction — grading equipment, delivery trucks, weather, and foot traffic all take a toll. Plan for re-staking:

- **Budget for it.** A typical grading project may require 2 to 4 re-stake visits. Price accordingly.
- **Respond quickly.** A 24-hour turnaround is the standard expectation. If the contractor needs stakes to stay on schedule and you are unavailable, the project delays and the relationship suffers.
- **Re-check control** before re-staking. If control monuments have been disturbed during construction (common near active grading), you need to re-establish control before staking.
- **Document the re-stake.** Record which stakes were replaced and why. If the same stakes are destroyed repeatedly, discuss better protection measures with the contractor.

## Protecting stakes

Stakes are the contractor's reference points. When they are destroyed, work stops or goes wrong. Help protect them:

- Place stakes as far from the work zone as practical while still being useful.
- Ask the contractor to barricade critical stakes (cones, delineators, flagging perimeter).
- On long-term projects, consider semi-permanent marks (mag nails, PK nails in concrete, capped rebar) for control points that must survive months of construction.
- Photograph every stake after setting. The photograph documents that the stake existed and what it said.

## Liability and responsibility boundaries

The surveyor provides accurate positions and elevations. The contractor builds to them. The boundary is clear in principle but blurry in practice:

- **Surveyor's responsibility:** Correct coordinates, correct elevations, correct cut/fill computation, clear marking, timely delivery. If a pipe is set to the wrong invert because the surveyor computed the wrong elevation, that is the surveyor's error.
- **Contractor's responsibility:** Reading the stakes correctly, maintaining proper grade between stakes, protecting stakes, building to the marked references. If the pipe crew misreads a cut of 1.32 as 1.23, that is the contractor's error.
- **Gray areas:** Stakes set at the correct offset but the contractor does not pull back to the feature correctly. Or the contractor re-grades an area and does not request re-staking. Or the surveyor stakes from a superseded design. Document the design version, the staking date, and the communication to reduce ambiguity.

Carry professional liability insurance. In Indiana, surveyors engaged in construction staking should ensure their policy covers construction-related claims, not just boundary and mapping work.

## When things go wrong

Construction errors happen. When they involve the survey:

1. **Stop and assess.** Do not argue in the field. Gather the facts: what was staked, what was built, where is the discrepancy?
2. **Review the records.** Check your stakeout report, data collector raw file, and the design version used.
3. **Re-measure.** Go to the field and independently verify the as-built condition against the design.
4. **Report honestly.** If the error is yours, say so promptly. If it is not, present the documentation clearly and let the engineer and contractor resolve responsibility.
5. **Notify your insurance carrier** if there is a potential claim.

Defensiveness is the enemy of resolution. A clear record and honest communication resolve most disputes faster and cheaper than litigation.
