> **TL;DR**
> 1. Stake building corners with **offset stakes** (typically 5 to 10 ft outside the building footprint) because the actual corners will be excavated — the offsets survive and allow the contractor to string-line the foundation.
> 2. Verify the building rectangle by measuring **diagonals** — if opposite diagonals are equal (within 0.02 ft for residential, 0.01 ft for commercial), the corners are square.
> 3. Provide a **floor elevation reference** (benchmark on a stable feature) and the design finished floor elevation (FFE) so the contractor can set forms to the correct height.

## Preparing the stakeout

Before going to the field:

1. Extract building corner coordinates from the site plan. Confirm they are in the same coordinate system as the project control.
2. Verify the building setbacks against the site plan dimensions and zoning requirements. If the building shifts by even 1 ft, a setback violation can require demolition.
3. Obtain the design finished floor elevation (FFE) and the bottom-of-footing elevation.
4. Compute offset stake positions at the specified distance outside each corner, along the building face lines extended.

## Setting offset stakes

Drive hubs at the offset positions. Each hub and guard stake should show:

- Corner designation (e.g., "NW Corner," "A1," or the grid reference from the structural plans).
- Offset distance and direction to the building corner (e.g., "10' to corner, along N wall").
- Cut or fill to the FFE or top-of-footing, as requested by the contractor.
- FFE design elevation.

Offset stakes must be far enough from the building to survive foundation excavation. For a typical residential foundation, 5 to 10 ft is sufficient. For large commercial buildings with deep excavations, 15 to 25 ft may be needed.

## Batter boards

Batter boards are horizontal boards nailed to vertical posts at a fixed elevation (usually FFE or a convenient round number above FFE) at each offset position. The contractor stretches string lines between batter boards; the intersection of two string lines defines the building corner position.

The surveyor's role:

1. Set the batter board posts at the offset locations.
2. Level across all batter boards to a common elevation. The top of the batter board must be at the specified reference elevation, within 0.01 ft.
3. Mark the exact building face line on the top of each batter board with a nail or saw kerf. The string line is stretched to these marks.
4. Verify the string-line intersections match the design corner positions.

On some projects, the contractor sets batter boards and the surveyor only sets hubs. Clarify the division of responsibility at the pre-construction meeting.

## Diagonal verification

After setting all corners (or offset stakes), verify squareness:

1. Measure the diagonal distance between opposite corners (A to C and B to D for a rectangular building).
2. If the building is rectangular, the two diagonals must be equal.
3. Tolerance: within 0.02 ft for residential, 0.01 ft for commercial and institutional.
4. If the diagonals disagree beyond tolerance, re-check the corner coordinates and the field staking. A common cause is a transposed coordinate or a misidentified corner.

For non-rectangular buildings (L-shapes, complex footprints), break the footprint into rectangular components and check each rectangle's diagonals independently. Also verify key dimensions (wall lengths) between adjacent corners.

## Floor elevation reference

The contractor needs a stable, accessible elevation reference to set foundation forms and check finished floor height. Provide:

- A **temporary benchmark (TBM)** near the building — a PK nail in a curb, a mag nail in a stable concrete surface, or a driven rebar with a cap. Choose a location that will not be disturbed during construction.
- The TBM elevation, referenced to project datum (NAVD88 or project benchmark).
- The design FFE.
- The computed difference between TBM and FFE (e.g., "TBM is 2.34 ft below FFE").

Mark the TBM clearly and protect it with flagging. If the TBM is disturbed, the contractor will set forms to the wrong elevation.

## Foundation excavation limits

Some projects require staking the excavation limits — the perimeter of the area to be dug, including working room outside the foundation walls.

- Excavation limits are typically 2 to 3 ft outside the foundation wall, but the plans or specifications may specify more.
- Stake the excavation limit with offset stakes or paint lines on the ground.
- On sites with tight lot lines or adjacent structures, verify that the excavation will not encroach on neighboring property or undermine existing foundations.
