> **TL;DR**
> 1. Utility stakes are set at **offset positions** (typically 5 to 10 ft from the pipe centerline) with the **invert elevation, pipe size, and flow direction** written on the lath — the pipe crew builds to the invert, not the rim.
> 2. Stake every **structure location** (manholes, inlets, cleanouts, valve pits) and every change in alignment, grade, or pipe size.
> 3. Mark **trench depth references** so the excavation crew knows how deep to dig before the pipe arrives — a pipe laid on improperly graded bedding will fail the grade check.

## What to stake

For storm, sanitary, and water line construction, stake:

- **Structure centers** (manholes, inlets, catch basins, junction boxes, valve vaults). These are the primary control points for the pipe crew.
- **Pipe alignment** between structures, at 50 ft intervals or at every change in horizontal direction. The crew needs to know the pipe centerline position, not just the structure locations.
- **Alignment changes.** Any horizontal bend, vertical deflection point, or transition in pipe size or material.
- **Service lateral connections** (sanitary laterals to buildings, water service taps) if required by the scope.

## Offset stake placement

Pipe trenches destroy anything on the centerline. Set offset stakes perpendicular to the pipe run:

- Standard offset: 5 to 10 ft from the pipe centerline, on the side that will not be disturbed by spoil piles or equipment access.
- Use the same offset distance consistently along a run so the crew can pull a consistent string.
- For deep utilities (sanitary mains at 10 to 20 ft depth), use a wider offset (10 to 15 ft) to keep the stake outside the trench banks.

## Information on the lath

Each utility stake should show:

1. **Station or point ID** — matching the plan sheet reference.
2. **Offset distance and direction** — "10' LT to CL" with an arrow pointing toward the pipe.
3. **Invert elevation** — the inside bottom of the pipe at that station. This is the number the pipe crew cares about most.
4. **Cut to invert** — the depth from the existing ground at the stake to the design invert. The excavation crew uses this to gauge trench depth.
5. **Pipe size and material** — e.g., "15" RCP" or "8" PVC SDR-26."
6. **Flow direction** — an arrow indicating the downstream direction.

For structures, additionally note:

- **Rim elevation** (design, if specified; otherwise, match existing ground or finished grade).
- **All invert elevations** entering and leaving the structure, with pipe directions (e.g., "INV IN (N) 781.23, INV OUT (S) 781.05").
- **Structure type and size** (e.g., "48 in. MH" or "Type C inlet").

## Storm sewer staking

Storm sewer runs typically follow the plan profile. Key considerations:

- Pipe slopes are often steep (1% to 5% or more). Verify the invert elevations at each structure match the profile.
- Inlet locations must be coordinated with curb staking — the inlet is set into the curb and gutter, so horizontal position and flow line elevation must agree.
- Verify that the outlet structure (outfall, detention pond inlet) is staked with the correct invert. The entire run drains to this point.

## Sanitary sewer staking

Sanitary sewers have minimum slopes defined by pipe size (per Ten States Standards or local ordinance). Typical minimums:

| Pipe size | Minimum slope |
|---|---|
| 8 in. | 0.40% |
| 10 in. | 0.28% |
| 12 in. | 0.22% |
| 15 in. | 0.15% |

- Verify that design slopes meet or exceed these minimums. If a plan slope is below the minimum, flag it to the engineer.
- Sanitary lateral connections (wyes or tees) should be staked at the main line station where the lateral ties in, with the lateral invert noted.
- Manholes should be staked with all pipe inverts, including the invert of any drop connections.

## Water line staking

Water line staking differs from gravity sewer staking because water mains are pressurized and do not depend on invert slopes for flow. Key items:

- Stake the **horizontal alignment** at changes in direction (bends), tee intersections, valve locations, and hydrant locations.
- Provide the **depth of bury** (typically 42 to 48 in. of cover in Indiana, per local frost depth requirements; verify with the design and local code).
- Stake **fitting locations** (bends, tees, reducers) with the fitting type and size noted.
- Stake **hydrant locations** precisely — hydrants must be at the planned position relative to the street, sidewalk, and property line.
- Valve locations should be staked with the valve size and type.

## Trench depth references

The excavation crew digs the trench before the pipe crew lays pipe. Provide trench depth information on the lath:

- **Cut from ground to invert** gives the excavation crew a depth target.
- Account for **pipe bedding** (typically 4 to 6 in. of crushed stone below the pipe invert). The trench bottom must be 4 to 6 in. below the invert.
- On steeply sloping ground, the cut changes significantly from one stake to the next. Verify the cut at each stake independently rather than interpolating.
