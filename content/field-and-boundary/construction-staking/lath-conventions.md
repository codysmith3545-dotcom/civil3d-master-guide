> **TL;DR**
> 1. Every stake gets a **station (or point number), offset distance and direction, and cut or fill** written clearly in lumber crayon or permanent marker — the contractor reads stakes, not your data collector.
> 2. Use the **APWA uniform color code** for flagging: blue = water, green = sewer, red = electric, yellow = gas, orange = telecom, pink = temporary survey marks, white = proposed excavation.
> 3. Drive a **guard stake** (tall lath with flagging) next to every hub so it is visible before equipment runs it over.

## Anatomy of a marked stake

A properly marked stake communicates four pieces of information at a glance:

1. **Identity.** Station and offset, or point number and description (e.g., "STA 4+50, 25' RT" or "MH-7").
2. **Offset.** Distance and direction from the stake to the design feature. An arrow on the lath points toward the feature.
3. **Cut or fill.** The vertical difference between the existing ground at the design point and the design elevation. Cut means material must be removed; fill means material must be added.
4. **Design elevation** (optional but common). Written below the cut/fill value so the contractor can verify with their own grade rod.

Example lath marking:

```
 STA 12+00
 25' LT  →
 C 1.32
 EL 782.45
```

This reads: station 12+00, 25 ft left of centerline (arrow points toward centerline), cut 1.32 ft, design elevation 782.45.

## Writing conventions

- Use **lumber crayon** (keel) or **permanent marker** (Sharpie industrial). Lumber crayon survives rain better than standard markers.
- Write on the **flat face** of the lath, facing the direction the contractor will approach.
- Write **large enough** to read from 10 ft away. Abbreviations: C = cut, F = fill, LT = left, RT = right, CL = centerline.
- Station increases left to right when facing the direction of increasing stationing. "Left" and "right" are defined looking in the direction of increasing stations.
- For non-alignment staking (buildings, structures), write the point number and a description instead of a station.

## Color coding (flagging)

Flagging tape tied to the top of the lath provides color-coded identification visible from a distance. The APWA Uniform Color Code is the industry standard:

| Color | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Pink | Temporary survey marks (your stakes) |
| Red | Electric, conduit, cables |
| Yellow | Gas, oil, steam |
| Orange | Telecom, cable TV, fiber optic |
| Blue | Water |
| Green | Sewer, storm drain |
| Purple | Reclaimed water |
| White | Proposed excavation limits |

For construction staking, **pink flagging** is the default for survey stakes. Some firms add a second color to indicate the feature type (e.g., pink + blue for water line staking). Agree on the convention with the contractor at the pre-construction meeting.

Some jurisdictions and contractors use specific flag colors for specific stake types (e.g., orange for slope stakes, blue for finish grade). When in doubt, ask.

## Guard stakes

A guard stake is a tall piece of lath (typically 36 to 48 in.) with flagging, driven next to a hub or tack. Its purpose is visibility — equipment operators see the tall flagged lath before they see (or run over) a 1 in. hub flush with the ground.

- Drive the guard stake 6 to 12 in. from the hub, on the side away from the feature being staked.
- Tie flagging at the top.
- Write the staking information on the guard stake, not the hub. The hub is the precise point; the guard stake carries the information.

## Hub and tack

For precise staking (building corners, control, curb returns), drive a wooden hub flush with the ground and set a tack (small nail) in the top of the hub at the exact design position. The guard stake next to the hub carries the station, offset, and cut/fill.

For less precise work (slope stakes, rough grading offsets), the lath itself driven at the approximate position is sufficient.

## Protecting stakes

Stakes get destroyed by weather, equipment, and people. To minimize losses:

- Photograph each stake after setting. The photo is your proof of what you staked and where.
- Ask the contractor to protect stakes (cones, flagging perimeter, equipment buffers).
- Budget for re-staking. On active construction sites, expect to replace 10 to 30% of stakes per visit.
- On high-traffic sites, consider offset stakes far enough from the work zone to survive, even if it means longer offsets.
