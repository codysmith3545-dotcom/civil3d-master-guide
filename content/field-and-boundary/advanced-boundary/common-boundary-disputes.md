> **TL;DR**
> 1. Most boundary disputes fall into a few patterns: fence-line vs deed-line discrepancies, structural encroachments, gaps and overlaps between adjacent deeds, and conflicting prior surveys. The surveyor encounters these routinely.
> 2. The surveyor's role is fact-finder — document what is, explain what the deeds say, and let the parties and their attorneys sort out the legal consequences. The surveyor does not adjudicate disputes.
> 3. When a dispute is apparent, recommend that the client consult legal counsel before taking action. Many disputes are resolved through boundary-line agreements, quitclaim deeds, or mediation rather than litigation.
> 4. The survey plat and report should show both the deed boundary and the occupation evidence, clearly distinguished, with enough detail for a court to understand the situation.

## Fence-line vs deed-line discrepancies

The single most common dispute pattern. A fence has been in place for years or decades, both neighbors treat it as the property line, and a new survey reveals that the deed boundary is elsewhere.

### What the surveyor does

- Locate the fence and measure its position relative to the deed boundary. Report the offset distance and direction (e.g., "Wood fence located 2.8 ft south of the deed line along the east boundary").
- Note the fence's condition, approximate age, and material.
- Determine whether the fence was intentionally placed as a boundary marker or for another purpose (livestock containment, pet enclosure, privacy).
- Show both the deed boundary and the fence on the plat, clearly labeled.
- In the report, note the discrepancy and state that the deed boundary was established from the record evidence. Do not opine on whether the fence constitutes a boundary by adverse possession or acquiescence — that is a legal determination.

### Possible legal outcomes (for the client's attorney to evaluate)

- **Fence is within tolerance of the deed line.** No dispute — the fence is close enough that it represents a reasonable placement of the line.
- **Adverse possession.** If the elements are met (see [Adverse possession](adverse-possession.md)), the occupier may have acquired title to the strip.
- **Acquiescence.** If both parties recognized the fence as the boundary over a long period, the fence may be the legal boundary by agreement. See [Acquiescence and practical location](acquiescence-and-practical-location.md).
- **Neither doctrine applies.** The deed boundary controls. The fence is simply in the wrong place. The property owner may need to relocate it or negotiate with the neighbor.

## Encroachments

An encroachment occurs when a structure, improvement, or use extends across a property boundary onto an adjacent parcel. Common types:

- **Building encroachments.** A house, garage, or shed that crosses the property line. These are high-stakes disputes because the structure cannot be easily relocated.
- **Driveway encroachments.** A driveway that extends onto the neighbor's land, sometimes by several feet.
- **Landscaping encroachments.** Retaining walls, planting beds, patios, and pools that cross the line.
- **Eave and overhang encroachments.** The building footprint is within the boundary, but the eaves or roof overhang extend across it.
- **Underground encroachments.** Foundations, footings, drain tiles, and utility lines that cross the boundary below grade. These are invisible without excavation or utility locates but may be revealed by as-built surveys.

### Surveyor's documentation

For each encroachment, the plat should show:

- The type and nature of the encroachment.
- The distance it extends past the boundary (to 0.1 ft or better).
- The length of the encroachment along the boundary.
- The story (above grade, at grade, below grade if known).

The ALTA/NSPS standards require that encroachments be shown on the survey. The title company relies on this information to determine whether to except the encroachment from coverage or to require resolution before closing.

## Gaps and overlaps

When adjacent deeds are plotted from their metes-and-bounds descriptions, the resulting parcels may not fit together perfectly:

- **Gap (hiatus):** A strip of land between two parcels that is not described in either deed. Neither owner has record title to the strip.
- **Overlap:** The descriptions of two adjacent parcels cover the same strip of land. Both owners have a claim to the strip.

### Causes

- Deed descriptions drafted at different times from different starting points without coordinating with the adjoiner's description.
- Rounding errors in distances or bearings compounded over a long chain of title.
- Incorrect legal descriptions based on tax maps or informal sketches rather than surveys.
- Ambiguous calls in older deeds (e.g., "to the big oak tree" when the tree is gone and its location uncertain).

### Surveyor's approach

1. Research both deed chains thoroughly. Determine seniority — the senior deed controls the shared line.
2. If a gap exists, identify the most likely original intent. Was the gap created by error or by intention? In most cases, it is error, and the senior deed's description controls.
3. If an overlap exists, the senior conveyance prevails. The junior parcel is constrained to the remainder.
4. Show the gap or overlap on the plat with dimensions. Explain the analysis in the report.
5. Recommend that the parties' attorneys resolve the gap or overlap by corrective deed, boundary-line agreement, or quiet-title action (IC 32-26-1).

## Conflicting prior surveys

When two or more prior surveys of the same boundary reach different conclusions, the current surveyor must reconcile them:

- Identify the basis of each prior survey: what evidence did the surveyor hold, and what methodology was used?
- Determine whether new evidence has emerged since the prior surveys.
- Apply the hierarchy of evidence to reach an independent conclusion.
- Document the prior surveys on the plat (showing each surveyor's monuments if still in place) and explain in the report why the current opinion agrees or disagrees with each prior survey.

Do not simply adopt a prior survey without independent analysis. The current surveyor is responsible for the current opinion.

## When to recommend mediation or legal counsel

The surveyor should recommend that the client consult an attorney when:

- An encroachment is significant (a structure, not just a fence post).
- A gap or overlap cannot be resolved by record analysis alone.
- Adverse possession or acquiescence is a likely factor.
- A neighbor disputes the survey and threatens legal action.
- The client asks the surveyor to "move the line" or "make it work" — the surveyor should explain that the boundary is a factual determination, not a negotiable position.

Many boundary disputes are resolved without litigation through:

- **Boundary-line agreements:** Both parties sign a recorded agreement establishing the agreed boundary. The surveyor prepares the legal description and plat for the agreement.
- **Quitclaim deeds:** One party quitclaims the disputed strip to the other, sometimes for nominal consideration.
- **Mediation:** A neutral mediator facilitates negotiation. The surveyor may be called to present findings.

## Documenting disputes on the plat

When a dispute exists, the plat should:

- Show the deed boundary as determined by the surveyor, clearly labeled as such.
- Show the occupation line (fence, edge of improvement, etc.) as a separate feature, clearly labeled.
- Note the discrepancy in a schedule of notes or in the report.
- Avoid language that implies a legal conclusion (e.g., "boundary by adverse possession" or "disputed boundary"). Use factual language: "Fence located 3.1 ft east of deed line" or "Garage encroaches 1.4 ft onto adjoining parcel per Deed Record 2005-123456."
