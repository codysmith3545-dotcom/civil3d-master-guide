> **TL;DR**
> 1. Acquiescence is the doctrine that a boundary line recognized by adjoining landowners over a long period becomes the legal boundary, even if it differs from the deed line. Unlike adverse possession, acquiescence is based on mutual agreement — implied from conduct — rather than hostility.
> 2. Practical location is a related concept: when the original surveyor's line is uncertain but the parties have treated a specific physical feature (fence, hedge, ditch) as the boundary for an extended period, that feature may be held as the boundary by practical location.
> 3. Indiana courts have recognized both doctrines, though the case law is fact-intensive and the boundary between acquiescence and adverse possession is not always sharp.
> 4. The surveyor documents the evidence for or against acquiescence — the physical features, their age, and the parties' conduct — but does not make the legal determination.

## Acquiescence defined

Acquiescence occurs when two adjacent landowners, by their conduct over an extended period, mutually recognize a line as their common boundary. The line becomes legally binding because both parties accepted it, not because one party forced it upon the other.

The key distinction from adverse possession:

| Factor | Acquiescence | Adverse possession |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Nature | Mutual agreement (implied from conduct) | Unilateral taking (hostile) |
| Intent | Both parties accept the line | Possessor claims against the true owner |
| Permission | Irrelevant — based on agreement | Must be without permission |
| Hostility | Not required | Required |
| Result | Boundary established by agreement | Title transferred to possessor |

## Elements of acquiescence in Indiana

Indiana courts have identified these elements, though the precise formulation varies by case:

### Mutual recognition

Both landowners must have treated the line as the boundary. Evidence of mutual recognition includes:

- Both parties maintained their respective sides of a fence without objecting to its location.
- Both parties described the fence or other feature as "the property line" to third parties.
- Neither party claimed or used land on the other's side of the line.
- Improvements on both sides respect the line.

If only one party recognizes the line and the other is unaware or indifferent, acquiescence may not be established. Mutual conduct is the essential element.

### Extended period

The recognition must persist over a substantial period. Indiana courts have not established a fixed number of years for acquiescence (unlike the 10-year statutory period for adverse possession). Some courts have looked to the adverse possession period as a guide; others have found acquiescence established in shorter periods when the evidence of mutual recognition is strong.

### Physical evidence

The acquiesced boundary is almost always marked by a physical feature: a fence, a hedge row, a ditch, a wall, a row of trees, or a cleared line. The feature need not be continuous or perfect, but it must be identifiable and must have served as the understood boundary.

### Uncertainty in the true line

Acquiescence is most readily applied when the true boundary was uncertain or disputed and the parties resolved the uncertainty by adopting a practical line. If both parties knew exactly where the deed line was and simply agreed to ignore it, the analysis may shift to an oral boundary-line agreement (which has its own requirements, including consideration and, ideally, a writing).

## Practical location

Practical location is closely related to acquiescence and is sometimes treated as a subset of it. The doctrine holds that when the original boundary cannot be determined with certainty from the record, and the parties (or their predecessors) have located the boundary on the ground and treated it as the line for an extended period, that practical location controls.

Practical location is particularly relevant in PLSS areas where:

- Original section corners or quarter corners have been lost and cannot be restored with certainty.
- The original survey was imprecise, and multiple interpretations of the line are plausible.
- Landowners subdivided their holdings informally, using fences or natural features as boundaries, decades before any professional survey was performed.

The practical-location doctrine gives legal weight to the on-the-ground reality when the paper record is ambiguous.

## Field evidence the surveyor documents

When acquiescence or practical location may be a factor, the surveyor should document:

### The physical feature

- Type (fence, hedge, ditch, wall, tree line).
- Location, measured precisely relative to the deed boundary.
- Length — does it extend the full length of the shared boundary or only part of it?
- Age and condition. An old, deteriorating fence with mature trees growing through it is more persuasive than a new fence.
- Construction history if determinable (wire type, post material, style consistent with a particular era).

### Improvements on each side

- Do improvements on both sides respect the feature as the boundary? If one neighbor's shed is built up to the fence and the other neighbor's garden extends to the fence, both treat it as the line.
- Are there any improvements that cross the feature, suggesting that it was not treated as the boundary?

### Maintenance patterns

- Who maintains each side? If Owner A mows to the fence and Owner B mows to the fence from the other side, both are treating the fence as the boundary.
- Has either party ever claimed or used land on the other's side of the feature?

### Testimony

The surveyor is not an attorney and does not take depositions, but statements from the landowners during fieldwork (e.g., "That fence has been there since my grandfather's time, and we've always treated it as the property line") are useful context. Note the statement in the field log with the speaker's name and date.

## Surveying implications

When evidence of acquiescence or practical location is present:

1. **Show both lines on the plat.** Depict the deed boundary as determined from the record and the occupation/fence line as a separate feature. Label each clearly.
2. **Measure the discrepancy.** State the offset between the deed line and the occupation line at key points.
3. **Describe the evidence in the report.** Note the type, age, and condition of the physical feature, and the apparent pattern of use on each side.
4. **Do not hold the fence as the boundary unless a court has so determined.** The survey should show the deed boundary as the primary boundary. The occupation evidence is informational.
5. **Recommend legal consultation.** If the discrepancy is significant (more than a foot or two) and affects improvements or property use, advise the client to consult an attorney about whether acquiescence may apply.

## Acquiescence vs boundary-line agreement

A boundary-line agreement is an express agreement between adjacent landowners to fix a boundary at a specific location. It is typically in writing and recorded. Acquiescence is implied from conduct — no written agreement exists.

If the parties want to formalize an acquiesced boundary, they can execute a boundary-line agreement and record it. The surveyor can prepare the legal description and plat for the agreed boundary.
