> **TL;DR**
> 1. Riparian rights attach to land bordering rivers and streams; littoral rights attach to land bordering lakes. In both cases, the water boundary is ambulatory — it moves with the water, subject to the accretion/avulsion distinction.
> 2. Accretion (gradual, imperceptible change in the shoreline) shifts the boundary with the water. Avulsion (sudden, violent change) does not — the boundary remains at the pre-avulsion location.
> 3. The ordinary high water mark (OHWM) is the critical line for most water boundaries, but determining it in the field requires judgment — look for vegetation changes, soil characteristics, debris lines, and bank morphology.
> 4. Indiana-specific rules govern navigable lakes (public trust doctrine under IC 14-26-2) and non-navigable streams (riparian owners typically own to the thread of the stream).

## Riparian vs littoral

The terms are often used interchangeably in casual practice, but they have distinct meanings:

- **Riparian:** Rights and boundaries associated with flowing water — rivers, streams, creeks.
- **Littoral:** Rights and boundaries associated with standing water — lakes, ponds, reservoirs.

The legal principles are similar but not identical, particularly regarding the thread-of-stream doctrine (which applies only to flowing water) and the public trust doctrine (which in Indiana applies primarily to navigable lakes).

## Ordinary high water mark (OHWM)

The OHWM is the line on the bank established by the fluctuations of water and indicated by physical characteristics. It is the boundary between the riparian/littoral owner's upland and the bed of the waterbody.

### Determining the OHWM in the field

There is no single, universal method. The surveyor looks for converging indicators:

- **Vegetation line:** The transition from aquatic or wetland vegetation to upland species. A clear change in plant community often marks the OHWM.
- **Soil characteristics:** Below the OHWM, soils are frequently saturated and may show hydric characteristics (gleying, mottling). Above, soils are better drained.
- **Debris and drift lines:** Accumulated organic debris (leaves, branches, seeds) deposited by high water events. Caution: a single storm event can deposit debris above the OHWM — look for persistent, repeated deposit patterns.
- **Bank morphology:** A break in slope, erosion scarp, or undercut bank at the point of ordinary high water.
- **Staining and discoloration:** Water marks on rocks, bridge abutments, or tree trunks.

No single indicator is conclusive. The surveyor should document multiple indicators and their positions, then form a professional opinion on the OHWM location. Photographs are essential.

### OHWM vs floodplain

The OHWM is not the same as the FEMA 100-year floodplain boundary. The floodplain is a regulatory construct based on statistical modeling. The OHWM is a physical and legal construct based on observed water action. The OHWM is typically much closer to the water than the 100-year flood line.

## Accretion

Accretion is the gradual, imperceptible addition of land by the deposit of soil, sand, or sediment along a water boundary. Key legal principles:

- The addition must be **gradual and imperceptible** — you cannot watch it happen day to day, but over months or years the change is evident.
- The newly formed land (alluvion) belongs to the riparian/littoral owner whose land it adjoins.
- The property boundary moves with the accretion. The owner gains land.

## Reliction

Reliction is the gradual, imperceptible recession of water, exposing land that was previously submerged. The legal treatment is the same as accretion — the exposed land belongs to the adjacent riparian/littoral owner, and the boundary moves with the water.

## Avulsion

Avulsion is a sudden, perceptible change in the course or level of a waterbody — for example, a river cutting a new channel during a flood, or a lake level dropping suddenly due to a dam breach. The legal treatment is opposite to accretion:

- The property boundary **does not move** with the water. It remains at the pre-avulsion location.
- The owner of the land exposed by avulsion continues to own it, but the boundary stays where it was.
- The practical effect: after avulsion, the boundary may be in the middle of dry land or in the middle of a new water channel.

The distinction between accretion and avulsion can be difficult to determine in the field. A flood event that deposits several feet of sediment in a week is avulsion, not accretion. A gradual build-up of 6 in. of sediment per year over a decade is accretion. The surveyor documents what is observed; if the classification is disputed, it is a legal question for the courts.

## Thread of stream doctrine

For non-navigable streams in Indiana, each riparian owner owns the bed of the stream to the **thread** — the center line of the main channel. This means:

- If the stream runs through the middle of a tract, the owner owns the entire bed.
- If the stream forms a boundary between two owners, each owns to the center.
- The thread is the middle of the main channel at ordinary stage, not at flood or low water.

For navigable waters, the state holds title to the bed under the public trust doctrine. In Indiana, the distinction between navigable and non-navigable has been litigated repeatedly. IC 14-26-2 designates certain lakes as public freshwater lakes, giving the state authority over the bed below the legal lake level.

## Meander lines vs boundary lines

In PLSS states including Indiana, GLO surveyors ran meander lines along the banks of navigable rivers and lakes. These meander lines are **not** boundary lines. They are approximations of the water's edge, surveyed to compute the area of adjacent sections and fractional lots.

The actual boundary is the water (the OHWM or thread of the stream, depending on the waterbody type). If the water has moved since the original GLO survey, the boundary has moved with it (assuming accretion/reliction, not avulsion), even though the meander line on the GLO plat has not.

This distinction catches many surveyors off guard. Do not retrace the meander line and call it the boundary. The meander line is evidence of where the water was; the current water position (adjusted for accretion/avulsion) is the boundary.

## Indiana-specific considerations

- **Public freshwater lakes (IC 14-26-2):** The state holds the bed of public freshwater lakes in trust. Littoral owners own to the legal lake level but not below. The DNR regulates activities on and in public freshwater lakes.
- **Non-navigable streams:** Riparian owners own to the thread. The state has no claim to the bed unless a specific statute applies.
- **Drainage ditches and regulated drains:** Under IC 36-9-27, county-regulated drains have specific easement and maintenance provisions. These are not natural watercourses and the riparian/littoral doctrines do not apply in the same way. The drain easement, not the OHWM, controls the boundary of the encumbrance.
- **Reservoir boundaries:** Reservoirs created by damming are typically governed by the flowage easement or fee acquisition that created the reservoir, not by riparian law. The boundary is defined by the taking or easement, not by the current water level.

## Surveying water boundaries

Practical steps for a water-boundary survey:

1. **Research.** Determine whether the waterbody is navigable or non-navigable, natural or artificial. Obtain GLO plats and field notes. Review any DNR or Army Corps of Engineers permits or determinations.
2. **Determine the controlling boundary.** OHWM, thread of stream, legal lake level, or easement line — depending on the waterbody type and legal framework.
3. **Field observation.** Walk the bank. Identify and flag OHWM indicators. Take photographs at each flagged location with a scale reference. Record GPS positions.
4. **Document the methodology.** In the report, explain which indicators were used, where they converged, and how the OHWM line was determined. Note any areas of ambiguity.
5. **Show on the plat.** Depict the water boundary as an approximate line with a note such as "Approximate ordinary high water mark as determined by field observation on [date]." Do not imply false precision.
