> **TL;DR**
> 1. Adverse possession in Indiana requires possession that is actual, open, notorious, exclusive, continuous, hostile (without permission), and under claim of right for **10 years** (IC 32-21-7-1).
> 2. The surveyor does not determine whether adverse possession has occurred — that is a legal and judicial question. The surveyor documents the physical evidence of possession: fences, occupation lines, improvements, vegetation changes, and the relationship between those features and the deed boundary.
> 3. Evidence of adverse possession is common in Indiana, particularly in older urban neighborhoods and rural areas where fence lines have been in place for decades without a survey.
> 4. Adverse possession transfers title to the possessor. Prescriptive easement (IC 32-23-1) grants only a right to use, not ownership. The elements are similar but the outcomes are different.

## Elements of adverse possession in Indiana

IC 32-21-7 establishes the framework. Indiana courts have interpreted the statute to require:

### Actual possession

The claimant must physically occupy and use the land in a manner consistent with ownership. Mere entry or occasional use is not enough. Examples of actual possession: cultivating the land, maintaining a lawn, building and using a structure, enclosing the area with a fence.

### Open and notorious

The possession must be visible and apparent — such that the true owner, exercising reasonable diligence, would know that someone else is occupying the land. A hidden use (underground utilities, buried structures) may not satisfy this element.

### Exclusive

The claimant must possess the land to the exclusion of the true owner and the general public. Shared use with the true owner or with others undermines exclusivity. However, "exclusive" does not mean no one else ever sets foot on the land — it means the claimant possesses it as an owner would.

### Continuous

The possession must be uninterrupted for the entire statutory period. Seasonal use can qualify if it is consistent with how an owner would use the property (e.g., a lakeside lot used only in summer). Gaps in possession reset the clock unless the claimant can establish tacking — joining successive periods of possession by different claimants in privity (e.g., seller to buyer).

### Hostile (without permission)

The possession must be without the true owner's consent. If the owner gave permission — even informally — the possession is not hostile. A license (permission to use) can be revoked at any time and defeats a claim of adverse possession.

"Hostile" does not require ill intent. It means only that the possession is inconsistent with the true owner's rights and without permission.

### Under claim of right

The claimant must possess the land with the intent to claim it as their own. In Indiana, this element is sometimes subsumed into the hostility requirement. A claimant who knows the land belongs to someone else but occupies it openly and without permission is possessing under claim of right.

### Statutory period: 10 years

IC 32-21-7-1 requires 10 continuous years of adverse possession. Some special rules apply:

- **Payment of taxes.** IC 32-21-7-1(b) provides a shorter period of adverse possession (as few as 10 years with payment of property taxes) and strengthens the claim. Payment of taxes on the disputed area is strong evidence of adverse possession but is not an absolute requirement in Indiana.
- **Government land.** Adverse possession does not run against the state of Indiana or its political subdivisions.
- **Disabilities.** IC 32-21-7-2 extends the limitations period if the true owner is under a legal disability (minor, incompetent) at the time the adverse possession begins.

## Field evidence the surveyor looks for

During a boundary survey, the surveyor should document any evidence that the physical occupation of the property differs from the deed boundaries:

### Fences

The most common indicator. A fence that does not follow the deed line — particularly a fence that has been in place for decades — raises the question of adverse possession (or acquiescence; see [Acquiescence and practical location](acquiescence-and-practical-location.md)). Document:

- Location of the fence relative to the deed boundary (offset distance and direction).
- Age and condition of the fence (material, deterioration, repair history).
- Whether the fence encloses a maintained area (mowed lawn, garden) or appears to be a property-line fence.

### Improvements

Buildings, sheds, driveways, retaining walls, patios, and decks that cross the deed boundary. Measure the encroachment precisely and note on the plat.

### Cultivation and maintenance

A lawn mowed up to a certain point, a garden maintained across the deed line, or a planting bed that extends onto the neighbor's land by deed.

### Vegetation changes

A distinct change in vegetation (maintained lawn vs unmaintained brush) along a line that does not match the deed boundary.

### Use patterns

Worn paths, parking areas, or storage areas that extend across the deed line.

## The surveyor's role

The surveyor is a fact-finder, not a judge. When evidence of potential adverse possession is found:

1. **Measure and document.** Show the occupation line (fence, edge of maintenance, etc.) on the survey plat with its distance from the deed boundary.
2. **Describe in the report.** Note the evidence and its apparent age and condition. Do not opine on whether adverse possession has been established — that requires a legal analysis the surveyor is not qualified to make.
3. **Inform the client.** Explain that the occupation does not match the deed and recommend consulting an attorney if the discrepancy is significant.
4. **Show the deed boundary.** The survey must still show the boundary as determined from the deeds and evidence under the hierarchy of evidence. The occupation line is additional information, not a replacement for the deed boundary (unless a court has already adjudicated adverse possession).

Surveyors should avoid language on the plat that implies a legal conclusion about adverse possession. Appropriate language: "Fence located 2.3 ft south of deed line. See report." Inappropriate language: "Boundary by adverse possession" (unless a court order so provides).

## Adverse possession vs prescriptive easement

| Element | Adverse possession | Prescriptive easement |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Result | Transfer of title | Right to use (no title transfer) |
| Exclusivity | Required | Not required — shared use is permissible |
| Type of use | Possession as owner | Use of a specific type (access, drainage, etc.) |
| Statutory period | 10 years (IC 32-21-7) | 10 years (IC 32-23-1) |
| Recording | Quiet-title action required | Court adjudication required |
| Effect on survey | Boundary may change | Easement added, boundary unchanged |

The distinction matters for the surveyor because adverse possession changes the boundary line (once adjudicated), while a prescriptive easement does not.
