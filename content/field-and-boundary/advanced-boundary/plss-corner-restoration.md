> **TL;DR**
> 1. The critical distinction is between **obliterated** and **lost** corners. An obliterated corner's position can be recovered from evidence (witness trees, bearing trees, accessories, testimony, measurements from nearby corners). A lost corner has no recoverable evidence and must be restored by proportioning.
> 2. Single proportioning places a lost corner on a straight line between the nearest existing corners on the same line, distributing the excess or deficiency proportionally. Double proportioning places a lost corner at the intersection of two single-proportion lines (one north-south, one east-west).
> 3. Indiana county surveyors are statutory custodians of PLSS corner records under IC 36-2-12-11. Before restoring any corner, check the county surveyor's corner perpetuation file — the corner may already be documented.
> 4. Once a corner is restored, it must be monumented and filed with the county surveyor. A corner properly restored and filed becomes the legal corner.

## The PLSS in Indiana

Indiana was surveyed under the Public Land Survey System beginning in 1799 in the southeastern part of the state (the "Gore") and continuing through the 1830s for the northern counties. Most of Indiana falls under the Second Principal Meridian. The original surveys established section corners, quarter-section corners, and meander corners for navigable waters.

The original monuments were typically wooden posts (2 in. to 4 in. square) with witness trees (bearing trees) blazed and marked with the section, township, and range. After two centuries, few original wooden monuments survive. Many corners have been perpetuated by subsequent surveys — replaced with iron pins, pipes, or stone monuments — but some remain undocumented or lost.

## Obliterated vs lost corners

This is the most important distinction in corner restoration, and getting it wrong invalidates the restoration.

### Obliterated corner

A corner whose physical monument has been destroyed or removed, but whose **position can be determined beyond reasonable doubt** from other evidence. The position is recovered, not recalculated. Evidence that can recover an obliterated corner:

- **Witness (bearing) trees.** If the GLO notes record bearing trees and those trees (or their stumps) can be identified, the corner position can be recovered by intersection of the recorded bearing and distance from each witness.
- **Accessories.** Pits, mounds, charcoal deposits, or other marks described in the GLO notes.
- **Reference monuments.** Subsequent surveyors who tied the corner to reference points. Check the county surveyor's corner records.
- **Testimony.** A landowner or prior surveyor who can testify to the corner's position from personal knowledge (rare and diminishing in value with age).
- **Fences and occupation.** Long-standing fence intersections or other improvements that align with the corner position from multiple independent sources.

If any of these evidence types can establish the position beyond reasonable doubt, the corner is obliterated (not lost) and the recovered position controls — even if the recovery method is less precise than proportioning.

### Lost corner

A corner whose position **cannot be determined beyond reasonable doubt** from any evidence. All physical evidence and reliable testimony are gone. A lost corner can only be restored by proportioning, following the BLM Manual of Surveying Instructions.

A corner is not lost merely because the monument is missing. It is lost only when no evidence of any kind can establish its position. The surveyor must exhaust all avenues of evidence recovery before concluding that a corner is lost.

## Proportioning methods

### Single proportioning

Used to restore a lost corner that lies on a surveyed line between two existing (found or properly restored) corners. The procedure:

1. Identify the nearest existing corners on the same line, one on each side of the lost corner.
2. Measure the total distance between the existing corners.
3. Compare this distance to the sum of the original (GLO record) distances for the same line segments.
4. Distribute the excess or deficiency proportionally among all the record intervals.

**Example:** A lost corner on the section line between Section 5 and Section 8 is being restored. The nearest existing corners are the NW corner of Section 8 (1,320 ft south by record) and the NW corner of Section 5 (1,320 ft north by record). The measured distance between the two existing corners is 2,645 ft. The record total is 2,640 ft. The excess is 5 ft. The lost corner is placed at 1,320/2,640 x 2,645 = 1,322.5 ft from the NW corner of Section 8 (or equivalently, 1,322.5 ft from the NW corner of Section 5). The 5 ft excess is distributed equally because the two record intervals are equal.

Single proportioning also distributes the bearing (direction) proportionally if the existing corners do not lie on the record bearing.

### Double proportioning

Used to restore a lost corner that lies at the intersection of two surveyed lines — typically a township corner or a standard corner. The procedure:

1. Restore the corner's position on the north-south line by single proportioning between the nearest existing corners north and south.
2. Restore the corner's position on the east-west line by single proportioning between the nearest existing corners east and west.
3. The restored position is the intersection of the two single-proportion lines. If the two results differ (they usually do by a small amount because of independent measurement errors), the final position is at the intersection point — not the average of two points.

Double proportioning is used for township corners, range line corners, and any corner that controls lines in two directions. Section corners in the interior of a township are restored by single proportioning along the section line on which they lie, not by double proportioning (because they control only one line).

### Quarter-corner restoration

The standard rule for a lost quarter-section corner is:

- **Interior quarter corners (on north-south or east-west section lines):** Restore by single proportioning between the two section corners on the same line.
- **Quarter corners on the north and west boundaries of a township:** These were set by the original surveyor at the midpoint of the section line (by record), but the closing distances to the north and west township boundaries may be irregular. Restore by single proportioning between the section corner and the closing corner.

The center quarter corner (center of section) was not set by the original GLO surveyor. It is established at the intersection of the lines connecting opposite quarter corners on the section boundaries. This is a computed point, not a proportioned one.

## Indiana county surveyor's role

IC 36-2-12-10 and IC 36-2-12-11 assign the county surveyor specific duties related to PLSS corners:

- **Corner perpetuation (IC 36-2-12-11).** The county surveyor maintains a corner perpetuation file — a record of corner positions, descriptions, and reference ties submitted by licensed surveyors. Before restoring any corner, the surveyor should check this file.
- **Corner records.** When a licensed surveyor finds or restores a PLSS corner, the surveyor should file a corner record with the county surveyor. The record should include the corner's position (coordinates), description of the monument found or set, reference ties, basis of bearings, and the evidence supporting the position.
- **County surveyor review.** In some Indiana counties, the county surveyor reviews corner restorations and maintains an index of established corners. This review provides a layer of quality control and helps prevent conflicting restorations by different surveyors.

## Procedure for corner restoration

1. **Research.** Obtain GLO field notes and plats for the relevant township. Review the county surveyor's corner perpetuation file. Collect all prior surveys in the area. Identify the nearest existing corners.

2. **Fieldwork.** Search the corner location for physical evidence: old monuments, accessories, witness trees, fence-line intersections. Search a broad area — the original monument may have been displaced or the original survey may have been imprecise. Use a metal detector for iron monuments.

3. **Classify.** Based on the evidence found, classify the corner as existent (monument found in place), obliterated (position recoverable from evidence), or lost (no recoverable evidence).

4. **Recover or restore.** If obliterated, recover the position from the evidence found. If lost, apply the appropriate proportioning method. Document every step.

5. **Monument.** Set a durable monument at the restored corner. Use a minimum 1/2 in. diameter iron rod or pipe, minimum 24 in. long, with a cap bearing the surveyor's license number (per 865 IAC 1-12-10).

6. **Set reference monuments.** Set at least two reference monuments tied to the corner, so that the corner can be recovered if the primary monument is disturbed.

7. **File.** File a corner record with the county surveyor's office. Include coordinates, monument description, reference ties, basis of bearings, GLO record data, proportioning calculations (if applicable), and a narrative explaining the classification and restoration methodology.

## Common errors

- **Proportioning before exhausting evidence.** Declaring a corner lost without adequate search. A corner is lost only when all evidence is gone — not when the monument is merely missing.
- **Using the wrong proportioning method.** Single proportioning for a corner that requires double proportioning, or vice versa.
- **Holding a found monument without verifying it.** A monument found at the approximate location is not necessarily the original or a proper restoration. Verify it against the GLO record, witness trees, and prior surveys.
- **Failing to file.** Restoring a corner and not filing a record with the county surveyor. The next surveyor will not know the corner has been restored and may repeat the work — or reach a different result.
- **Ignoring the county surveyor's file.** Restoring a corner that has already been properly restored and filed, then setting a different monument. This creates a pincushion and undermines the public record.
