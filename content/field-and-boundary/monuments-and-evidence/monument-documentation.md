> **TL;DR**
> 1. Every monument found or set must be documented with photographs (top, side, context), measured position (coordinates and/or ties), condition notes, and identification (cap stamping, material, size).
> 2. Field notes should include a sketch showing the monument's relationship to nearby features, along with the surveyor's opinion on whether it is in its original position.
> 3. Thorough documentation protects the surveyor professionally and preserves evidence for future retracements — what you record today may be the only surviving evidence in 50 years.

## Photograph protocol

Photographs are the most efficient way to preserve monument evidence. For each monument found or set, capture:

### Top view

Looking straight down at the monument. This shows:
- Cap stamping (name, license number, firm).
- Cross-cut or punch mark on brass disk.
- Condition of the cap or disk surface.
- Material and diameter.

Place a scale reference (ruler, tape measure, or coin) next to the monument for size reference.

### Side view

From ground level, showing:
- How much of the monument is exposed above grade.
- Tilt angle (if any).
- Condition of the shaft (corrosion, bending, breaks).
- Surrounding soil condition (disturbed, compacted, root-bound).

### Context view

From 5 to 10 ft away, showing:
- The monument's relationship to nearby features (fence, sidewalk, building, tree, utility).
- General site conditions.
- Enough background to help a future surveyor relocate the spot.

Include a north arrow or compass direction in context photos when practical. Geotagged photos from a phone or tablet provide useful metadata.

## Position documentation

Record the monument's position in at least one of these forms:

- **Coordinates** — state plane or project coordinates, with the coordinate system and datum identified. This is the modern standard.
- **Ties** — measured distances and directions to at least two permanent features (building corners, utility poles, fence posts). Traditional method, still valuable as a backup.
- **Offset from a traverse point** — if the monument cannot be occupied directly, record the angle and distance from a nearby traverse point.

For PLSS section corners, reference ties to at least four accessory objects (reference monuments, fence corners, utility poles, trees) are standard practice in Indiana county surveyor corner records.

## Condition notes

Record the following for every monument:

| Field | What to note |
|---|---|
| Material | Iron pipe, rebar, concrete, stone, railroad spike, PK nail, mag nail, axle, other |
| Size | Diameter and length (or cross-section for concrete). Measure with calipers or tape. |
| Cap/disk | Present or absent. If present: stamping text, material (plastic, aluminum, brass), condition |
| Verticality | Plumb, slightly tilted, heavily tilted. Estimate the tilt angle and direction. |
| Depth | How far above or below grade. "Flush," "2 in. above grade," "4 in. below grade" |
| Corrosion | None, light surface rust, heavy corrosion, disintegrating |
| Surrounding soil | Undisturbed, recently excavated, fill material, pavement, root-bound |
| Opinion | Surveyor's assessment: "appears undisturbed," "suspected disturbed — see narrative," "newly set" |

## Field note sketch

A sketch in the field notes should show:

- The monument's position relative to nearby features (dimensions included).
- North arrow.
- Nearby property lines or fences.
- Other monuments in the vicinity.
- Reference ties if measured.
- Any relevant context (utility trench, grading cut, fence offset).

The sketch does not need to be to scale but should be proportional and clearly labeled. Digital field-note systems (Trimble Access, Carlson SurvCE) support photo-linked point records; these supplement but do not replace a written description.

## What to record for monuments set

When the surveyor sets a new monument, document:

- Monument type, material, size, and cap stamping.
- Depth set (typically 18" to 30" for rebar in Indiana; deeper where frost heave is a concern).
- Coordinates.
- Why the monument was set at this position (references to found evidence, record dimensions, proportioning result).
- Photo of the set monument.

## Preserving records

865 IAC 1-12 requires Indiana licensed surveyors to maintain survey records for the duration of their practice. Digital photographs should be archived with the project file, backed up, and organized by point number or monument identifier. Field notes — whether paper or digital — are part of the permanent record.

When a surveyor retires, the records must be transferred to a successor, donated to the county surveyor, or otherwise preserved. Lost field records are irreplaceable.
