> **TL;DR**
> 1. Indiana PLSS section corners were originally set by GLO deputy surveyors in the early 1800s. Original monuments (stones, posts, mounds) rarely survive; what you find today is usually a county-surveyor re-monument.
> 2. IC 36-2-12 charges the county surveyor with perpetuating section corners. Re-monuments are typically concrete with a brass disk or rebar with a cap referencing the county surveyor.
> 3. When the original monument is lost (no physical evidence or reliable testimony), the corner must be restored by proportionate measurement per the BLM Manual of Survey Instructions, not by simple record distance.

## Original GLO monuments

The General Land Office (GLO) surveys of Indiana ran from roughly 1799 to 1840, depending on the treaty and purchase area. Original monuments included:

- **Wooden posts** — typically 3" to 4" square, with notches indicating township, range, and section. Almost none survive.
- **Stones** — where available, stones were set at corners with witness marks. Some survive in southern Indiana's hilly terrain.
- **Mound and pit** — at township and section corners in prairie or flat ground, the deputy surveyor built an earth mound adjacent to a pit. These are obliterated by agriculture.
- **Witness (bearing) trees** — one to four trees were marked with blaze and scribe near each corner, with bearing and distance recorded. Witness trees are occasionally found as scarred stumps or mature trees, but most are long gone.

The GLO field notes and plats are the primary record of what was set, where, and what witness objects were recorded. Indiana GLO records are available digitally through the BLM General Land Office Records website.

## County surveyor re-monuments

Under IC 36-2-12, the county surveyor is responsible for the perpetuation of section corners and quarter-section corners. In practice this means:

- **Re-monumentation**: setting a durable monument (concrete with brass disk, or rebar with cap) at the position of a corner after conducting a corner perpetuation survey.
- **Corner record**: filing a record of the monument set, coordinates, references, and the basis for the position with the county surveyor's office and (in some counties) the county recorder.
- **Corner cards / corner records**: many Indiana counties maintain a system of corner records (sometimes called corner cards) that document the monument type, position, reference ties, and the surveyor who perpetuated it.

Not all counties have active perpetuation programs. Some counties have perpetuated nearly every section corner; others have significant gaps. The surveyor should check with the county surveyor's office before beginning a section-corner search.

## Identifying corner evidence in the field

When searching for a section corner, the surveyor should:

1. **Research first.** Obtain the GLO plat and field notes, any existing corner records from the county surveyor, and recent surveys in the area that may have found or referenced the corner.
2. **Search the record position.** Use coordinates from corner records or proportioned position from found adjacent corners.
3. **Look for physical evidence.** This includes the monument itself, remnants of an older monument (pipe stub, stone fragments), witness tree scars, fence-line intersections that predate modern surveys, and any depression or mound pattern.
4. **Use a metal detector.** Most re-monuments are ferrous or have ferrous components. A systematic search pattern around the record position is standard practice.
5. **Interview landowners and neighbors.** They may know where a corner marker was seen before construction or cultivation disturbed it.

## Corner status classification

The BLM Manual of Survey Instructions classifies corners as:

- **Existent** — the original monument or an acceptable replacement is found in its original, undisturbed position, or its position can be established beyond reasonable doubt from accessories (witness trees, reference monuments, etc.).
- **Obliterated** — the physical monument is gone, but the position can be established from reliable evidence (witness trees, reference ties in corner records, fences, etc.).
- **Lost** — no physical monument and no reliable evidence of its position. A lost corner must be restored by proportionate measurement between the nearest found corners on the same line, per the BLM Manual.

The distinction between obliterated and lost is critical. An obliterated corner is re-established at the position indicated by evidence. A lost corner is restored by proportionate measurement, which may place it at a different position than simple record distance would indicate.

## Proportionate measurement

When a section corner is lost, the BLM Manual prescribes proportionate measurement: the missing corner is placed proportionally between the nearest found corners on the same original survey line, distributing the excess or deficiency in proportion to the original recorded distances.

For example, if original record distances place corners A, B (lost), and C along a section line at 40.00 ch, 40.00 ch, and the measured distance from A to C is 79.80 ch, corner B is placed at 39.90 ch from A (proportional share of the shortfall).

Single and double proportioning are used depending on whether the corner lies on one survey line (quarter-section corners) or at the intersection of two lines (section corners). The BLM Manual provides detailed procedures for each case.

## Indiana-specific notes

- IC 36-2-12-10 requires that when a licensed surveyor finds a section corner or quarter corner, and the corner has not been previously perpetuated, the surveyor must notify the county surveyor.
- IC 36-2-12-12 authorizes the county surveyor to establish corners at locations determined by survey and to file a corner record.
- The county surveyor's position on a disputed corner carries significant weight in Indiana courts, though it is not conclusive. A licensed surveyor who disagrees must present their own evidence and analysis.
