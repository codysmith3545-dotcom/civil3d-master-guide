> **TL;DR**
> 1. Courts rank boundary evidence in a fixed hierarchy: **natural monuments > artificial monuments > record bearing and distance > adjoiners > area/quantity**. Higher-ranking evidence controls when elements conflict.
> 2. The hierarchy exists because more permanent and less easily manipulated evidence is considered more reliable — a stone in the ground is harder to move than a number on a deed.
> 3. The surveyor's job in retracement is to weigh all available evidence against this hierarchy and reconcile conflicts, not simply to re-measure record distances.

## The hierarchy

The hierarchy of evidence applies in boundary retracement (resurveys) where the task is to locate an existing boundary, not create a new one. The ranking, from most controlling to least:

### 1. Natural monuments

Natural monuments are geographic features that existed at the time the boundary was created: rivers, lakes, ridgelines, marked trees (witness trees or line trees), rock outcrops. They are ranked highest because they are the most permanent and the least subject to human error in description. A deed that calls for "the bank of White River" controls over a distance that disagrees.

Natural monuments can move. Rivers migrate, lakes fill. The legal question then becomes whether the movement was gradual (accretion/reliction, and the boundary moves with it) or sudden (avulsion, and the boundary stays at the former location).

### 2. Artificial monuments

Artificial monuments are objects placed by humans to mark boundaries: iron pipes, rebar with caps, concrete monuments, stone monuments, wooden stakes (rarely surviving), railroad spikes, PK nails, and fence corners. They rank second because they are placed to mark the specific boundary, but they can be disturbed.

A found artificial monument controls over record distances. If a section corner monument is found 0.3 ft from its record position, the monument holds. The surveyor must evaluate whether the monument is in its original, undisturbed position — see [disturbed monuments](disturbed-monuments.md).

### 3. Record bearing and distance

The bearing and distance stated in the deed or plat rank third. They are the surveyor's primary tool when monuments are missing. Record distances are subject to the measurement technology of the day — a 19th-century chain measurement carries different precision than a modern total station measurement. Where record distances conflict with found monuments, the monuments control.

Distances generally control over bearings when the two conflict (bearings are more susceptible to compass error), but this is not absolute.

### 4. Adjoiners (record of adjoining tracts)

The calls to adjoining landowners or tracts in a deed can help locate a boundary when monuments and distances fail. "Thence along the line of Smith's land" establishes that the boundary coincides with Smith's line. If Smith's line can be located, the boundary follows.

### 5. Area or quantity

Area is the weakest evidence. A deed states "containing 40 acres, more or less" — the "more or less" is the clearest signal that area is approximate. Area yields to every other element above it. Surveyors should never adjust a boundary to fit a deed's stated area when doing so contradicts monuments or distances.

## Practical application

The hierarchy is not a mechanical rule. Courts consider the **intent of the parties** at the time the boundary was created. All evidence is weighed together, and the hierarchy guides the weight given to each element. In practice:

- When a found monument contradicts the record distance by a small amount, the monument controls.
- When a found monument contradicts the record distance by a large amount and there is evidence of disturbance, the surveyor may set the monument aside and rely on distance.
- When there is no monument and no reliable distance, adjoiners and proportioning (in PLSS sections) become the primary tools.

## Indiana considerations

Indiana follows the general common-law hierarchy. The state's PLSS framework adds the federal Manual of Survey Instructions (BLM) as the controlling authority for restoring lost or obliterated section corners through proportionate measurement. IC 36-2-12 governs county surveyor responsibilities for corner perpetuation.

Indiana case law consistently holds that senior (earlier) conveyances control over junior conveyances, and that long-acquiesced boundaries (practical location over a long period) can override record calls. The surveyor must research both the chain of title and any evidence of practical location.
