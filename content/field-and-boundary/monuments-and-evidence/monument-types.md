> **TL;DR**
> 1. The most common monuments in Indiana surveys are **rebar with plastic caps**, **iron pipes**, and **concrete monuments**. Each has a typical context, age range, and condition profile.
> 2. Monument identification matters: material, diameter, length, cap stamping (if any), and condition all go into the field record and affect evidentiary weight.
> 3. Older monuments (stone, axles, railroad spikes) may lack precision in placement but carry high legal weight as original evidence.

## Iron pipes

Iron pipes (also called "iron pins" in older deeds) are the most commonly found older monument in Indiana. Typical diameters are 1/2", 3/4", and 1" (outside diameter). Lengths range from 18" to 36". Pipes corrode over time; a heavily rusted pipe may be barely recognizable. The interior is hollow, which distinguishes a pipe from solid rebar.

Iron pipes without caps were standard practice through the mid-20th century. A pipe found without identification is still a monument, but its evidentiary value depends on whether its position is consistent with the record and whether it can be attributed to a particular survey.

## Rebar with caps

Rebar (reinforcing bar) with a stamped plastic or aluminum cap is the modern standard. Indiana licensed surveyors are required to set monuments stamped with their name or registration number per 865 IAC 1-12-12. Common sizes are #4 (1/2" diameter) and #5 (5/8" diameter), typically 18" to 30" long.

Cap information to record: surveyor's name, registration number, firm name if present, and any project or date stamping. The cap is the identifying element — rebar without a cap has reduced evidentiary weight because it cannot be attributed to a specific survey.

## Concrete monuments

Concrete monuments are typically 4"x4" or 6"x6" cross-section, 24" to 36" long, with a brass or aluminum disk set in the top. They are used for subdivision control corners, section corners, and other positions intended to be permanent. County surveyors in Indiana set concrete monuments at PLSS section corners as part of perpetuation programs under IC 36-2-12.

Concrete monuments are heavy and difficult to disturb accidentally, which increases their evidentiary reliability. Look for a stamped disk, a cross-cut or punch mark in the disk, and condition of the concrete (cracking, spalling, frost heave).

## Stone monuments

Stone monuments are among the oldest found in Indiana, dating to original GLO surveys and early private surveys. They may be undressed fieldstone, quarried limestone, or sandstone. Identification is by position, size, and sometimes a notch or drill hole. Stone monuments carry high legal weight as potential original evidence but can be difficult to distinguish from naturally occurring rock.

## Railroad spikes

Railroad spikes are commonly used as monuments in paved areas — road intersections, curb lines, and sidewalks — where driving rebar is impractical. They are typically 6" long with a wide head. Railroad spikes are easily disturbed by road maintenance and have limited longevity in high-traffic areas. Record the spike's position relative to permanent features.

## PK nails

PK nails (Parker-Kalon nails, sometimes called "survey tacks") are hardened steel nails with a cupped head, driven into pavement, concrete, or wood. They are used to mark traverse points, reference points, and sometimes property corners in paved areas. PK nails are small, easily overlooked, and easily destroyed by resurfacing. Typical life in a traveled road surface is one to three years.

## Axles

Axles (wagon axles, truck axles) are found as monuments in rural Indiana, particularly at section corners. Setting an axle was a practical choice: a heavy, durable piece of steel driven deep into the ground. An axle at a section corner may date to the 19th century. They are typically found with a metal detector and are identifiable by their cylindrical shape, diameter (1" to 2"), and length (often 3' to 5' driven vertically).

## Mag nails

Mag nails (magnetic nails) are case-hardened steel nails designed to be relocated with a magnetic locator. They are commonly set as control points and reference marks in paved areas. They are not typically used as property corner monuments but may be set as reference points for corners that cannot be set in their true position.

## Age and condition indicators

| Monument type | Typical era | Expected condition after 50+ years |
|---|---|---|
| Iron pipe, no cap | Pre-1970 | Heavy corrosion, may be bent or broken at grade |
| Rebar with cap | 1970s–present | Cap may be damaged; rebar corrodes less than pipe |
| Concrete monument | Variable | Cracking, spalling; disk may be loose or missing |
| Stone | Pre-1900 | May be buried, shifted by frost, or unrecognizable |
| Railroad spike | Variable | Often destroyed by road work |
| PK nail | Variable | Rarely survives more than a few years in pavement |
| Axle | Pre-1950 | Deeply set; found with metal detector, good survival |
