> **TL;DR**
> 1. A disturbed monument has been moved from its original position by construction, agriculture, frost heave, erosion, or deliberate tampering. It no longer marks the true corner and must not be relied upon without analysis.
> 2. Field indicators include tilted rebar, a monument inconsistent with record distances and adjoiners, fresh soil disturbance, utility excavation scars, and a position that conflicts with other controlling evidence.
> 3. Re-establishment of the corner requires assembling independent evidence (reference ties, adjoining surveys, record distances, accessories) and documenting the reasoning in the survey narrative.

## How monuments get disturbed

Monuments are disturbed more often than many practitioners expect. Common causes:

- **Construction activity** — grading, trenching, and foundation excavation displace or destroy corners. Contractors sometimes pull corners out of the ground and attempt to replace them, often in the wrong position.
- **Agricultural operations** — deep plowing, drain tile installation, and land clearing push monuments laterally or pull them to the surface.
- **Frost heave** — in Indiana's freeze-thaw climate, monuments set at shallow depth can migrate upward and laterally over decades. Rebar shorter than 24" is particularly susceptible.
- **Erosion and flooding** — bank erosion along streams and drainage ditches can undermine or remove monuments.
- **Road construction and maintenance** — road widening, ditch regrading, and utility installation in the right-of-way destroy monuments near road frontages.
- **Deliberate tampering** — rare but documented. A landowner may pull or move a monument to support a claim. This is a criminal offense in Indiana under IC 35-43-1-3 (criminal mischief involving a survey monument).

## Field indicators of disturbance

The surveyor should evaluate every found monument for possible disturbance. Warning signs:

| Indicator | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| Monument is tilted more than a few degrees from vertical | Lateral force has been applied (equipment, frost) |
| Monument is bent or broken at or below grade | Equipment strike (backhoe, plow) |
| Monument is at a different depth than expected for its type | Pulled up and reset, or pushed down |
| Cap is missing from rebar that should have one | Cap was sheared off by equipment |
| Fresh concrete or disturbed soil around monument | Recently reset, possibly by non-surveyor |
| Position conflicts with record distances by more than measurement tolerance | Monument may have moved, or the record is in error |
| Position conflicts with multiple adjoining found monuments | Strongest indicator of disturbance |
| Monument is in a utility trench backfill zone | Displaced during trenching and tossed back in |

No single indicator is conclusive. The surveyor must weigh all evidence together.

## Evaluating a suspected disturbed monument

When a monument appears disturbed, the surveyor should:

1. **Document the as-found position.** Measure and record coordinates, photos from multiple angles, condition notes, and context (nearby utilities, construction, grading).
2. **Compare to independent evidence.** Check the monument's position against record distances from found undisturbed corners, adjoining surveys, plat dimensions, and reference ties from corner records.
3. **Assess the magnitude and direction of discrepancy.** A 0.05 ft discrepancy on a 200 ft line is within measurement tolerance. A 2.0 ft discrepancy is not. The direction of offset may indicate the mechanism (lateral push from a known construction activity, for example).
4. **Search for accessories.** Reference monuments, witness corners, and reference ties from the original survey or a corner record may survive even when the primary monument is disturbed.
5. **Decide.** The surveyor must reach a professional opinion: is this monument in its original position, or is it disturbed? There is no formula for this decision. It is a professional judgment based on the totality of the evidence, documented in the survey narrative.

## Re-establishment procedures

If the surveyor concludes the monument is disturbed and the true corner position can be determined from other evidence:

1. **Establish the position** from the best available evidence (found undisturbed monuments, record distances, reference ties, proportionate measurement if a PLSS corner).
2. **Set a new monument** at the re-established position, with the surveyor's cap and identification per 865 IAC 1-12-12.
3. **Document the re-establishment** in the field notes and survey narrative: what was found, why it was rejected, what evidence was used to re-establish the position, and the surveyor's reasoning.
4. **Note the disturbed monument** on the plat or survey drawing. Show its found position and the re-established position. Typical notation: "Found 5/8" rebar with cap LS 12345, tilted, 1.3' NE of established position — disturbed, not held."

## Notification to county surveyor

For PLSS section corners and quarter corners, IC 36-2-12 requires that the surveyor notify the county surveyor when a perpetuated corner is found disturbed. The county surveyor has authority to conduct a corner perpetuation survey to re-establish and re-monument the corner. Private surveyors should coordinate with the county surveyor before setting a new monument at a section corner.

For private property corners, there is no statutory notification requirement, but the surveyor should note the disturbance in the survey record for future practitioners.
