> **TL;DR**
> 1. Indiana law (IC 36-2-12-14) requires a Surveyor's Report for original surveys and may require one for retracements depending on county practice. The report is filed with the county surveyor and/or county recorder.
> 2. The report must include monuments found and set, basis of bearings, methodology and equipment, a narrative explaining the surveyor's analysis, and a description of the boundary established.
> 3. The narrative is the most important part — it preserves the surveyor's reasoning so that future surveyors and courts can understand why the boundary was placed where it was.

## When a Surveyor's Report is required

IC 36-2-12-14 requires that when a licensed surveyor performs an original survey (establishing a new boundary, such as a subdivision), the surveyor shall file a report with the county surveyor. The statute also contemplates reports for retracement surveys.

In practice, filing requirements vary by county. Some county surveyors require a report for every boundary survey; others only for original surveys or when section corners are involved. The surveyor should check with the county surveyor's office for local requirements before beginning work.

865 IAC 1-12 (Standards of Practice) requires the surveyor to maintain a complete survey record regardless of whether a formal report is filed. The record must be sufficient for another competent surveyor to understand and retrace the work.

## Contents of the Surveyor's Report

A complete report includes:

### Identification

- Surveyor's name, license number, firm name, and contact information.
- Client name and purpose of the survey.
- Date of fieldwork and date of report.
- Legal description of the property surveyed (section, township, range, lot/block if platted).

### Monuments found

- Type, size, material, cap stamping, and condition of every monument found.
- Position (coordinates and/or ties to other points).
- Opinion on whether the monument is in its original, undisturbed position.
- If a monument was found disturbed, the evidence and reasoning for that conclusion.

### Monuments set

- Type, size, material, and cap stamping of every monument set.
- Position (coordinates).
- Reason for setting (new corner, replacement of disturbed monument, reference monument).

### Basis of bearings

- The reference system used (e.g., NAD83(2011) Indiana East Zone, or bearing between two found monuments).
- How the basis was established (GNSS observation, published control, record bearing held).

### Methodology and equipment

- Survey method (GNSS, total station traverse, combination).
- Equipment make and model.
- Adjustment method and results if a network was adjusted.
- Combined scale factor and whether coordinates are grid or ground.

### Narrative

The narrative is the core of the report. It explains the surveyor's analysis and decision-making:

- What research was conducted (deed research, plat review, prior surveys, county surveyor corner records, GLO records).
- What evidence was found in the field and how it relates to the record.
- Where conflicts exist between elements of evidence and how they were resolved, referencing the hierarchy of evidence.
- Why each boundary line was placed where it was.
- Any unresolved questions, gaps in evidence, or potential disputes noted.

The narrative must be detailed enough that a future surveyor reading only the report can understand the basis for the boundary without re-doing the research.

### Plat or map

The report typically includes or references a survey plat showing the boundary, monuments, dimensions, bearings, areas, and relevant features.

## Filing

- File with the county surveyor's office. Some counties accept digital submissions; others require paper originals.
- Some counties also require filing with the county recorder, particularly for original surveys and subdivision plats.
- Retain a copy in the surveyor's own records. 865 IAC 1-12 requires retention for the duration of the surveyor's career and transfer to a successor upon retirement or death.

## Common deficiencies

County surveyors and reviewing surveyors frequently note these problems in submitted reports:

- **No narrative.** A plat with dimensions is not a report. The reasoning must be documented.
- **Basis of bearings not stated.** Without this, future surveyors cannot reproduce the bearing system.
- **Monuments found but not described.** Every found monument must be documented, even those not held.
- **No mention of conflicting evidence.** If conflicts existed, the report must explain how they were resolved.
- **Missing coordinates.** Modern practice expects coordinates on all monuments set and found.
