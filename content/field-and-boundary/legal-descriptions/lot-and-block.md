> **TL;DR**
> 1. A lot-and-block description identifies a parcel by reference to a **recorded subdivision plat**: lot number, block number (if used), subdivision name, recording reference (book/page or instrument number), county, and state.
> 2. Lot-and-block is sufficient when the parcel matches the platted lot exactly. When the parcel is part of a lot, a combination of lots, or a lot plus an abutting unplatted strip, a **metes-and-bounds** description is needed.
> 3. The plat is the controlling document. If the recorded plat dimensions and a metes-and-bounds description of the same lot conflict, the plat generally controls.

## Format

A lot-and-block description is short because the work of describing the boundary was done on the plat:

> Lot 14 in Block 3 of Meadowbrook Estates, Section Two, a subdivision in Hamilton County, Indiana, as per plat thereof recorded in Plat Book 12, Page 45 (or Instrument No. 2019-012345) in the Office of the Recorder of Hamilton County, Indiana.

The essential elements are:

1. **Lot number** (and block number, if the subdivision uses blocks).
2. **Subdivision name**, including the section or phase if the subdivision was platted in phases.
3. **County and state.**
4. **Recording reference.** Older plats use plat-book and page; newer plats use an instrument number. Include whichever reference the recorder assigned. If both exist, citing both is acceptable.
5. **Recorder's office.** "In the Office of the Recorder of [County], Indiana" establishes where to find the plat.

## When lot-and-block is sufficient

Lot-and-block is the correct form when:

- The parcel being conveyed is an **entire platted lot** (or a combination of entire lots in the same subdivision).
- The lot has not been replatted, vacated, or partially conveyed since the plat was recorded.
- No metes-and-bounds exception is needed to carve out an easement, a right-of-way taking, or a partial conveyance.

If any of the above conditions fails, the description must include metes-and-bounds language. Common situations requiring hybrid or full metes-and-bounds descriptions:

- **Part of a lot.** "The North 50.00 feet of Lot 7 in..." requires metes-and-bounds courses to identify which 50.00 feet.
- **Lot plus vacated alley or street.** If a public way has been vacated and the abutting lot owner takes the vacated strip by operation of law, the description must reference the vacation ordinance and describe the combined parcel.
- **Replat.** When a replat supersedes the original plat, cite the replat, not the original. If the replat covers only some lots, identify whether the subject lot is on the original or the replat.

## The plat as controlling document

The recorded plat is a conveyance document. Its dimensions, bearings, curve data, and area are part of the public record. If a subsequent deed restates the plat dimensions and gets them wrong, the plat controls (absent an express intent to convey different boundaries).

Practical consequences:

- A surveyor retracing a platted lot measures the lot per the plat, not per a later deed that may contain typographical errors.
- Monuments set on the ground at the time of platting control over the dimensions shown on the plat. If the recorded plat says 100.00 feet between two corners but the original monuments are 100.14 feet apart, the monuments control.
- Block corners and lot corners set by the original platting surveyor are original monuments; they hold the same weight in evidence as section corners hold in the PLSS.

## Replats

A replat is a new plat that supersedes all or part of an earlier plat. Indiana statutes and local subdivision-control ordinances govern the replat process, which typically requires:

- Consent of affected lot owners.
- Approval by the plan commission.
- Recording of the new plat in the recorder's office with a reference to the original plat being superseded.

After a replat is recorded, new conveyances should reference the replat. Older descriptions referencing the original plat remain valid for chain-of-title purposes but may cause confusion if the lot configuration changed.

## Common pitfalls

- **Wrong recording reference.** Transposing plat-book and page numbers renders the description ambiguous. Always verify against the recorder's index.
- **Citing a superseded plat.** If a replat has been recorded, cite the replat. Citing the original plat may describe a lot that no longer exists in its original configuration.
- **Omitting the phase or section.** Large subdivisions platted in phases often reuse lot numbers across phases. "Lot 5, Meadowbrook Estates" is ambiguous if there is a Lot 5 in both Section One and Section Two.
- **Assuming lot-and-block is sufficient for a partial lot.** A conveyance of "the East half of Lot 12" without metes-and-bounds courses leaves the dividing line undefined and creates a title problem.
