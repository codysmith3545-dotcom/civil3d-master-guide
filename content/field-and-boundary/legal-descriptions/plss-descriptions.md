> **TL;DR**
> 1. The Public Land Survey System (PLSS) divides land into 6-mile-square townships, then 1-mile-square sections, then aliquot parts (halves, quarters, quarter-quarters).
> 2. Indiana was surveyed under the U.S. Public Lands Survey starting in 1815 from the Second Principal Meridian (and the First Principal Meridian along the southwest). Most descriptions in Indiana commence at a section corner.
> 3. A nominal section is 640 acres; a quarter-quarter ("forty") is 40 acres. **Government lots** were used where the regular subdivision could not produce a 40-acre aliquot — typically along navigable water, state lines, or where survey errors broke the grid.

## Townships, sections, and aliquot parts

A PLSS township is a 6-mile by 6-mile area identified by Township (north or south of a base line) and Range (east or west of a principal meridian). Each township contains 36 sections, numbered in a boustrophedonic (snake) pattern: section 1 in the northeast, running west to section 6, dropping south to section 7, running east to section 12, and so on, ending with section 36 in the southeast.

A regular section is one mile square, nominally 640 acres. Section subdivision walks down by halves and quarters:

- Half section: 320 acres (e.g., the North Half).
- Quarter section: 160 acres (e.g., the Northeast Quarter, abbreviated "NE 1/4").
- Quarter-quarter section: 40 acres (e.g., the Northeast Quarter of the Northeast Quarter, "NE 1/4 NE 1/4"). Locally called a "forty."
- Subdivisions below the quarter-quarter (sixteenths) are by half or quarter of the quarter-quarter.

A description like "the Southwest Quarter of the Northwest Quarter of Section 14, Township 16 North, Range 4 East" identifies a specific 40-acre aliquot.

## Indiana PLSS overview

Indiana was largely surveyed from the **Second Principal Meridian**, an 86°28' W meridian whose initial point is in southern Indiana, with the **base line** at approximately 38°28' N. Survey began in 1815 and ran for several decades; the original General Land Office (GLO) field notes and plats are in the BLM-GLO records system. A small area in the southwest of the state was surveyed from the First Principal Meridian.

For retracement, the GLO field notes describe the original chains (66-foot Gunter's chains) and witness trees set by the original deputy surveyors. Where original monuments are gone, the field notes and the BLM 2009 *Manual of Surveying Instructions* govern the proportionate-measurement methods used to re-establish lost corners. Counties under IC 36-2-12 maintain perpetuation records for section corners; the county surveyor is the statutory custodian.

## Section subdivision rules

When subdividing a section, the rules in the *Manual of Surveying Instructions* apply:

1. Section corners and quarter corners are controlling. They are not "calculated" — they are physical monuments set by the original survey or by re-establishment.
2. The center of a regular section is the intersection of the lines connecting opposite quarter corners (North-South line connecting the North quarter corner to the South quarter corner; East-West line connecting the East quarter corner to the West quarter corner). It is not the geometric center of the section.
3. Quarter-quarter (sixteenth) corners on the boundary of a section are set at the midpoint between the section corner and the quarter corner.
4. Interior sixteenth corners are set at the intersection of lines connecting opposite sixteenth corners on the section boundary.
5. Where a section is fractional or irregular, government lots are used in lieu of regular aliquots.

These rules are protected: a private surveyor cannot change them by clever measurement.

## Fractional sections and government lots

A section is fractional when the regular grid cannot be completed — most often because the section abuts a navigable river, a lake, a state boundary, or an earlier survey. The original deputy surveyors drew **government lots** on the GLO plat to capture the irregular remainder. Each government lot has a number, an area in acres (computed by the original surveyors), and a position on the GLO plat.

Practical implications:

- A government lot's area is what the GLO plat says it is, even if a modern measurement disagrees by a few acres. The grant ran by the GLO area.
- A government lot fronting on water has a riparian or littoral boundary; the upland boundary is the meander line shown on the GLO plat, but the **actual** boundary is the water at the date of statehood (or the date of the survey, depending on jurisdiction). Indiana follows the rule that the meander line is not the boundary.
- In Indiana, lake-front and river-front parcels described to a government lot need careful research; a number of state-specific decisions affect the boundary at the water.

## Common Indiana descriptions

Examples of common Indiana caption styles:

- "Part of the Northeast Quarter of Section 14, Township 17 North, Range 3 East, Hamilton County, Indiana."
- "All of Government Lot 2 in Section 7, Township 33 North, Range 9 East, Steuben County, Indiana."
- "The South Half of the Southwest Quarter of Section 32, Township 16 North, Range 4 East of the Second Principal Meridian, Marion County, Indiana, except that part taken for road right-of-way."

Always include the principal meridian when ambiguity is possible (a small area of southwestern Indiana is in the First Principal Meridian system; township-range numbers can repeat between meridians).

## Common pitfalls

- Treating the **center of section** as the geometric center of measured lines instead of the intersection of opposite-quarter-corner lines.
- Computing aliquot acreage from modern measurements when the parcel is fractional and a government lot governs.
- Failing to cite the principal meridian.
- Reading a 19th-century deed's "chains" as feet — a chain is 66 feet, a "link" is 0.66 feet.
