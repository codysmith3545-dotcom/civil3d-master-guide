> **TL;DR**
> 1. Boundary surveying is the application of evidence rules to reconstruct the lines created by the original grantor. The surveyor does not create boundaries; the surveyor locates them.
> 2. The classical hierarchy (often called the "dignity of calls") generally favors evidence in this order: natural monuments, artificial monuments, adjoiners, courses (bearings), distances, area, and coordinates last.
> 3. Intent of the parties controls. The hierarchy is a tool for inferring intent when the writing is ambiguous.
> 4. Indiana follows the general common-law rules with statutory and regulatory overlays from 865 IAC and the recorded plat acts.

## Evidence hierarchy

When a deed call conflicts with what is found on the ground, courts and surveyors use a roughly stable priority:

1. **Natural monuments** (rivers, ridges, identified trees still standing) — least likely to have been moved or mismeasured.
2. **Artificial monuments** (stones, iron pins, concrete posts) — must be identified as the original or controlling monument.
3. **Adjoiner calls** (a call to a neighbor's line or corner).
4. **Course** (bearings).
5. **Distance.**
6. **Area** (square footage or acreage).
7. **Coordinates** (rarely controlling in older deeds; common in modern subdivision plats).

This is a presumption, not an absolute rule. Intent of the parties controls when discernible.

## Original vs. retracement surveys

- **Original survey.** Creates lines that did not previously exist (subdivision plat, partition, first severance from a larger tract). The original surveyor's monuments and lines, however imperfectly placed, are the law of that survey.
- **Retracement.** Recovers an original survey's lines using evidence on the ground and in the record. The retracing surveyor follows the original footsteps; mathematical reconstruction is a last resort.

## Indiana context

- 865 IAC 1-12 requires the surveyor to identify monuments found, evaluate their authority, and report the basis for boundary location decisions.
- The Indiana Court of Appeals has affirmed in multiple opinions (e.g. issues of acquiescence, recognition and acquiescence, and boundary by agreement) that long-recognized lines may control over deed measurements.

## Common exam framing

- Two deeds describe adjoining tracts; the deeds overlap or leave a gap. Which controls? Apply senior rights and the hierarchy.
- An original iron pin is found 2 ft from the platted dimension. Does it control? Yes, generally — original monuments control over later measurements.
- A fence has stood on a line that conflicts with the record description for 30 years. May the surveyor relocate the line to match the deed? Indiana acquiescence and adverse possession doctrines may have already moved it. The surveyor reports the conflict; only a court adjudicates ownership.
