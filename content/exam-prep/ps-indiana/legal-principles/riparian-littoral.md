> **TL;DR**
> 1. Riparian rights attach to land along a flowing watercourse (river, stream). Littoral rights attach to land along a static water body (lake, ocean, pond).
> 2. The boundary of a riparian parcel typically extends to the ordinary high-water mark (OHWM) for public navigable waters, and to the thread of the stream (center) for non-navigable streams.
> 3. Indiana navigable waters: title to the bed is held by the state. Non-navigable streams: bed belongs to the adjoining landowners to the thread.
> 4. Allocation of new water frontage among multiple riparian owners on irregular shorelines uses one of several judicial methods: pie-slice (proportional to upland frontage along a curve), perpendicular-to-thread, or extension-of-side-lines. The choice depends on local case law and shoreline geometry.

## Definitions

- **Riparian.** Pertaining to a flowing watercourse.
- **Littoral.** Pertaining to a non-flowing water body (a lake or ocean).
- **OHWM (ordinary high-water mark).** The line on the bank or shore established by the fluctuations of water and indicated by physical characteristics such as a clear, natural line on the bank, shelving, changes in soil character, destruction of terrestrial vegetation, or the presence of litter and debris.
- **Thread of the stream.** The deepest channel or, where the channel is ambiguous, the geometric centerline.

## Indiana navigability

Indiana classifies waters as navigable (state title to the bed) or non-navigable (private title to the thread). The DNR maintains an official list of navigable waters in Indiana. Examples of navigable Indiana waters include the Ohio River, Wabash River below a certain point, and the West Fork of the White River below a certain point.

## Allocation of irregular shorelines

When multiple riparian owners share a curved or irregular shoreline (a bay, a meander loop), and the navigable channel lies offshore, three methods are commonly applied to allocate access to the channel:

1. **Pie-slice / proportional method.** Each owner's wedge is proportional to their upland frontage along the shore.
2. **Perpendicular-to-thread.** Side lines are drawn perpendicular to the channel.
3. **Extension of side lines.** Upland side lines are extended into the water.

State case law decides which applies in which geometry. Indiana follows the Wattles or "long-and-short" line method in some lake cases and a proportional method in others.

## Avulsion vs. accretion

- **Accretion.** Slow, gradual deposit of soil along a shoreline. The boundary moves with the new shoreline; the riparian owner gains land.
- **Avulsion.** Sudden change in a watercourse (a flood cuts a new channel). The boundary does not move; it remains where the old channel was.
- **Reliction.** Permanent retreat of a water body, exposing land. Treated like accretion.
