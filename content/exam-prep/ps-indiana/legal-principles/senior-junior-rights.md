> **TL;DR**
> 1. When a single grantor conveys multiple parcels over time, the earlier conveyance is "senior" and the later "junior." The senior deed takes its full description; the junior takes what is left.
> 2. Gaps and overlaps in description text are resolved against the junior deed. The junior parcel loses overlap area; the junior parcel keeps gap area only if the senior boundary does not extend to it.
> 3. A simultaneous conveyance (recorded plat) creates parcels that are all coequal: senior/junior does not apply among lots of the same plat, but the plat as a whole is senior to later out-conveyances.
> 4. Indiana follows the general common-law senior rights rule. The Indiana plat acts (IC 36-7-3 series) treat platted lots as simultaneously created.

## Why seniority matters

Most boundary conflicts arise because the same grantor described adjoining tracts in successive deeds without re-surveying, and the descriptions did not exactly close. The senior rights doctrine resolves the conflict by giving the earlier grantee priority.

## Sequential vs. simultaneous

- **Sequential.** A landowner sells off tracts over time. Each deed is dated and recorded in order. Apply senior rights to gaps and overlaps.
- **Simultaneous.** A landowner records a subdivision plat that creates many lots at once. All lots are simultaneously created — they have equal rights. Gaps and overlaps within the plat are typically resolved by proration along the boundary among the affected lots.

## Indiana application

When a surveyor in Indiana encounters a record overlap between two deeds, the surveyor must:

1. Determine the recording date of each deed.
2. Apply the deed's description to the ground; identify monuments, occupation, and adjoiner evidence.
3. Where the descriptions conflict and no extrinsic evidence resolves them, the earlier-recorded deed controls.
4. Report the conflict in the surveyor's report (865 IAC 1-12).

## Worked example

Grantor sells Tract A in 1965 with a metes-and-bounds description that calls for "200 feet along the north line of the Smith property." In 1972 the same grantor sells Tract B, which when plotted overlaps Tract A by 8 ft.

- Tract A (1965) is senior. The 1965 deed gets its full 200 ft.
- Tract B (1972) is junior. Tract B's description is reduced by the 8 ft overlap.
