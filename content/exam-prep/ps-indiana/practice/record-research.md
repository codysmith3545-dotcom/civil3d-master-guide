> **TL;DR**
> 1. Boundary surveying begins in the courthouse. A surveyor must research the deed of the subject parcel, all adjoiner deeds, recorded plats, easements of record, and prior surveys before going to the field.
> 2. Indiana record sources: county recorder (deeds, plats, easements), county surveyor (tie cards, surveyor's records, prior surveys filed under IC 36-2-12), county assessor (parcel maps for context only, not authoritative), and INDOT (right-of-way plans).
> 3. Title commitments from a title company are useful but are not a substitute for direct research. The surveyor's certification stands on the surveyor's own work.
> 4. A complete research file is part of the record retention requirement (865 IAC 1-12-13).

## Standard research checklist

1. **Subject deed.** Most recent vesting deed and all relevant prior deeds back to the original severance.
2. **Adjoiner deeds.** Vesting deeds for every parcel that shares a boundary with the subject.
3. **Recorded plats.** Subdivision, condominium, or partition plats that include or border the subject parcel.
4. **Easements and encumbrances of record.** Utility easements, drainage easements, access easements, restrictive covenants.
5. **Prior surveys.** Survey plats filed with the county surveyor or referenced in deeds.
6. **Tie cards.** Section-corner perpetuation records from the county surveyor's office (IC 36-2-12).
7. **Right-of-way plans.** State, county, and municipal road records.

## Indiana-specific resources

- **Indiana Recorders' offices.** Each of Indiana's 92 counties maintains a recorder of deeds. Marion County deeds and plats are indexed online (Marion County Recorder).
- **County surveyor offices.** Surveyor's records, tie cards, and many historical surveys are public records. Some Indiana counties (notably Hamilton, Hendricks, and Boone) have well-developed online portals; others require an in-person visit.
- **INDOT Real Estate Division.** Right-of-way plans for state highways.

## Title commitments

Title companies prepare commitments that list deeds of record, easements, and encumbrances. A surveyor receiving a commitment should:

- Verify each document listed actually exists in the recorder's office.
- Plot each easement and encumbrance on the boundary plat.
- Note any document referenced in a deed but not listed in the commitment.

The surveyor's seal certifies the boundary work, not the title; the surveyor is not a title examiner.
