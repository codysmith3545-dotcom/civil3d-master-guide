> **TL;DR**
> 1. The Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices (MUTCD) is the federal standard for signs, pavement markings, signals, and temporary traffic control on all U.S. public streets and highways.
> 2. The current edition is the 11th (December 2023), published by FHWA with AASHTO as a joint sponsor through the National Committee on Uniform Traffic Control Devices.
> 3. States adopt the federal MUTCD, adopt it with a state supplement, or publish a state MUTCD that is "in substantial conformance"; Indiana publishes the *Indiana MUTCD*.

## What the MUTCD is

The MUTCD is the national rulebook for the devices used to communicate with the driving public. It is published by the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA), but it is developed through the National Committee on Uniform Traffic Control Devices, on which AASHTO sits along with FHWA, ITE, IMSA, and other stakeholders. Compliance is required on all roads open to public travel under 23 CFR Part 655.

The 11th Edition was issued by FHWA in December 2023 and replaces the 2009 Edition with revisions. States have a federally established compliance period during which they must adopt the new edition or an in-substantial-conformance state version.

Unlike the AASHTO Green Book, the MUTCD is a U.S. government work and is **not** copyrighted in the same way; FHWA publishes the full text on its website. State MUTCDs and supplements may have additional copyright considerations and adoption procedures.

## What it covers

- **Part 1.** General principles, definitions, scope.
- **Part 2.** Signs — regulatory, warning, guide, and specific information signs. Includes sizes, colors, retroreflectivity, placement.
- **Part 3.** Pavement markings — longitudinal lines, crosswalks, stop bars, arrows, words, colors, retroreflectivity.
- **Part 4.** Highway traffic signals — signal warrants, head arrangement, phasing, pedestrian signals, accessible pedestrian signals (APS), preemption.
- **Part 5.** Low-volume roads.
- **Part 6.** Temporary traffic control (work zones). Likely the section a civil designer touches most often. Defines typical applications (TAs), taper lengths, drum and barricade spacing, advance-warning sign series.
- **Part 7.** Traffic control for school areas.
- **Part 8.** Traffic control at highway-rail and highway–light rail grade crossings.
- **Part 9.** Bicycle facilities (signs, markings, signals).
- **Part 10.** Traffic control for railroad and light-rail-transit grade crossings (in some editions consolidated with Part 8).

The 11th Edition reorganized and expanded several parts, notably guidance on bicycle facilities, automated and connected vehicles, autonomous-vehicle considerations, and accessible pedestrian features. Verify the current part numbers in the table of contents before citing.

## Federal MUTCD vs. state supplements

23 CFR 655.603 gives states three paths:

1. **Adopt the federal MUTCD.** The federal text governs as published.
2. **Adopt the federal MUTCD with a state supplement.** The federal text governs except where the state supplement modifies or adds to it.
3. **Adopt a state MUTCD that is in substantial conformance.** The state document governs; FHWA reviews it for conformance with the federal manual.

Indiana follows path 3. INDOT publishes the *Indiana MUTCD*, which mirrors the federal manual with state-specific modifications. Use the Indiana MUTCD for any project on a public road in Indiana — state, county, or municipal. The Indiana MUTCD is hosted on the INDOT Engineering Standards page.

## Practical implications for civil drawings

- **Maintenance of traffic (MOT) sheets.** TA references in your MOT come from MUTCD Part 6 (or the Indiana MUTCD equivalent). Match the typical application name and figure number on the sheet.
- **Signage and striping plans.** Sign codes (R for regulatory, W for warning, M for marker, etc.) and standard sign sizes come from MUTCD Part 2. Pavement marking widths and colors come from Part 3.
- **Signal plans.** Head arrangement, mast-arm lengths, and signal phasing follow Part 4.
- **Permits.** Driveway and access permits often reference sight-distance triangles and sign placements that ultimately trace back to the MUTCD.
