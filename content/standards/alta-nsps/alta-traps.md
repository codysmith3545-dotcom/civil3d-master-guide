> **TL;DR**
> 1. Most ALTA survey deficiencies fall into a few categories: **missing or wrong Table A items**, **incomplete Schedule B-II coverage**, **wrong certification language**, and **failure to show observable evidence** (encroachments, utilities, access).
> 2. The title company and lender rely on the ALTA survey to remove the survey exception from the title policy. A deficient survey delays closing or, worse, results in an uncovered loss.
> 3. Prevention comes from a disciplined workflow: read the title commitment before going to the field, confirm Table A items in writing, and use a checklist before signing the plat.

## Trap 1: Missing or wrong Table A items

The client (or the client's attorney or lender) selects optional Table A items at the time the survey is ordered. Common failures:

- **Not confirming Table A items in writing.** A verbal request to "do the usual Table A items" is ambiguous. Get the list in writing before starting field work.
- **Performing items that were not requested** and billing for them. This is a business problem, not a standards problem, but it erodes trust.
- **Omitting a requested item from the plat.** If Table A item 11(a) (location of utilities observed on site) was requested, every visible utility feature must appear on the plat. A missing transformer pad or manhole is a deficiency.
- **Confusing Table A item 6(a) and 6(b).** Item 6(a) requires parking-space striping and ADA-space identification. Item 6(b) requires the total parking count. Delivering 6(a) without 6(b) (or vice versa) is a common error.

**Prevention:** Maintain a Table A checklist that the client signs at engagement. Cross-reference it before certifying the plat. See [Table A items](table-a.md).

## Trap 2: Incomplete Schedule B-II coverage

The 2021 standards require the surveyor to address every Schedule B-II exception in the title commitment:

- **Plot it if it can be plotted.** Easements with a defined location (metes-and-bounds description, "the north 10 feet of Lot 5") must appear on the plat.
- **Note it if it cannot be plotted.** Blanket easements, restrictions without spatial dimension, and items with insufficient description to locate must be listed in the notes with an explanation of why they cannot be plotted.
- **Do not ignore it.** A Schedule B-II exception that appears on neither the plat nor in the notes is a deficiency.

Common failures:

- **Not reading the title commitment before going to the field.** The surveyor arrives on site without knowing which easements to look for.
- **Receiving an updated title commitment after the field work is complete** and not revisiting the plat to address new exceptions.
- **Missing a "utility easement per plat"** that is actually shown on the subdivision plat but was not carried to the ALTA plat.

**Prevention:** Read the entire title commitment before the first field visit. Mark up each Schedule B-II item with its disposition (plotted, noted, or needs research). After the plat is drafted, verify every item against the markup.

## Trap 3: Wrong certification language

The 2021 standards prescribe specific certification language. The surveyor may modify it only in limited ways:

- Adding the name of additional relying parties.
- Deleting references to Table A items that were not requested.
- Adding state-required language (see [state overlays](state-overlays.md)).

Common failures:

- **Using outdated certification language** from the 2011 or 2016 standards. The 2021 language differs; using old language may cause the title company to reject the survey.
- **Omitting a relying party.** The lender, the borrower, and the title company are typically named. If one is missing, the survey cannot be relied upon by that party.
- **Adding unauthorized disclaimers.** Disclaiming liability for subsurface conditions, environmental matters, or flood-zone determinations that are not part of the ALTA survey is acceptable. Disclaiming liability for matters that the standards require the surveyor to address is not.

**Prevention:** Keep a current copy of the 2021 certification template. Update the relying-party list for each project. Have the template reviewed by the firm's attorney.

## Trap 4: Failure to show encroachments

The standards require the surveyor to show improvements on the surveyed property and to identify encroachments:

- **Building encroachments across boundary lines** (the subject building onto an adjoiner, or an adjoiner's building onto the subject).
- **Building encroachments into setback lines** (if setback lines are shown per Table A item 18, or if they are otherwise part of the survey scope).
- **Fence encroachments.**
- **Pavement and parking encroachments.**

Common failures:

- **Measuring to the building foundation but not to overhangs, eaves, or canopies.** Some encroachments are above grade. If the overhang crosses the boundary, it must be shown.
- **Not measuring to the adjacent property.** If the subject building is within a few feet of the boundary, the surveyor must determine whether it encroaches. "We didn't survey the adjoiner" is not an excuse under the ALTA standards.

**Prevention:** Measure every improvement that is close to a boundary line. If an encroachment is ambiguous (within measurement tolerance), note it on the plat and let the title company decide.

## Trap 5: Parking count errors

Table A item 6 (parking) is one of the most commonly requested items. Errors include:

- **Miscounting spaces.** Large parking lots with faded striping require careful counting. Walk the lot; do not count from aerial imagery.
- **Missing ADA spaces.** ADA-accessible spaces (van-accessible, standard accessible, and access aisles) must be identified separately.
- **Not noting non-standard spaces.** Compact spaces, tandem spaces, and spaces that do not meet local code dimensions should be noted.

**Prevention:** Count in person. Photograph the lot. Verify the count against the site plan if available.

## Trap 6: Missing utility evidence

Table A item 11(a) requires the surveyor to show the location of observed above-ground utility facilities. Item 11(b) requests additional information. Common failures:

- **Ignoring overhead wires.** Overhead electric and communication lines crossing the property are utility evidence. Show the poles and the direction of the wires.
- **Missing underground utility evidence.** Valve boxes, manhole covers, cleanouts, vent pipes, meter pits, and pavement patches in utility-trench patterns are observable evidence of underground utilities.
- **Not contacting utility companies.** The 2021 standards expect the surveyor to note observable evidence. If Table A item 11(b) is requested, the surveyor must also contact utilities. Do not confuse the two items.

**Prevention:** Walk the entire property. Mark every utility feature in the field. Cross-reference against the title commitment for utility easements.

## General risk mitigation

- **Use a checklist.** A pre-certification checklist that mirrors the 2021 standards sections and Table A items catches most deficiencies before the plat is signed.
- **Peer review.** Have a second licensed surveyor review the plat and certification before delivery.
- **Keep the title commitment in the file.** If the survey is challenged, the file must show what title commitment was used, what Schedule B-II items were addressed, and how.
- **Date the field work.** The certification date and the field-work date are different. If significant time passes between field work and certification, note whether conditions may have changed.
