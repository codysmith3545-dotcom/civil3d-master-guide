> **TL;DR**
> 1. The 2021 ALTA/NSPS standards prescribe the surveyor's certification language; the surveyor signs and seals the plat with that language and the date of the most recent fieldwork update.
> 2. The certification is *to* named parties: the buyer, the lender, and the title-insurance company. Adding or removing parties later requires a re-certification, normally for a fee.
> 3. State-specific overlays apply on top of the federal standard — in Indiana, the State Board of Registration for Professional Surveyors imposes seal, signature, and report-content rules that are independent of ALTA.

## What the certification says

The 2021 standards include a fixed certification template. It is a single paragraph in which the surveyor states that the survey was made in accordance with the 2021 Minimum Standard Detail Requirements for ALTA/NSPS Land Title Surveys, jointly established and adopted by ALTA and NSPS, and that it includes Items [...] of Table A as elected by the client. The blank fields are:

- Names of the parties to whom the certification runs.
- Date of the certification (the date the surveyor completed or updated the field work, not the date of the title commitment).
- The list of Table A items included.
- The signature, seal, and license number of the licensed surveyor.

The standards permit only narrow modifications to this language. State law modifications are common and accepted; substantive deletion or weakening of the certification is not. A title insurer that receives a survey with an altered certification will often refuse it.

## Who the certification runs to

The certification names the parties relying on the survey. Typical parties are:

- **Buyer / borrower.** The party acquiring the property.
- **Seller**, where the seller is delivering the survey.
- **Lender** (and its successors and assigns).
- **Title insurance company** issuing the policy.

Adding parties after delivery — for example, when an additional lender joins a deal — requires a written re-certification request. The surveyor will typically re-issue the plat with a new date and the additional party named, and may charge a re-certification fee. The standards do not contemplate "running to the world" certifications; the named-parties model is part of how surveyor liability is bounded.

## What the certification does, legally

A common misconception is that the certification is a guarantee of accuracy or a warranty against errors. It is not. It is a statement that:

1. The survey was prepared in accordance with the named professional standard.
2. The named Table A items were performed.
3. The named parties may rely on the survey.

The legal effect is to extend the surveyor's professional duty of care to the named parties. A party not named in the certification generally has no standing to sue the surveyor for negligence, although third-party reliance theories vary by state.

The certification does not displace the surveyor's licensure obligations under state law — those obligations are independent and may impose a higher standard than the ALTA/NSPS minimum. The certification also does not warrant that title is good; that is the job of the title commitment and the policy, not the survey.

## Indiana state-specific overlays

Indiana surveyors are licensed by the State Board of Registration for Professional Surveyors under 865 IAC. Indiana imposes additional requirements on every survey, ALTA or otherwise:

- **Surveyor's report.** Indiana requires a written surveyor's report under 865 IAC 1-12 for boundary surveys (the *Rule 12* report). The ALTA plat is the survey, but the Rule 12 report is a separate Indiana deliverable that explains the surveyor's analysis of evidence and conclusions about uncertainty.
- **Seal and signature.** The plat must bear the surveyor's seal, signature, and the date signed. Digital seals are permitted under specific conditions in Indiana.
- **Relative positional precision.** Indiana also imposes its own measurement standards; the ALTA RPP must be reported in addition.
- **Description.** The legal description on the plat must conform to Indiana's standards for land descriptions — for example, calls to monuments must use language consistent with state practice.

Other states impose their own overlays. Always verify the current state board rules where the property is located before finalizing a certification.

## Practical considerations

- Get the list of certification parties in writing from the client before fieldwork. Adding a major lender at closing on a Friday afternoon is the most common cause of last-minute re-certifications.
- Match the date of certification to the date of last fieldwork. If a site visit is repeated to update something material, the certification date moves; document the reason.
- Do not hand-edit the standard certification language. If a state overlay requires modification, use the modification the standards permit and note it in the report.
