> **TL;DR**
> 1. The **2021 ALTA/NSPS Minimum Standard Detail Requirements** govern commercial title-insurance surveys nationwide; in Indiana, the surveyor must also comply with **865 IAC 1-12**, which is stricter on monumentation and reporting.
> 2. **Table A** items are *negotiated* between the surveyor, client, lender, and title insurer; they are not all mandatory. The surveyor should not perform a Table A item without a fee for it.
> 3. **Schedule B-II** exceptions on the title commitment drive the survey's depiction of easements, restrictions, and other encumbrances. Every Schedule B-II item with a recorded location should be plotted or affirmatively noted as undeterminable.
> 4. The **certification block** is a contract: certify only to parties who paid for the survey and only to the standards the survey actually meets. Indiana surveyors should not certify to ALTA/NSPS if the survey scope did not include the required research and measurement standards.

## Learning objectives

- Negotiate a Table A item list that matches the project scope and budget.
- Read a Schedule B-II and translate each item into a plottable or noted feature on the survey.
- Compose an ALTA/NSPS certification block consistent with 2021 standards and Indiana licensure.
- Identify the most common defects in ALTA surveys submitted for title insurance.

## Reading

### 1. What the ALTA/NSPS standard is

The ALTA/NSPS Minimum Standard Detail Requirements is a joint product of the American Land Title Association and the National Society of Professional Surveyors. The 2021 edition is the current version and superseded the 2016 standard.

The standard's purpose is to give the title-insurance industry a uniform survey product on which an extended-coverage policy (ALTA Owner's or Loan policy with survey-related endorsements) can be issued. It is not a substitute for state minimum standards; the surveyor must comply with **both** the ALTA standard and the state rule. In Indiana, that means 865 IAC 1-12 monumentation, accuracy, and reporting requirements apply in addition.

### 2. The structure of the standard

The 2021 ALTA/NSPS standard has six main sections plus Table A:

1. **Purpose** - defines what the survey is for.
2. **Request for survey** - what the surveyor must receive (record description, title commitment with all Schedule B-II exceptions, copies of recorded easements).
3. **Surveying standards and standards of care** - state minimum standards apply.
4. **Records research** - the surveyor must obtain and review the title commitment and recorded easements at a minimum.
5. **Field work** - what must be located: boundary, monuments, exterior improvements, encroachments, evidence of easements.
6. **Plat or map preparation** - what must appear on the drawing.

### 3. Table A items

Table A is the negotiated scope. The 2021 list includes (numbering varies; consult the current standard):

- **Item 1** - Monumentation of corners.
- **Item 2** - Address.
- **Item 3** - Flood zone information.
- **Item 4** - Gross land area.
- **Item 5** - Vertical relief.
- **Item 6(a)/(b)** - Zoning classification and zoning report.
- **Item 7(a)/(b)/(c)** - Building setbacks per zoning; building height; floor area.
- **Item 8** - Substantial features on the property.
- **Item 9** - Parking count and dimension.
- **Item 10(a)/(b)** - Party walls and visible utilities.
- **Item 11** - Underground utilities.
- **Item 13** - Names of adjoining owners.
- **Item 14** - Site distance triangles at intersections.
- **Item 16** - Observable evidence of earth-moving, building, or other recent changes.
- **Item 17** - Proposed changes to the right-of-way.
- **Item 18** - Wetland delineation by appropriately qualified persons.
- **Item 19** - Offsite easements or servitudes benefiting the property.
- **Item 20** - Professional liability insurance certification.
- **Item 21** - Other - any agreed scope.

The 2021 edition's item numbering is the authoritative reference; verify against the current standard before ordering or quoting.

**Common Table A pitfalls:**

- Quoting Item 11 (underground utilities) and then locating only "as marked by 811." The standard requires the surveyor to use reasonable methods to locate underground utilities; "as marked" alone is rarely sufficient without explicit limitation.
- Plotting Item 6(a) zoning without obtaining a written zoning report from the jurisdiction. The surveyor should certify only what the surveyor has verified.
- Performing Item 18 wetland delineation without a qualified professional. Surveyors are not wetland delineators by default.

### 4. Schedule B-II analysis

Schedule B-II of the title commitment lists exceptions to coverage. Each exception must be addressed on the survey:

- **Easements** with a recorded reference: plot the easement to scale from the recorded description.
- **Easements without a recorded reference** (e.g., "an easement for ingress and egress over the existing driveway"): show the apparent location and note "as referenced in [document]."
- **Building or zoning restrictions**: plot setbacks if Table A items 6/7 are ordered; otherwise, note "subject to recorded restrictions, document [#]."
- **Mineral or oil/gas reservations**: typically noted but not plotted unless surface uses are documented.
- **Other matters** (lis pendens, judgments): usually noted, not plotted.

For each Schedule B-II item, the surveyor's options are: **plot**, **note as plottable but indeterminate**, or **note as not plottable**. The third option requires a written explanation.

### 5. The certification block

The 2021 standard provides a specific certification text. The surveyor certifies that the survey was made in accordance with the standards in effect on the survey date and meets the accuracy standards adopted by ALTA/NSPS for the use of the named parties.

**The named parties matter.** The certification is a contract: a party named on the certification can rely on the survey to the standards stated and can sue if it falls short. Adding a party after the fact (a lender wants to be added six months later) typically requires a re-certification and may require a re-walk to confirm conditions have not changed.

**Do not certify to ALTA/NSPS if the work does not meet ALTA/NSPS.** The most common Indiana surveyor mistake is to certify to ALTA/NSPS without performing the research required - especially the Schedule B-II plotting and the Table A items the client paid for. A short scope without a corresponding short certification is asking for a malpractice claim.

### 6. The most common defects

Defect patterns in ALTA surveys seen in claims:

- **Missing certification party.** Lender or title insurer not named; survey unusable for closing.
- **Schedule B-II items unaddressed.** Surveyor never received the title commitment, or received it and ignored it.
- **Underground utilities marked but not located.** Survey shows 811 paint marks dated weeks before the survey; not adequate.
- **Setbacks plotted from outdated zoning text.** The zoning text changed between the surveyor's research and the closing date.
- **Encroachments visible in the field but not shown on the plat.** A fence 0.4 ft over the line, a corner of a shed crossing the setback - both are findings the surveyor must show.

### 7. Indiana-specific notes

865 IAC 1-12 requires:

- Monuments of the type and material the rule specifies, regardless of ALTA Item 1's wording.
- The Indiana surveyor's seal and signature, beyond the ALTA certification block.
- Compliance with Indiana research expectations (more deed history than ALTA strictly requires for many parcels).
- Retention of the research and field records per 865 IAC.

When the ALTA standard and 865 IAC differ, the **stricter** rule applies.

## Self-assessment

<details>
<summary>Question 1</summary>

The client asks for an ALTA survey with Table A Items 1, 2, 3, 4, and 11. The fee is set. After the work begins the lender requests Item 6(a) zoning. What is the correct response?

**Answer:** Negotiate a change order for Item 6(a). The original fee did not cover zoning research; performing it without a fee invites both unprofitable work and a surveying-without-scope problem.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2</summary>

Schedule B-II item 7 is "an easement for utility purposes, recorded in Deed Book 612, Page 145." The deed describes an easement "20 feet in width, centered on a line beginning at the northeast corner of the property and running southwesterly to a point on the south line." How do you plot this?

**Answer:** Plot a 20-ft wide easement centered on the described line. If the south termination is ambiguous, plot to the apparent termination shown by physical evidence (an existing utility line in the indicated direction) and note any ambiguity.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3</summary>

The certification block names only the buyer. The lender calls a week later asking to be added. What is the correct procedure?

**Answer:** Re-issue the survey with an updated certification block including the lender. If the survey is more than the standard staleness period (commonly 90 days under lender policy), the surveyor should re-walk the site to confirm conditions before re-certifying.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4</summary>

When the ALTA Item 1 monument specification conflicts with 865 IAC 1-12, which controls?

**Answer:** The stricter rule controls. In practice, 865 IAC 1-12 monumentation is stricter than the typical ALTA Item 1 default, so the Indiana rule prevails.
</details>
