> **TL;DR**
> 1. A **lost corner** can only be restored after a diligent search has failed to recover the original or sufficient evidence to fix the position. Most "lost" corners are actually **obliterated** - position can be recovered from collateral evidence.
> 2. **Proportional measurement** is the legal method of last resort. Single proportion is for line corners; double proportion is for section corners on both directions of subdivision.
> 3. Evidence weighting follows a hierarchy: original monument > witness trees and accessories > field notes > collateral testimony > proportional measurement.
> 4. In Indiana, the **County Surveyor** maintains the legal record of section corners under IC 36-2-12 and a restoration should be reported to the County Surveyor for inclusion in the section corner record.

## Learning objectives

- Distinguish between existent, obliterated, and lost corners.
- Apply single and double proportional measurement consistently with the BLM Manual of Surveying Instructions.
- Document the evidence supporting a corner restoration in a form acceptable to the County Surveyor.
- Recognize when GLO field notes may be unreliable and what to do in that case.

## Reading

### 1. Existent, obliterated, and lost

The BLM Manual of Surveying Instructions (2009) and centuries of case law recognize three states a PLSS corner can be in:

- **Existent.** The original monument, or its legally recognized replacement, is in place. Recovery is by physical inspection.
- **Obliterated.** The original monument is no longer present, but **sufficient evidence** allows the corner position to be re-established. Evidence sources include witness trees, accessories (mounds, pits, stone deposits), bearings and distances to other monuments, fence lines that have stood since the original survey, testimony of credible witnesses with personal knowledge.
- **Lost.** No evidence of the original position remains. The corner must be re-established by proportional measurement among adjoining existent corners.

The legal effect of the three categories is profoundly different. An existent or obliterated corner is **fixed** at the original position, even if proportional measurement would put it elsewhere. A lost corner is **set** at the proportional position, which may not match the original.

The surveyor's duty is to perform a **diligent search** before declaring a corner lost. The search should include:

- Surface inspection of the calculated proportional position and a generous radius around it.
- Probing or excavation if reasonable accessories are reported.
- Magnetometer survey for iron monuments or buried iron accessories.
- Interviews with adjoining landowners.
- Review of County Surveyor records, prior survey records, and contemporaneous surveys.
- Comparison with the original GLO field notes for witness trees, mounds, line trees, and bearings.

A "lost" finding should be documented with the search history, not just stated.

### 2. The original monument and witness evidence

The GLO surveys of the early 1800s in Indiana set wooden posts (sometimes stones) at section and quarter-section corners. The posts are usually long gone, but the **accessories** can persist:

- **Witness trees.** Marked with a blaze, scribed with section and township numbers. Indiana hardwoods (oak, hickory, beech) sometimes survive 200 years; the blaze grows over but can be exposed by careful chiseling.
- **Mounds and pits.** GLO surveyors heaped earth and dug pits as supplementary marks. Time and tillage erase these, but a careful soil profile in a wooded fence row can still show them.
- **Stone deposits.** A "deposit of stones" or a "memorial stone" near the corner is sometimes recoverable when the wooden post is not.

Original field notes, available through the BLM General Land Office Records site (glorecords.blm.gov), describe each corner and its accessories. Reading the notes is non-negotiable for serious retracement.

### 3. Proportional measurement

When a corner is genuinely lost, proportional measurement re-establishes its position.

**Single proportion** is used for a corner on a line, with existent corners on either side. The lost corner is set at a position that maintains the original record proportion of distance between the existent corners. For example, if the original notes call the corner 40.00 chains from the section corner and 40.00 chains from the quarter corner (a quarter-quarter), and the measured distance between existent corners is 80.15 chains, the lost corner falls 40.075 chains from each.

**Double proportion** is used for a section corner. Two pairs of bearings-and-distances are used: one on each direction of subdivision. The lost corner is placed at the intersection of the proportionate latitudes (north-south) and the proportionate departures (east-west) from the four nearest existent corners.

A few practical points:

- **Use the same units the GLO used.** Indiana GLO surveys are in chains (66 ft). Convert measurements consistently.
- **Account for known correction in the original.** The 1855 surveys typically have a closing convention different from a modern least-squares adjustment; documented errors in the original should be carried forward, not "corrected."
- **Proportion the record distance, not the latest measurement.** The record proportion is what controls.

### 4. Evidence weighting

When evidence conflicts (a found stone disagrees with a proportional position by 8 ft), the surveyor applies the legal hierarchy:

| Priority | Evidence | Comment |
|----------|----------|---------|
| 1 | Original monument in place | Controls |
| 2 | Accessories (witness tree blaze, scribed stone) | Controls if accessory's relationship to corner is original and uncorrupted |
| 3 | Collateral monuments (next corner up the line) | Controls if connected by an unbroken line of original calls |
| 4 | Fence lines coincident with the line for 60+ years | Treat as evidence of corner location, not as a substitute |
| 5 | Testimony of contemporaries | Weigh credibility; cross-check with documents |
| 6 | Proportional measurement | Used only when 1-5 are exhausted |

The surveyor's narrative should walk through the hierarchy explicitly: "evidence A was tested and rejected because..., evidence B was found and accepted because..." A successor surveyor or a court should be able to audit the reasoning.

### 5. Monumentation requirements

865 IAC 1-12 requires the surveyor to set a monument of specified type and material at every corner the survey establishes or restores. Indiana typically expects:

- A **steel rod or rebar of at least 5/8 inch diameter, at least 30 inches long**, with a **plastic or aluminum cap** stamped with the surveyor's license number.
- A **stone or concrete monument** at section corners when the rule applies.
- A **witness corner** when the true corner cannot be set (e.g., in a paved road) with a documented offset.

The restoration should be reported to the **County Surveyor** under IC 36-2-12 within the timeframe set by county rule. The report should include a sketch showing the recovered or proportional position, the evidence used, and the monument set.

### 6. A worked example

A surveyor in Hancock County is asked to retrace the south line of Section 14, T17N, R7E. The SE corner of Section 14 is recovered as a stamped stone consistent with GLO notes; the SW corner is not found. The south-quarter corner is also not found. Adjoining survey records by the County Surveyor show that a fence has stood along the south line for at least 70 years and that the line passes through a tree blaze the surveyor identifies as a hickory line tree from the original notes.

Decision: the south line is **obliterated, not lost**. The line is fixed by the SE corner and the line tree. The SW corner is recovered as the intersection of the line tree's line with the west line of Section 14 (also obliterated and fixed by a different set of accessories). Proportional measurement is **not** used because sufficient collateral evidence exists.

The County Surveyor record receives the new monument, the evidence narrative, and a sketch. The legal status: corner restored to original position.

## Self-assessment

<details>
<summary>Question 1</summary>

You find a 1/2-inch iron pin near the calculated proportional position of a section corner. No stamping; no witness evidence. Should the pin control the corner?

**Answer:** Not on its face. An unstamped iron pin is uncalled-for evidence; investigate its source (prior survey records, County Surveyor records) before accepting or rejecting. If no source is established, the pin is hearsay and the proportional position likely controls (unless other evidence supports the pin).
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2</summary>

The original GLO field notes report a witness tree at North 45 deg East, 80 links from the corner. You find a candidate hickory with an old blaze at North 47 deg East, 90 links. Should this evidence be accepted?

**Answer:** Probably yes if no better evidence is available. GLO bearings and distances were measured by compass and chain with known error budgets; a few degrees and a few links of slop is normal. The corner is then offset back from the recovered tree at the record distance and bearing, with a noted residual.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3</summary>

What is the difference between single and double proportional measurement?

**Answer:** Single proportion is used for a lost corner on a single line between two existent corners; it preserves the record proportion of distance. Double proportion is used for a section corner lost on both directions of subdivision; it intersects proportionate latitudes and departures from the four nearest existent corners.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4</summary>

Under Indiana law, who maintains the legal record of section corners?

**Answer:** The County Surveyor, per IC 36-2-12.
</details>
