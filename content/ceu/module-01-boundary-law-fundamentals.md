> **TL;DR**
> 1. **Junior/senior rights**: when adjoining deeds describe the same line differently, the older (senior) deed controls. The junior conveyance takes what is left.
> 2. **Adverse possession** in Indiana requires open, notorious, exclusive, continuous, and hostile possession for **10 years**, plus the claimant must have paid the taxes (IC 32-21-7).
> 3. **Acquiescence** and **practical location** can establish a boundary line different from the record description, but require long-standing recognition by both owners.
> 4. **Riparian rights** in Indiana follow the common law: ownership of land bordering a non-navigable stream extends to the thread (centerline); on navigable waters, to the ordinary high-water mark.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module the licensee should be able to:

- Apply the priority of calls (senior rights, written intent, possession) when reconciling overlapping descriptions.
- Identify the statutory elements of adverse possession under Indiana law and distinguish them from acquiescence.
- Apportion riparian frontage along a non-navigable Indiana stream using the long-lake or pie-slice methods as appropriate.
- Recognize when a boundary survey must elevate a senior right above a more accurate measurement.

## Reading

### 1. The hierarchy of priorities

Boundary retracement is governed by an ordered list of priorities. When two pieces of evidence disagree, the surveyor follows the order rather than the more "precise" measurement.

1. **Senior rights.** The earlier conveyance from a common grantor controls the line. The grantor cannot give to a junior what was already conveyed to a senior. This is not negotiable on accuracy grounds; an inch in the senior deed beats a hundredth in the junior survey.
2. **Written intent of the parties.** Look first at the four corners of the deed. If the calls are ambiguous, extrinsic evidence (the survey referenced, the plat on file, contemporaneous correspondence) can clarify.
3. **Calls for surveys.** A call to "the X survey of [date]" makes that survey controlling for the line it describes, even if a later survey is more precise.
4. **Calls for monuments.** Natural monuments (rivers, trees) outrank artificial monuments (iron pipes, concrete posts), which outrank record monuments (a corner of another tract called for in the deed).
5. **Calls for adjoiners.** "Thence along the line of Jones" places the line where Jones's title actually runs, not where the bearing-distance call would put it.
6. **Bearings and distances.** Bearings generally outrank distances; both yield to higher-priority calls.
7. **Coordinates and area.** Lowest priority. Area calls are usually descriptive, not controlling, unless every other call has been exhausted.

This is the *common law* order. 865 IAC 1-12 codifies the surveyor's duty to weigh evidence consistent with these priorities, not to "average" inconsistent calls into an arbitrary compromise.

### 2. Junior/senior rights illustrated

Suppose a grantor conveyed the east 100 ft of a 200-ft-wide parent tract to Adams in 1955, then conveyed "the remainder, being the west 100 ft" to Baker in 1960. A modern survey reveals the parent tract is actually 198.4 ft wide.

- Adams (senior) gets a full 100 ft from the east line.
- Baker (junior) gets 98.4 ft, not 100 ft, because the senior conveyance already took its share.

The surveyor's job is to identify the senior deed, give it its full call, and absorb the deficiency (or surplus) in the junior tract. A common error is to "split the difference" between Adams and Baker, which has no legal basis and usually creates a title problem.

### 3. Adverse possession in Indiana

Indiana's adverse possession statute (IC 32-21-7-1) requires the claimant to demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence:

- **Open and notorious** possession (visible enough that the record owner would know if they looked).
- **Exclusive** possession (not shared with the record owner or the public).
- **Continuous** possession for **10 years** without significant interruption.
- **Hostile** possession (without permission, claiming the land as one's own).

In Indiana, unlike many states, the claimant must also have **paid the taxes** on the disputed strip during the statutory period. This is a substantive requirement that often defeats adverse possession claims along driveways and fence-line strips that are not separately assessed.

The surveyor's role is **not** to adjudicate adverse possession - that is for a court. But the surveyor should note long-standing occupation that conflicts with the record line, report it on the plat, and recommend the client seek legal advice. 865 IAC 1-12 requires the surveyor to show fences, occupation lines, and obvious encroachments.

### 4. Acquiescence and practical location

Where adverse possession fails (often for lack of hostility or tax payment), Indiana courts may recognize a boundary by **acquiescence**. The elements:

- A line that was treated as the boundary by both adjoining owners.
- For a long period (Indiana case law typically requires at least the **20-year** prescriptive period for boundary acquiescence, though some opinions accept shorter periods under specific facts).
- With mutual recognition (often a fence both parties maintained).

**Practical location** is a similar doctrine: when the original deed line is uncertain and the adjoining owners agreed on a line, then occupied accordingly, that line becomes the boundary. The agreement need not be written; the parties' conduct supplies the evidence.

Again, the surveyor reports occupation evidence and lets a court decide if it rises to acquiescence. The point of the survey is to make the evidence visible to whomever needs it.

### 5. Riparian rights along Indiana waters

Indiana distinguishes between **navigable** and **non-navigable** waters. The Indiana Department of Natural Resources maintains a list of navigable streams; if the water is on the list (or has been judicially declared navigable), the bed belongs to the state and riparian ownership extends to the **ordinary high-water mark**.

For non-navigable streams, the common law is that ownership extends to the **thread** (centerline) of the stream. Apportionment of frontage among adjoining riparians is typically done by:

- **The pie-slice method** for circular or rounded shorelines (e.g., natural lakes). Each owner's portion of the centroid is proportional to their shore frontage.
- **The long-lake method** for elongated water bodies. Frontage is projected perpendicularly to a constructed central line.

The Indiana Supreme Court has applied both methods depending on the water body's geometry; the surveyor's choice should be justified in the report.

### 6. Putting it together

A competent boundary retracement follows a defensible sequence:

1. Research the chain of title back to a common grantor or original survey.
2. Identify the senior conveyance and any subsequent splits.
3. Locate all called-for monuments in the field; record what was found, where, and in what condition.
4. Measure occupation (fences, hedges, walls, pavement edges) and report conflicts with the record.
5. Apply the priority of calls to reconcile conflicts.
6. Set new monuments only at corners the surveyor is professionally satisfied are correct.

The work product - plat plus narrative - should make the surveyor's reasoning auditable. If a successor surveyor (or a judge) cannot tell why you set a corner where you did, the work is incomplete.

## Self-assessment

<details>
<summary>Question 1</summary>

The senior deed calls for 100 ft of frontage. A modern survey shows the parent tract is 99.2 ft wide. The junior deed calls for "the remainder." How wide is the junior tract?

**Answer:** Zero point negative eight feet, i.e. nothing. The senior takes its full 100 ft, the junior takes the remainder, which here is a deficiency of 0.8 ft. The junior owner has a claim against the common grantor, not against the senior.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2</summary>

A fence has stood 4 ft inside the record line of the Jones tract for 22 years. The Smiths, the adjoining owners, mowed up to the fence and maintained it. The Smiths did not pay taxes on the disputed strip. Could the Smiths succeed in an adverse-possession claim under Indiana law?

**Answer:** Probably not, because IC 32-21-7-1 requires the claimant to have paid the taxes on the disputed strip. The Smiths may, however, have a viable acquiescence claim given the 22-year recognition.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3</summary>

Two surveyors disagree on the location of a section line. Surveyor A relied on a found stone monument called for in 1880 field notes. Surveyor B relied on a more recent GPS-derived coordinate. Which evidence should control?

**Answer:** The stone monument, assuming it is the original or undisturbed, controls under the priority of calls (call for monument outranks call for bearing/distance/coordinate). Surveyor B's higher precision does not override the legal hierarchy.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4</summary>

Identify three categories of evidence that Indiana 865 IAC 1-12 expects to appear on a boundary survey plat.

**Answer:** Any three of: found monuments, set monuments, fences and occupation lines, encroachments, basis of bearings, surveyor's certificate, related record references, and adjoining-deed information.
</details>
