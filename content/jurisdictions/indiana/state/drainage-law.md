> **TL;DR**
> 1. Indiana governs rural and urban drainage through a system of **regulated drains** (IC 36-9-27), administered by **county drainage boards** with technical support from the **county surveyor** (IC 36-2-12).
> 2. A regulated drain is a legally established watercourse or ditch whose maintenance is funded by assessments on benefiting landowners. Unauthorized obstruction, alteration, or connection to a regulated drain is prohibited.
> 3. Indiana applies a **modified reasonable-use doctrine** for surface water drainage — a landowner may not materially increase the quantity or alter the natural course of surface water to the detriment of a downstream or adjacent owner. The old common-enemy doctrine has been largely abandoned in Indiana case law.

## Statutory framework

### IC 36-9-27 — Regulated drains

IC 36-9-27 (and its predecessors) establishes the legal framework for drainage districts and regulated drains in Indiana:

- **Drainage board.** Each county has a drainage board (typically the county commissioners sitting as the drainage board) with authority to establish, reconstruct, vacate, and maintain regulated drains.
- **Regulated drain.** A ditch, tile, or open channel that has been legally established through a drainage-board proceeding. Once established, the drain is maintained from a fund supported by **drainage assessments** on parcels that benefit from the drain.
- **Petition process.** Landowners petition the drainage board to establish a new drain, reconstruct an existing one, or vacate a drain that no longer serves a purpose. The county surveyor prepares a **Surveyor's Report** (see below) evaluating the engineering and cost of the proposed work.
- **Assessments.** The drainage board determines the watershed that benefits from the drain and allocates maintenance costs among landowners based on benefit. Assessments run with the land and appear in the county's tax records.

### IC 36-2-12 — County surveyor

The county surveyor is the statutory technical officer for drainage matters:

- **IC 36-2-12-12** — The county surveyor has jurisdiction over the maintenance and reconstruction of regulated drains.
- **IC 36-2-12-14** — The **Surveyor's Report** is the formal engineering report that the county surveyor prepares for the drainage board. It addresses the proposed drain's alignment, grade, cross-section, hydraulic capacity, estimated cost, and the watershed served. The drainage board cannot act on a petition without the Surveyor's Report.
- **IC 36-2-12-9** — The county surveyor maintains records of section corners, property corners, and benchmarks. This survey-records function overlaps with the county surveyor's drainage duties because regulated-drain surveys create controlling monuments.
- **IC 36-2-12-11** — The county surveyor has the right to enter private land for survey purposes related to drainage and corner perpetuation.

## Types of drains

- **Legal drain (regulated drain).** Established by petition and drainage-board order. Maintained at public expense through assessments.
- **Mutual drain.** A drain that serves two or more landowners but has not been formally established as a regulated drain. Under IC 36-9-27.4, mutual drains have their own maintenance framework — each benefiting landowner shares the cost of maintenance proportionally, and disputes are resolved by the drainage board.
- **Private drain.** A drain entirely on one property serving only that property. Not subject to drainage-board jurisdiction unless it connects to a regulated or mutual drain.

## Connecting to a regulated drain

Connecting a new outlet (e.g., a subdivision's storm sewer outfall) to a regulated drain requires **drainage-board approval**. The county surveyor reviews the proposed connection for hydraulic impact. Expect to provide:

- Hydrology/hydraulic calculations showing that the new discharge does not exceed the drain's capacity.
- A plan showing the connection location, invert elevation, pipe size, and headwall/endwall details.
- Evidence that downstream capacity is adequate.

The drainage board may condition approval on downstream improvements, additional detention, or revised assessments.

## Surface water doctrine in Indiana

Indiana case law has moved from the **common-enemy doctrine** (each landowner may repel surface water without liability) toward a **modified reasonable-use doctrine**:

- A landowner may improve drainage on their property, but may not **materially increase** the quantity of water discharged onto a neighbor's land or **concentrate** diffuse surface water into a channel that did not previously exist.
- The test is reasonableness: whether the improvement is reasonable under the circumstances, considering the foreseeability of harm, the utility of the improvement, and the gravity of the injury. Key Indiana cases include *Argyelan v. Haviland* (Indiana Supreme Court, 1982) and subsequent decisions.
- Developers converting agricultural land to impervious surfaces bear a heightened duty because the increase in runoff volume and peak flow is predictable and quantifiable.

For design purposes, this means that post-development stormwater management (detention, retention, rate control) is not just a regulatory requirement — it is a common-law obligation in Indiana.

## Practical implications for site development

1. **Check for regulated drains.** Before designing a site, obtain the county surveyor's regulated-drain maps. If a regulated drain crosses or abuts the property, coordinate with the county surveyor early.
2. **Do not obstruct.** Filling, grading over, or building on a regulated drain without drainage-board approval is a violation.
3. **Drainage-board approval timelines.** Drainage boards typically meet monthly. Plan for 60 to 90 days for review and approval of a new connection.
4. **Easements.** Regulated drains carry prescriptive or statutory easements for maintenance access. Even if the easement is not recorded, the county surveyor has the right to access the drain for maintenance.
5. **Surveyor's Report.** If your project triggers a reconstruction or reclassification of an existing drain, the county surveyor will prepare a Surveyor's Report at the developer's expense.
