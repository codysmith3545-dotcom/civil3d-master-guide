> **TL;DR**
> 1. Silt fence is a **temporary perimeter sediment barrier**, designed for sheet flow only — never concentrated flow. The Indiana Stormwater Quality Manual sets maximum contributing slope length by gradient (e.g., **100 ft for slopes ≤ 5%**, decreasing to **25 ft for slopes 30-50%**).
> 2. Trench-and-toe-in the fence: dig a **6-in deep × 4-in wide trench** along the upslope side, lay the bottom of the fabric in the trench, backfill, and compact. Posts spaced **6-10 ft** on center.
> 3. Use **J-hooks** (the fence wraps upslope at low points along the contour) to keep concentrated flow from out-flanking the fence; **tie the ends of the fence into the hillslope** at a higher elevation than any pondable area behind the fence.

## What silt fence does

Silt fence is a woven geotextile fabric attached to wooden or steel posts that intercepts sheet flow on disturbed slopes. Water ponds against the fabric, sediment settles out, and clarified water seeps through the fabric. Performance is set by:

- Fabric specification (apparent opening size, flow rate, tensile strength).
- Quality of trench-and-toe-in.
- Post spacing and reinforcement.
- Geometry (length of contributing slope, alignment along contour).

Silt fence is a **sediment** control, not an **erosion** control. It catches sediment that has already eroded; it does not prevent erosion. Pair it with stabilization (seeding, blankets, mulch).

## Maximum contributing slope length

Indiana Stormwater Quality Manual / INDOT IDM Chapter 205 (consistent with NCSU BMP Manual):

| Slope | Max contributing slope length (ft) |
|---|---|
| < 2% | 200 |
| 2% to 5% | 100 |
| 5% to 10% | 60 |
| 10% to 20% | 40 |
| 20% to 30% | 25 |
| 30% to 50% | 15 |

Longer slope lengths require either **interrupting the slope** with a benched ditch / sediment trap, or **a more robust BMP** (super silt fence, compost socks, sediment basin).

## Trenching and post detail

The standard installation:

1. **Trench:** 6-in deep, 4-in wide, along the contour on the upslope side of the fence line. Trench is dug just before fence installation.
2. **Posts:** 1-1/4 in × 1-1/4 in hardwood or steel T-posts; minimum 4 ft long; driven 18 in into the ground (deeper in soft soils). Spacing 6-10 ft on center; 6 ft on slopes > 5%.
3. **Fabric height:** 36 in minimum; 24 in above ground level after toe-in.
4. **Toe-in:** Lay the bottom 6-8 in of fabric horizontally in the trench, on the upslope side. Backfill the trench and compact.
5. **Wire backing:** Required on slopes > 10% or where ponding exceeds 18 in. 14-gauge welded wire, 12-in × 12-in mesh, attached to the upslope face of the posts and over the fabric.
6. **Joints:** Where rolls meet, wrap the two ends together around a post and fasten; never butt-join.

## J-hooks and contour alignment

A J-hook is a section of fence that curves upslope at low points along the contour to prevent water from accumulating against the fence and finding a path around the end. Hook geometry:

- Length of the hook = **about 6-10 ft** wrapping up the slope.
- Hook ends at an elevation **≥ 6 in higher than the top of the standing water** that would pond behind the fence.
- Place hooks at every 100-ft segment on slopes > 5% and at every low spot.

Without J-hooks, ponded water concentrates along the fence and flows around the down-slope end of a fence run, scouring the bare slope it was supposed to protect.

## Tie-in at the ends

Both ends of every silt-fence run must turn up-slope to terminate at an elevation **above** the maximum ponding elevation behind the fence. If the fence simply ends along the contour, water rises until it overtops the end and discharges off-site unfiltered.

## Maintenance

Indiana CGP requires inspection of silt fence every 7 days and after 0.5-in rain events. Maintenance actions:

- **Remove sediment** when accumulation reaches **1/3 of the fence height**. Dispose of in a stabilized area.
- **Replace damaged fabric** (rips > 6 in, UV degradation, or fabric pulled out of the trench).
- **Restake leaning posts** before they fail.
- **Re-seed disturbed soil** caused by maintenance access.

## Common failures

- Fence stapled to the upslope face of the post — fabric tears free on the first pond event. Fasten to the downslope face so ponded water pushes the fabric **against** the post.
- No trench — fabric only stapled to posts on the surface. Water undermines and flows under.
- Long unbroken runs on steep slopes — water concentrates, fence fails. Add J-hooks.
- Fence on a slope draining > 100 ft contributing length on 4% slope — exceeds the table. Use sediment trap or interrupt the slope.
- Wire backing omitted on a 12% slope — fabric balloons and rips off the posts.

## Worked example

12-ac mass-graded subdivision, 80 ft of contributing slope at 6% along the south property line, abutting an existing drainage ditch.

- Slope category 5-10% → max contributing length 60 ft. **80 ft exceeds the limit.**
- Options:
    1. Install an upslope interceptor swale 25-30 ft from the property line, breaking the slope. Drain the swale to a sediment trap. Run silt fence at the property line as a secondary barrier on the now-50-ft slope.
    2. Use super silt fence (silt fence with a chain-link backing); slope length increases to ~100 ft, satisfying the geometry.

Plan adds an interceptor swale 30 ft up-slope draining 50 ft east to a sediment trap at the existing low point. Silt fence at the property line installed with J-hooks every 100 ft.
