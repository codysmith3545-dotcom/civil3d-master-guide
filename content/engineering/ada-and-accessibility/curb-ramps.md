> **TL;DR**
> 1. Curb ramp types: perpendicular (straight into street), parallel (along curb line), combination, and diagonal (at intersection apex — discouraged because it directs users into the intersection rather than the crosswalk).
> 2. Key dimensions: max **8.33% (1:12)** running slope, max **2.0%** cross slope, flared sides max **10.0% (1:10)**, detectable warning surface **24 inches** deep across the full width of the ramp.
> 3. Every crosswalk at a signalized intersection must have a curb ramp. PROWAG requires two curb ramps per corner (one per crosswalk) at intersections — a single diagonal ramp serving both crosswalks does not comply.

## Curb ramp types

### Perpendicular curb ramp

The ramp runs perpendicular to the curb face, straight from the sidewalk into the street. This is the most common and preferred type.

- Running slope: max 8.33% (1:12).
- Cross slope of the ramp surface: max 2.0%.
- Flared sides: max 10.0% (1:10) where pedestrians must walk across the flare. If the flare is protected by a planter, wall, or other barrier that prevents pedestrian crossing, flare slope is unrestricted.
- Top landing: 48 in. min length (in the direction of ramp run), full width of the ramp, max 2.0% slope in any direction.
- Bottom landing: the gutter or street surface at the foot of the ramp should be no steeper than 5.0% for a distance of 24 in. from the bottom of the ramp (the counter slope).
- Detectable warning: 24 in. deep in the direction of travel, extending the full width of the ramp where it meets the street.

### Parallel curb ramp

The ramp runs parallel to the curb (along the sidewalk direction), lowering the pedestrian to street level at the curb line. Used where the sidewalk is narrow and there is not enough depth for a perpendicular ramp.

- Running slope: max 8.33% (1:12).
- The ramp lowers into a level landing (48 in. x 48 in. min) at the curb face.
- Detectable warning: 24 in. deep at the curb face.

Parallel ramps are less intuitive for blind pedestrians because the direction of travel changes from along the sidewalk to into the street at the bottom landing.

### Combination curb ramp

Combines perpendicular and parallel elements. The ramp descends parallel to the curb for part of the height, turns, and descends perpendicular to the curb for the remainder. Used in constrained geometries.

### Diagonal curb ramp (discouraged)

A single ramp at the apex (corner) of an intersection, serving both crosswalks. Diagonal ramps are discouraged and do not meet current PROWAG requirements because:

- They direct the pedestrian into the center of the intersection rather than into a crosswalk.
- They require the pedestrian to navigate traffic from two directions simultaneously.
- They do not provide a clear directional cue for blind pedestrians.

PROWAG requires separate curb ramps for each crosswalk at an intersection. Two perpendicular ramps per corner is the standard.

## Detectable warning surfaces

Detectable warning surfaces (truncated domes) provide a tactile cue at the transition from sidewalk to street. Requirements:

- **Size:** truncated domes with a base diameter of 0.9 in. to 1.4 in., top diameter 50% to 65% of base, height 0.2 in.
- **Spacing:** center-to-center 1.6 in. to 2.4 in. (in-line or offset).
- **Depth:** 24 in. in the direction of pedestrian travel.
- **Width:** the full width of the curb ramp or flush transition where it meets the street.
- **Color contrast:** must contrast visually with the surrounding surface. Yellow on gray concrete is the most common combination.
- **Placement:** the back edge of the detectable warning is at the curb line (back of curb or face of curb, depending on the ramp geometry).

## Gutter and counter slope

The gutter pan or street surface at the foot of the ramp creates a counter slope. If the counter slope is too steep, a wheelchair user transitioning from ramp to street may tip forward or get stuck.

- PROWAG limits the algebraic difference in grade between the ramp and the gutter to 11% (e.g., 8.33% ramp + 2.67% gutter = 11% algebraic difference).
- The gutter or street grade at the foot of the ramp should not exceed 5.0% for at least 24 in. from the foot of the ramp.

## Design considerations

- **Drainage.** Water must not pond at the base of the ramp. Grade the gutter to carry water past the ramp opening. A lip or dam at the base of the ramp blocks drainage and creates a hazard.
- **Lip height.** The maximum vertical discontinuity at the ramp-to-street transition is 1/4 in. (flush is preferred). Asphalt overlay that buries the curb reveal eliminates the lip problem.
- **Snow and ice.** Curb ramp depressions collect snow and ice. In Indiana, municipal snow removal practices affect usability. Design ramp grading to shed meltwater away from the ramp surface.
- **Sight distance.** The curb ramp should not be hidden behind parked cars, signs, or utility infrastructure. Align with the crosswalk markings.
