> **TL;DR**
> 1. Bus stop boarding/alighting areas must be at least **96 inches deep x 60 inches wide** (8 ft x 5 ft), with a maximum slope of **2.0% in any direction**, connected to the accessible pedestrian route.
> 2. Detectable warning surfaces are required at the **platform edge** where the boarding area is at the same elevation as the street or at rail transit platforms to delineate the drop-off edge.
> 3. Bus shelters must provide a minimum **30 in. x 48 in.** clear floor space inside, connected to the boarding area, with the same slope and surface requirements as the boarding area.

## Boarding and alighting area

The boarding and alighting area is the paved surface where passengers board and exit the bus. Requirements:

- **Dimensions:** 96 in. (8 ft) measured perpendicular to the curb or roadway, and 60 in. (5 ft) measured parallel to the curb. The 96-inch depth accommodates a wheelchair lift deployment.
- **Slope:** max 2.0% in any direction. This is critical — the boarding area is where the wheelchair lift or ramp deploys, and excessive slope causes the lift to malfunction or the wheelchair to roll.
- **Surface:** firm, stable, slip-resistant. Concrete is standard. The surface must support the concentrated load of a wheelchair lift pad.
- **Connection:** the boarding area must connect to the accessible pedestrian route (sidewalk, shared-use path) without a gap or level change greater than 1/2 inch.

## Curb height at bus stops

Where the boarding area has a curb:

- A raised curb (6 in. to 8 in.) allows near-level boarding for low-floor buses equipped with ramps. This is the preferred configuration for fixed-route transit.
- If the boarding area is at street level (no curb), the bus deploys a lift or bridge plate. The detectable warning surface is required at the edge where the boarding area meets the street.

## Detectable warnings

Detectable warning surfaces (truncated domes) at transit stops:

- **Bus stops at street level:** required at the curb edge of the boarding area if the boarding area is at the same elevation as the street (no curb separation).
- **Rail platforms:** required along the full length of the platform edge where the drop to the track exceeds a specified height. The detectable warning extends 24 in. back from the platform edge.
- **Bus rapid transit (BRT) platforms:** treated as rail platforms for detectable warning purposes.

At conventional bus stops with a standard curb, detectable warnings at the curb edge are not universally required under ADA, but PROWAG and local practices may require them. Check the local transit agency's design standards.

## Bus shelters

Where a bus shelter is provided, it must be accessible:

- **Clear floor space:** 30 in. x 48 in. minimum inside the shelter, connected to the boarding area at the same grade.
- **Entry opening:** wide enough for a wheelchair (32 in. minimum clear).
- **Slope:** max 2.0% in any direction within the shelter pad.
- **Knee/toe clearance:** if a bench is provided, the clear floor space may be adjacent to the bench, not under it.

The shelter must not block the accessible route on the sidewalk. A shelter placed in the middle of a narrow sidewalk that reduces the clear width below 48 in. is non-compliant.

## Route connection

The transit stop must be connected to the pedestrian network by an accessible route:

- The sidewalk or path connecting to the boarding area meets all accessible route requirements (width, slope, surface).
- If the transit stop is at a signalized intersection, accessible pedestrian signals and curb ramps are required.
- If the transit stop is at a midblock location, the accessible route connects to the nearest crosswalk or intersection.

## IndyGo and Indiana transit considerations

IndyGo (Indianapolis Public Transportation Corporation) and other Indiana transit agencies have design standards for bus stops that incorporate ADA requirements. IndyGo's Red Line BRT stations include raised platforms, detectable warnings, and level boarding. For conventional bus stops, IndyGo coordinates with the municipal engineering department for boarding pad construction.

Surveyors and engineers working on projects adjacent to transit routes should coordinate with the transit agency early in design to ensure boarding area requirements are met, especially where road widening, sidewalk reconstruction, or utility relocation may affect existing bus stops.
