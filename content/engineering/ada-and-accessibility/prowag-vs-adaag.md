> **TL;DR**
> 1. **ADAAG** (ADA Standards for Accessible Design) governs buildings and their sites — parking lots, building entrances, interior routes, and site walks on private or public property. **PROWAG** governs the public right-of-way — sidewalks, crosswalks, curb ramps, pedestrian signals, shared-use paths, and transit stops.
> 2. PROWAG was issued as a final rule by the U.S. Access Board in August 2023. Before PROWAG, public ROW accessibility was enforced under ADAAG by analogy, which created inconsistencies. PROWAG provides ROW-specific requirements.
> 3. Key practical differences: PROWAG allows sidewalk running slope to match road grade, requires two curb ramps per intersection corner, and includes requirements for pedestrian signals and shared-use paths that ADAAG does not address.

## Scope of each standard

### ADAAG (ADA Standards for Accessible Design, 2010)

Adopted by the Department of Justice (Title III — public accommodations and commercial facilities) and the Department of Transportation (Title II — state and local government programs). Governs:

- Buildings (new construction and alterations).
- Sites associated with buildings: parking lots, exterior walks, entrances, plazas, recreational facilities.
- State and local government facilities (courthouses, libraries, parks).

ADAAG does not specifically address public ROW elements like sidewalks, crosswalks, or curb ramps, though it has been applied to them by analogy.

### PROWAG (Public Right-of-Way Accessibility Guidelines)

Developed by the U.S. Access Board over many years and published as a final rule in August 2023. Governs:

- Pedestrian access routes within the public ROW.
- Sidewalks and shared-use paths.
- Crosswalks and street crossings.
- Curb ramps and blended transitions.
- Pedestrian signals and pushbuttons.
- On-street parking.
- Transit stops in the public ROW.
- Street furniture and other elements within the pedestrian access route.

## Key differences

### Running slope

| Element | ADAAG | PROWAG |
|---|---|---|
| Accessible route / sidewalk running slope | Max 5.0%. Exceeding triggers ramp requirements. | Max 5.0%, but sidewalk may match the grade of the adjacent roadway where constrained by the road profile. |

This is the most significant practical difference. Under ADAAG, a sidewalk steeper than 5.0% requires handrails and landings (ramp treatment), which is often infeasible on a public sidewalk following a hilly road. PROWAG recognizes the road grade as a constraint and allows the sidewalk to follow it.

### Cross slope

Both standards: max 2.0%. PROWAG explicitly addresses cross slope at driveway crossings and requires the pedestrian access route to maintain max 2.0% through the driveway apron.

### Curb ramps at intersections

| Requirement | ADAAG | PROWAG |
|---|---|---|
| Curb ramps per corner | One curb ramp per crosswalk (2 per corner implied but not explicit) | Two curb ramps per corner required — one aligned with each crosswalk |
| Diagonal ramps | Permitted (discouraged) | Do not comply. Each ramp must be within the crosswalk markings of the crosswalk it serves. |

PROWAG's requirement for two ramps per corner is a significant change for intersection reconstruction projects. Diagonal ramps, still common in older infrastructure, must be replaced with directional ramps when the intersection is altered.

### Pedestrian signals

ADAAG does not address pedestrian signals. PROWAG includes requirements for:

- Accessible pedestrian signals (APS) and pushbuttons.
- Pushbutton location (within reach range, adjacent to the curb ramp for the crossing it serves).
- Audible and vibrotactile walk indications.
- Pushbutton locator tone.

### Shared-use paths

ADAAG does not specifically address shared-use paths (multi-use trails for pedestrians and bicyclists). PROWAG includes requirements for:

- Minimum width: 60 in. (PROWAG) vs. 44 in. (ADAAG accessible route).
- Cross slope: max 2.0%.
- Grade: may follow terrain; steep grades do not trigger ramp requirements on shared-use paths.
- Detectable warnings at street crossings.

### On-street parking

PROWAG includes requirements for accessible on-street (parallel) parking:

- Accessible parallel parking spaces: wide enough for a wheelchair to deploy a lift (typically 18 ft to 22 ft long).
- Access aisle at the street surface, connecting to the curb ramp and accessible route.
- Marked and signed.

ADAAG addresses only off-street parking (parking lots and structures).

### Enforcement

- ADAAG is enforced through Department of Justice (DOJ) Title III enforcement and private lawsuits.
- PROWAG is enforced through Department of Transportation (DOT) Section 504 enforcement for recipients of federal funding, and through DOJ Title II enforcement for state and local governments. FHWA requires compliance for federally funded transportation projects.

## When both apply

A project can trigger both standards. Example: a commercial development with a new building (ADAAG governs the site) that includes frontage improvements — new sidewalk, curb ramps, and pedestrian signal modifications in the public ROW (PROWAG governs the ROW improvements).

The engineer must identify the boundary between private site and public ROW and apply the correct standard to each zone. Where the standards overlap (both have a 2.0% cross slope max), compliance with either satisfies both. Where they differ (running slope treatment), the applicable standard for that zone controls.
