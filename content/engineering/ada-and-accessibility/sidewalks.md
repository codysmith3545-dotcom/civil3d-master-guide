> **TL;DR**
> 1. Minimum sidewalk width is **48 inches** (4 ft); **60 inches** (5 ft) is preferred and required by many Indiana municipalities for new construction. Passing spaces (60 in. x 60 in.) are required every 200 ft if the width is less than 60 in.
> 2. Maximum cross slope is **2.0%** (1:50). Running slope should not exceed **5.0%** (1:20) unless the sidewalk follows the grade of an adjacent roadway.
> 3. Driveway crossings are the most common point of failure — the driveway apron must not impose a cross slope greater than 2.0% on the pedestrian access route.

## Width

| Standard | Minimum | Recommended |
|---|---|---|
| ADA (on-site) | 44 in. (36 in. at constrictions ≤ 24 in. long) | 60 in. |
| PROWAG (public ROW) | 48 in. clear pedestrian access route | 60 in. |
| INDOT (state ROW) | 48 in. (60 in. in urban areas) | 60 in. |
| Typical Indiana municipal standard | 48 in. to 60 in. | 60 in. |

The width dimension is the clear, unobstructed width. Utility poles, sign posts, fire hydrants, and mailboxes that encroach into the sidewalk reduce the effective width. A 60-inch sidewalk with a utility pole in the middle may have only 30 inches of clear width at the obstruction.

Where the sidewalk is less than 60 inches wide, a passing space of at least 60 in. x 60 in. must be provided at intervals of no more than 200 ft. This allows two wheelchair users to pass each other.

## Slopes

### Cross slope

Maximum 2.0% (1:50). Cross slope provides drainage but must not exceed the threshold that causes a wheelchair to veer. The 2.0% limit applies:

- Along the full length of the sidewalk.
- Through driveway crossings (the driveway apron must not slope across the pedestrian route at more than 2.0%).
- At intersections and crosswalks.

### Running slope

Maximum 5.0% (1:20) for accessible routes. In the public ROW (PROWAG), the sidewalk running slope may match the grade of the adjacent roadway even if it exceeds 5.0%, provided the sidewalk is constructed at or near the road grade. This exception recognizes that the street grade is a fixed constraint.

Where the running slope exceeds 5.0% on a private site (not following a road grade), the segment must be designed as a ramp with handrails, landings, and edge protection.

## Joints and surface texture

- **Joints.** Expansion joints, control joints, and construction joints must be no wider than 1/2 inch. Joints must be flush — a raised or depressed joint edge greater than 1/4 inch is a trip hazard and a barrier.
- **Surface.** Broom-finished concrete is the standard. The broom texture provides slip resistance. Smooth-troweled, stamped, or exposed-aggregate surfaces may not meet slip-resistance requirements unless tested.
- **Pavers.** Unit pavers (brick, concrete) are acceptable if joints are tight (1/4 inch max), the surface is level, and the pavers do not shift under wheelchair loads. Sand-set pavers on a flexible base are prone to settling and shifting; mortar-set or mechanically interlocked pavers on a rigid base are preferred.

## Obstructions and clear width

Objects in or adjacent to the sidewalk must not reduce the clear width below the minimum:

- **Permanent objects** (utility poles, fire hydrants, signal poles): maintain at least 48 in. (PROWAG) clear past the object.
- **Protruding objects:** objects mounted on walls or posts between 27 in. and 80 in. above the surface must not protrude more than 4 in. into the pedestrian route (they are undetectable by a cane below 27 in.).
- **Temporary obstructions** (sandwich boards, outdoor dining, construction barriers): must maintain the clear width or provide an accessible detour.

## Driveway crossings

Driveways that cross the sidewalk are the single most common accessibility failure. The driveway apron typically slopes from the road to the property at a grade much steeper than 2.0%. When the sidewalk crosses this apron, the pedestrian experiences the driveway slope as cross slope.

Solutions:

1. **Bring the sidewalk behind the apron.** Route the sidewalk behind the driveway throat where the grade is flatter.
2. **Level the apron through the pedestrian zone.** Reconstruct the driveway apron so that the pedestrian path through the apron maintains max 2.0% cross slope. The driveway grade transitions happen outside the pedestrian zone.
3. **Raise the sidewalk.** Bring the driveway up to the sidewalk level (a "raised driveway crossing"). The vehicle transitions up and down; the pedestrian remains level.

## Indiana municipal requirements

Indiana municipalities set their own sidewalk standards through local ordinances and design manuals. Common requirements:

- Width: 48 in. to 60 in. (many cities now require 60 in. for new construction).
- Material: concrete (asphalt sidewalks are prohibited in most Indiana municipalities).
- Thickness: 4 in. standard, 6 in. at driveway crossings.
- Setback from curb: varies (0 ft back-of-curb to 6 ft with a tree lawn/buffer strip).

Check the local standards for the specific municipality — Carmel, Fishers, Indianapolis, and Noblesville each have their own requirements.
