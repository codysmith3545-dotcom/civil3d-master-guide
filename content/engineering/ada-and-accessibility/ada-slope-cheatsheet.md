> **TL;DR**
> 1. Maximum running slope for an accessible route is **5.0% (1:20)**. Anything steeper triggers ramp requirements: max **8.33% (1:12)** running slope with handrails, edge protection, and landings.
> 2. Maximum cross slope is **2.0% (1:50)** on accessible routes, sidewalks, and ramp runs. Curb ramp cross slope also max 2.0%.
> 3. PROWAG (public ROW) and ADAAG (buildings/sites) share most slope values but differ in application context — know which standard governs your project element.

## Quick-reference table

| Element | Running slope max | Cross slope max | Other key dimensions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Accessible route (walking surface) | 5.0% (1:20) | 2.0% (1:50) | Min 44" clear width (36" at constriction up to 24" length) |
| Ramp run | 8.33% (1:12) | 2.0% (1:50) | Max 30" rise per run; handrails required both sides |
| Ramp landing (top and bottom) | 2.0% max in any direction | 2.0% | 60" x 60" min; 60" x 60" at direction change |
| Curb ramp (perpendicular) | 8.33% (1:12) | 2.0% (1:50) | Flared sides max 10.0% (1:10); 48" landing at top |
| Curb ramp (parallel) | 8.33% (1:12) | 2.0% (1:50) | 48" min clear at bottom of ramp |
| Sidewalk (PROWAG) | 5.0% (1:20) | 2.0% (1:50) | Min 48" width (60" preferred); running slope may match road grade |
| Parking space and access aisle | N/A | 2.0% max all directions | Level surface in all directions |
| Detectable warning surface | — | — | 24" deep in direction of travel |

## Running slope vs. cross slope

- **Running slope** is measured parallel to the direction of travel.
- **Cross slope** is measured perpendicular to the direction of travel.

On a sidewalk that follows a road, the running slope is along the sidewalk's length and the cross slope is side-to-side. Cross slope drains water but must not exceed 2.0% or it becomes a barrier for wheelchair users.

## Ramp requirements

A ramp is required when the running slope of an accessible route exceeds 5.0%. Ramp requirements:

- Max running slope: 8.33% (1:12).
- Max rise per run: 30" (a run at 8.33% is 30 ft long for 30" rise).
- Landings at top and bottom: 60" long x width of ramp, max 2.0% slope in any direction.
- Landings at changes in direction: 60" x 60" min.
- Handrails: required on both sides when rise exceeds 6". Gripping surface 34" to 38" above ramp surface. Extensions 12" beyond top and bottom of run.
- Edge protection: curb, wall, or rail to prevent wheelchair wheels from dropping off the edge.

## PROWAG vs. ADAAG distinctions

Most slope values are the same under both standards, but the application differs:

- **ADAAG** (ADA Standards for Accessible Design) governs **buildings and their sites**: parking lots, building entries, interior routes, site walks within a property.
- **PROWAG** governs **public rights-of-way**: sidewalks, crosswalks, curb ramps, pedestrian signals, shared-use paths within the public ROW.

Key practical differences:

| Parameter | ADAAG | PROWAG |
|---|---|---|
| Sidewalk running slope | Max 5.0% | May follow road grade if constrained; max 5.0% where grade permits |
| Cross slope | 2.0% | 2.0% (5.0% at certain driveways/crossings allowed in PROWAG draft, but 2.0% is the standard target) |
| Curb ramp turning space | 60" x 60" | 48" x 48" min at bottom (varies by ramp type) |
| Detectable warnings at transit | Required | Required, with specific placement criteria for bus stops |

When a project spans both private site and public ROW (e.g., a commercial development with a sidewalk connection to a public street), both standards may apply to different portions of the work.

## Measurement and tolerances

In the field, slopes are measured with a digital inclinometer, smart level, or total station. Construction tolerances are not explicit in the ADA standards, but accepted practice allows:

- Cross slope: constructed within 0.5% of the design target (e.g., design at 1.5%, constructed at 1.0% to 2.0%).
- Running slope: constructed within the maximum. There is no tolerance above the maximum — 8.33% means 8.33%.

Survey for compliance should report slopes to the nearest 0.1%. A slope reading of 2.1% cross slope technically exceeds the 2.0% maximum.
