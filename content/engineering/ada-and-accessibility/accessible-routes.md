> **TL;DR**
> 1. An accessible route is a continuous, unobstructed path connecting all accessible elements of a site — from parking and public transit to building entrances. Minimum clear width is **44 inches** (36 inches at constrictions no longer than 24 inches).
> 2. Running slope max **5.0%** (ramp requirements apply above this). Cross slope max **2.0%**. Surface must be **firm, stable, and slip-resistant**.
> 3. The accessible route must connect every required accessible element: parking, building entrance, public sidewalk, transit stop, and recreational facility. If any segment fails, the entire route is non-compliant.

## Definition

An accessible route is a continuous path that allows a person using a wheelchair, walker, crutches, or other mobility device to travel between accessible elements of a building or site without encountering barriers. It includes corridors, doorways, walks, ramps, elevators, and platform lifts.

The accessible route is not just a sidewalk. It is the entire linked chain of accessible path segments from origin to destination.

## Width requirements

| Condition | Minimum clear width |
|---|---|
| General accessible route | 44 in. (3'-8") |
| Constriction up to 24" in length | 36 in. (3'-0") |
| Passing space (every 200 ft max) | 60 in. x 60 in. |
| Doorway / gate opening | 32 in. clear (single leaf) |
| U-turn around an obstruction | 42 in. approach + 48 in. at turn |

The 44-inch minimum allows a wheelchair and a walking person to pass. Where the route narrows to 36 inches (e.g., between bollards or at a gate), the constriction cannot exceed 24 inches in length. Where the route is less than 60 inches wide, a passing space must be provided at intervals not exceeding 200 feet.

## Slope requirements

- **Running slope:** max 5.0% (1:20). If the running slope exceeds 5.0%, the segment is classified as a ramp and must comply with ramp requirements (max 8.33%, handrails, landings, edge protection).
- **Cross slope:** max 2.0% (1:50). Sufficient for drainage but not a barrier to wheeled mobility devices.

In PROWAG applications (public sidewalks), the running slope may match the grade of the adjacent roadway even if it exceeds 5.0%, provided the sidewalk is constructed as close to the road grade as technically feasible. This recognizes that street grades cannot always be kept below 5.0%.

## Surface requirements

The route surface must be:

- **Firm** — does not deform significantly under the weight of a wheelchair. Concrete, asphalt, brick pavers (tight joints), and compacted stone (< 3/8" aggregate, well-compacted) qualify. Loose gravel, sand, and mulch do not.
- **Stable** — does not shift under load. Interlocking pavers without adequate edge restraint may not qualify.
- **Slip-resistant** — provides traction when wet. Smooth-troweled concrete, polished stone, and painted surfaces may not qualify. Broom-finished concrete is the standard.

Gratings, if present in the route surface, must have openings no wider than 1/2 inch (in the direction perpendicular to travel) to prevent cane tips and small wheelchair wheels from catching.

Changes in level:

| Level change | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Up to 1/4" | May be vertical (square) |
| 1/4" to 1/2" | Must be beveled (max 1:2 slope) |
| Over 1/2" | Must be ramped (max 1:12) or eliminated |

## Connection points

The accessible route must connect:

- **Accessible parking spaces** — the access aisle of each accessible parking space connects to the accessible route without requiring travel behind parked vehicles.
- **Building entrances** — at least 60% of public entrances must be accessible (or all entrances on an accessible route). At a minimum, the primary entrance must be on an accessible route.
- **Public sidewalk / ROW** — the site's accessible route must connect to the public sidewalk or public way at the property boundary.
- **Public transit stops** — if a transit stop is adjacent to the site, the accessible route must connect to the boarding area.
- **Site amenities** — recreational facilities, mail kiosks, trash enclosures, and other site elements that are used by the public must be on an accessible route.

## Common design errors

- **Missing connection from parking access aisle to sidewalk.** The access aisle ends at a curb with no ramp or flush transition to the accessible route.
- **Cross slope exceeds 2.0% at driveway crossings.** The driveway apron slopes across the sidewalk at the driveway grade rather than maintaining the sidewalk cross slope. PROWAG requires the pedestrian access route to maintain max 2.0% cross slope through driveway crossings.
- **Obstructions in the clear width.** Signs, utility poles, fire hydrants, newspaper boxes, and outdoor dining furniture encroach on the accessible route.
- **Running slope exceeds 5.0% without ramp treatment.** A short steep section of sidewalk between a parking lot and an entrance is not treated as a ramp (no handrails, no landings).
