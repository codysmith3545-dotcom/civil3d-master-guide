> **TL;DR**
> 1. Post-construction BMPs treat runoff from developed sites to remove **total suspended solids (TSS)**, nutrients, and other pollutants before discharge. Indiana MS4 communities require post-construction BMPs on sites disturbing 1.0 acre or more.
> 2. Common BMP types: **bioretention** (rain gardens), **wet detention ponds**, **constructed wetlands**, **sand filters**, **infiltration basins/trenches**, **permeable pavement**, and **proprietary manufactured devices**.
> 3. The design standard is typically **80% TSS removal** (the "80% rule") or capture and treatment of the **water-quality volume (WQv)** — the first 1.0 in to 1.25 in of runoff from impervious area.
> 4. Every BMP requires a **maintenance plan** recorded with the plat or site plan, identifying the responsible party, maintenance schedule, and funding mechanism.

## Why post-construction BMPs are required

Federal NPDES Phase II regulations (40 CFR 122.34) require MS4 communities to implement post-construction stormwater management programs. In Indiana, this is codified in **Rule 13** (327 IAC 15-13). The goal is to control pollutants in runoff from new development and redevelopment after construction is complete — distinct from the temporary erosion-control BMPs required during construction under [Rule 5](idem-rule-5.md).

Pollutants of concern include:

- **TSS** — sediment from eroding surfaces and tracked soils.
- **Nutrients** — nitrogen and phosphorus from fertilizers and organic matter.
- **Hydrocarbons** — oil, grease, and fuel from parking lots and driveways.
- **Metals** — copper, zinc, and lead from roofing, vehicles, and industrial processes.
- **Bacteria** — from pet waste, wildlife, and failing septic systems.
- **Thermal pollution** — heated runoff from impervious surfaces degrades cold-water streams.

## Water-quality volume (WQv)

Most Indiana MS4 communities define the treatment target as the **water-quality volume (WQv)** — the volume of runoff from the water-quality storm that must be captured and treated before release. The water-quality storm is typically:

- **1.0 in** of rainfall over 24 hours (many Indiana communities, including Indianapolis DPW), or
- **1.25 in** of rainfall (Carmel, some Hamilton County communities).

WQv is computed as:

`WQv = P x Rv x A`

where P is the water-quality rainfall depth (inches), Rv is the runoff coefficient (commonly `Rv = 0.05 + 0.009 x I`, with I as percent imperviousness), and A is the contributing drainage area (acres). The result is in acre-inches; convert to cubic feet (1 ac-in = 3,630 cf) for sizing.

The BMP must capture the WQv and release it over a **drawdown period** — typically 24 to 72 hours depending on the BMP type and the local standard.

## BMP types

### Bioretention (rain gardens)

Shallow vegetated depressions with an engineered soil media (typically 60% sand, 20% topsoil, 20% compost by volume) underlain by gravel and an underdrain. Runoff ponds on the surface (6 to 12 in maximum ponding depth) and filters through the media.

- **TSS removal**: 85% to 95%.
- **Sizing**: media surface area typically 5% to 8% of the contributing impervious area.
- **Underdrain**: required where native soils have infiltration rates below about 0.5 in/hr (hydrologic soil groups C and D, common in central Indiana).
- **Advantages**: compact, can be integrated into parking lot islands and streetscapes.
- **Limitations**: not suitable for hotspot runoff (fueling areas, loading docks) without pretreatment.

### Wet detention ponds

Permanent-pool ponds with an extended detention zone above the pool. The permanent pool provides settling time; the extended-detention zone captures the WQv and releases it over 24 to 48 hours.

- **TSS removal**: 70% to 80% with a properly sized permanent pool.
- **Permanent pool volume**: typically equal to the WQv or 2 x WQv (varies by local standard).
- **Littoral shelf**: a 10-ft minimum width shallow bench (6 to 18 in depth) planted with emergent vegetation around 50% or more of the perimeter. Provides nutrient uptake and habitat.
- **Maintenance**: annual sediment survey, mowing, and outlet inspection. Sediment removal typically every 5 to 10 years.
- **Advantages**: effective for large drainage areas; provides detention and water quality in one facility.
- **Limitations**: requires significant space; standing water creates safety and mosquito concerns.

### Constructed wetlands

Similar to wet ponds but with a shallower pool (6 to 18 in) and emergent and submergent vegetation across most of the facility. Higher pollutant removal (especially nutrients) but requires more land area.

- **TSS removal**: 80% to 90%.
- **Area**: 3% to 5% of the contributing drainage area (significantly larger than bioretention).
- **Advantages**: high pollutant removal, habitat value, aesthetic.
- **Limitations**: large footprint; long establishment period; needs consistent base flow to sustain vegetation.

### Sand filters

Engineered sand bed (18 to 24 in depth) that filters runoff. Available as surface or underground (Austin-style) configurations.

- **TSS removal**: 80% to 90%.
- **Advantages**: small footprint (underground systems fit under parking lots); high TSS removal.
- **Limitations**: requires regular maintenance (raking, replacement of top layer); clogs if not pretreated for coarse sediment.

### Infiltration basins and trenches

Infiltrate runoff into the soil, eliminating surface discharge entirely for the water-quality storm. Only feasible where soils have adequate infiltration rates (≥ 0.5 in/hr; hydrologic soil group A or B) and the seasonal high water table is at least 2 ft below the basin bottom.

- **TSS removal**: effectively 100% (runoff does not discharge to surface water).
- **Geotechnical investigation required**: percolation test or double-ring infiltrometer test at the proposed basin elevation.
- **Setbacks**: 100 ft from wells, 50 ft from building foundations, 25 ft from property lines (varies by jurisdiction).
- **Limitations**: not feasible in clay soils (central Indiana till plain); risk of groundwater contamination if runoff is from a hotspot.

### Permeable pavement

Porous asphalt, pervious concrete, or permeable interlocking concrete pavers (PICP) that allow runoff to infiltrate through the pavement surface into a gravel reservoir below. Functions as both a driving/parking surface and a BMP.

- **TSS removal**: 80% to 95%.
- **Gravel reservoir**: typically 12 to 24 in of open-graded aggregate (40% void space).
- **Underdrain**: required in clay soils.
- **Limitations**: requires vacuum sweeping 2 to 4 times per year; not suitable for high-sediment areas or heavy-truck traffic.

### Proprietary manufactured devices

Pre-engineered units (hydrodynamic separators, filter cartridge systems, media filters) installed underground in the storm sewer system. Examples include Stormceptor, CDS, Filterra, and BioClean.

- **TSS removal**: varies by device; typically 50% to 80%. Must be verified by independent testing (NJDEP/TAPE/ETV protocols).
- **Advantages**: small footprint; can be retrofitted into existing storm sewer systems.
- **Limitations**: lower removal efficiency than bioretention or sand filters; maintenance-intensive (cartridge replacement, sediment cleanout); many Indiana MS4 communities do not accept these as standalone BMPs — they may be allowed for pretreatment only.

## Maintenance obligations

Every post-construction BMP in Indiana must have a recorded maintenance agreement or plan. The plan identifies:

- **Responsible party** — the property owner, HOA, or commercial entity.
- **Maintenance schedule** — inspection frequency (typically semi-annual), mowing, sediment removal, media replacement, outlet inspection.
- **Funding mechanism** — how maintenance is financed (HOA dues, commercial operating budget, escrow account).
- **Access** — the MS4 entity retains the right to inspect and, if necessary, perform maintenance at the owner's expense.

The maintenance agreement is typically recorded with the plat or site plan as a deed restriction or a separate instrument. Many Indiana MS4 communities (Indianapolis, Carmel, Fishers) have enforcement programs that inspect BMPs and issue notices of violation for deferred maintenance.

## Common review issues

- WQv calculated using total site area instead of impervious area — overestimates the volume; recalculate with the correct impervious fraction.
- Bioretention ponding depth exceeds 12 in — most standards cap at 12 in for safety and plant survival.
- No underdrain in a bioretention cell on soil group D — the cell will not drain; add an underdrain.
- Infiltration basin proposed on soil group C or D without a percolation test — not feasible without data.
- No recorded maintenance agreement submitted with the final plat — required by Rule 13.
- Proprietary device submitted as the sole BMP without independent performance data — many MS4s will not accept it.
